# Abstract 

The deployment of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) in high-stakes decision-making pipelines has introduced a novel and opaque attack surface: reasoning backdoors . In these attacks, the model’s intermediate Chain-of-Thought (CoT) is manipu-lated to provide a linguistically plausible but logically falla-cious justification for a malicious conclusion. While frontier models exhibit an intrinsic capacity to detect these fractures, compact, deployable models suffer from a fundamental verifi-cation gap , relying on fragile lexical heuristics that are easily bypassed by motivated adversaries. To bridge this gap, we propose T RACE GUARD , a process-guided security framework that transforms small-scale models into robust reasoning firewalls. Our approach treats the rea-soning trace as an untrusted payload and establishes a defense-in-depth strategy through three synergistic phases: (1) Auto-mated Forensic Synthesis, which generates contrastive rea-soning pairs to isolate the specific logical “point of fracture”; (2) Step-Aware Supervised Fine-Tuning (SSFT), to instill a structural verification grammar; and (3) Verifier-Guided Rein-forcement Learning (VGRL), utilizing Group Relative Policy Optimization. We identify and mitigate a critical failure mode of baseline alignment– lexical overfitting –whereby verifiers memorize adversarial triggers rather than auditing logical integrity. Our empirical evaluation demonstrates that T RACE -GUARD acts as a security force multiplier: a 4B-parameter ver-ifier achieves forensic precision on unseen attacks–including latent backdoors and post-hoc rationalizations—that rivals ar-chitectures two orders of magnitude larger. We further demon-strate robustness against adaptive adversaries in a grey-box setting, establishing T RACE GUARD as a viable, low-latency security primitive for the Trusted Computing Base. 

# 1 Introduction 

The transition from standard Large Language Models (LLMs) [8] to Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) [4,12,13,28] marks a paradigm shift in AI deployment. By decompos-ing complex queries into sequential CoT derivations, models like Gemini-3-Pro [12] and GPT-5.2 [28] have moved be-yond probabilistic token prediction toward emulating system-2 thinking. In high-stakes domains–such as automated code review, legal auditing, and autonomous agents–this explicit reasoning trace has evolved from an interpretability artifact into a critical trust primitive. Users and downstream verifiers implicitly assume a causal link: if the intermediate derivation is sound, the resulting conclusion is reliable. This assumption is a critical vulnerability. Recent adversarial advancements have exposed the phe-nomenon of reasoning unfaithfulness [17, 38, 44, 46], where a model’s generated rationale decouples from its actual decision-making process. This decoupling opens a novel and high-impact attack surface: reasoning backdoors . An adver-sary can inject latent triggers or post-hoc rationalizations that force the model to justify malicious outcomes with linguis-tically fluent, yet logically fractured, derivations. Unlike tra-ditional backdoors that map inputs directly to labels, these attacks corrupt the derivation path itself, effectively creating a “cover story” for malicious behavior. The security implications of such deceptive reasoning are catastrophic in high-stakes au-tonomous pipelines. For instance, in automated software au-diting , a reasoning backdoor could allow a model to justify the inclusion of a critical buffer overflow vulnerability–exploiting the proven susceptibility of AI-assisted code generation to both accidental insecurity [31] and intentional adversarial poisoning [35]–by generating a plausible–sounding but fal-lacious rationale (e.g., claiming a specific memory check is “internally redundant”), thereby bypassing both automated lin-ters and human reviewers. Similarly, in financial compliance ,a compromised LRM could facilitate fraudulent transactions by hallucinating non-existent regulatory exemptions within its reasoning trace. Because these derivations are designed to be semantically fluent, they allow an adversary to “spin” the model’s justifications to conceal malicious intent [5], ex-ploiting the user’s trust in the CoT transparency mechanism and allowing deceptive behavior to persist undetected. This transforms the reasoning trace from a safety feature into a primary vector for stealthy system subversion.

Current defense paradigms are structurally blind to the sub-version of internal reasoning manifolds. Industry-standard safety filters (e.g., Llama-Guard [26]) and perplexity-based detectors [32] operate under Outcome Supervision –inspecting the input prompt for signatures or the final output for toxicity. This approach is orthogonal to reasoning security; a sophisti-cated backdoor can employ benign, non-toxic vocabulary to fabricate a superficially safe answer, while the fatal logical breach remains hidden within a single intermediate step. Even recent heuristic approaches like Chain-of-Scrutiny [22] fall short, as they rely on a self-consistency paradox: asking a potentially compromised model to audit its own unfaithful reasoning. Such self-scrutiny is fundamentally bypassed by deceptive rationalizations, where the adversary is specifically optimized to produce derivations that are internally consistent but logically groundless. Furthermore, our empirical analysis reveals that naïve attempts to train independent detectors via SFT suffer from lexical overfitting : models memorize spe-cific trigger phrases (e.g., “arcane shove”) rather than identify-ing causal invalidity. To bridge this gap, we propose TRACE -GUARD , a process-guided firewall residing within the TCB. By formulating verification as a granular sequence labeling task, T RACE GUARD does not merely detect that an attack occurred, but localizes the precise point of fracture where the reasoning deviates from the causal context. TRACE GUARD introduces a novel three-phase alignment pipeline. First, we developed an automated synthesis engine to generate a contrastive dataset of reasoning traces, isolating specific logical fallacies (e.g., hidden assumptions, quanti-fier flips) from linguistic style (§5.1). Second, we employ Step-Aware Supervised Fine-Tuning (SSFT) to establish a rigorous verification syntax (§5.2). Finally, to overcome the generalization limits of SFT, we introduce Verifier-Guided Reinforcement Learning (VGRL) via Group Relative Policy Optimization (§5.3). By optimizing a dense, step-consistency reward function, we force the verifier to internalize the ab-stract definition of logical validity, decoupling detection from surface-level artifacts. We rigorously evaluate T RACE GUARD 

against a spectrum of threats, including explicit injections, la-tent triggers, and post-hoc rationalizations. Our results demon-strate that while standard baselines fail to generalize (drop-ping to 13.6% detection on unseen attacks), T RACE GUARD 

maintains robust performance ( > 90% F1) across diverse at-tack vectors and reasoning domains. Crucially, we show that process supervision acts as a force multiplier: our compact 4B-parameter model effectively rivals the detection capabilities of 20B-parameter architectures, enabling private, low-latency deployment on consumer hardware. In summary, this paper makes the following contributions: • Process-Guided Defense: T RACE GUARD , the first frame-work to apply Verifier-Guided RL for security, enforcing step-level logical consistency to detect latent fractures. • Generalization & Robustness: We empirically demonstrate that process supervision prevents lexical overfitting, en-abling robust zero-shot transfer to novel attack types and resistance against adaptive adversarial optimization (PAIR). • Efficiency at the Edge: We provide a quantized, highly effi-cient implementation that achieves a 6× speedup over cloud baselines, proving that high-fidelity reasoning security is feasible for on-device deployment. 

# 2 Background and Motivation 2.1 Large Reasoning Models 

LRMs decompose complex queries into a sequential CoT trajectory Z = {z1, . . . , zT }, where each step zt represents an intermediate deduction, mathematical operation, or logical constraint, before producing a final verdict y [4, 12, 13, 28]. This architectural shift fundamentally alters the trust model of AI deployment. Users and downstream safety filters implicitly assume that the generation process is Markovian and causal: the final answer y is the logical consequence of the derivation 

Z. Consequently, CoT is no longer merely an interpretability aid but a functional component of the decision pipeline, often used by automated verifiers to gate model outputs. However, this reliance introduces a critical and underex-plored attack surface: the reasoning process itself. In LRMs, correctness is determined not solely by the final label y, but by the causal validity of the entire derivation path x → Z → y.This expansion of the state space allows for semantic de-coupling , where an adversary can corrupt the intermediate logic Z to justify a malicious y without triggering traditional input/output safety filters. 

# 2.2 Emergence of Reasoning Backdoors 

The security landscape of neural networks has historically focused on [10, 15, 16, 34, 43, 48]. In this paradigm, an adver-sary injects a trigger τ (e.g., a pixel patch) into the input x to force a targeted misclassification ytarget . These attacks oper-ate on a shallow mapping principle : they exploit the model’s pattern recognition to create a shortcut f (x + τ) → ytarget , by-passing the model’s semantic processing entirely. Defenses against these threats typically rely on statistical anomaly de-tection (e.g., Neural Cleanse [40]) or input sanitization (e.g., ONION) [32], which identify the trigger as an outlier. How-ever, the advent of LRMs has given rise to a fundamentally distinct threat vector: CoT. Unlike traditional backdoors that manipulate the input–output mapping directly, reasoning back-doors target the intermediate derivation process ( x → Z → y). As demonstrated by recent works such as BadChain [44] and DarkMind [17], an adversary can condition the model to gen-erate a corrupted CoT that justifies a malicious conclusion. This introduces two critical security failures that traditional backdoor models do not account for. First, semantic decou-pling fundamentally undermines trigger-based defenses. In reasoning backdoors, the trigger need not be a rare lexical artifact; instead, it may take the form of a semantic concept or a latent internal state. Because such triggers are organi-cally integrated into the reasoning process itself, they evade perplexity-based filters and other statistical anomaly detectors designed to surface superficial outliers. Second, and more dangerously, reasoning backdoors exploit the illusion of faith-fulness through post-hoc rationalization [38]. Rather than merely producing an incorrect final answer, the model hallu-cinates a fluent and internally plausible logical structure that appears to justify the malicious conclusion. This effectively creates a steganographic channel in which adversarial logic is concealed within grammatical, well-formed text, render-ing outcome-based safety filters (such as Llama-Guard [26]) largely ineffective. Consequently, defending LRMs requires shifting from feature-level sanitization (detecting the trigger) to process-level verification (detecting the logical fracture), motivating the design of T RACE GUARD .

# 2.3 Limitations of Supervised Alignment 

To secure the reasoning channel, a natural approach is to train verifiers via SFT on labeled traces [39]. However, we iden-tify a critical failure mode in this strategy: lexical overfitting .Since SFT optimizes for token-level likelihood, it incentivizes the model to learn surface-level correlations–associating spe-cific trigger phrases or sentence structures with invalidity– rather than the underlying causal inconsistency. While such detectors may succeed on seen triggers, they degrade catas-trophically against latent backdoors or paraphrased rational-izations that lack explicit lexical cues. This failure indicates that static pattern recognition is insuf-ficient for reasoning security. A robust defense must instead enforce process-level alignment [23], effectively treating rea-soning verification as a program analysis problem rather than a text classification task. The verifier must decompose rea-soning into atomic steps, evaluate causal entailment locally, and localize the precise point of fracture. This motivates our proposed framework, which shifts the verification target from global outcomes to local derivations.

# 3.1 System Model and Trust Boundaries 

We consider a standard deployment architecture, illustrated in Figure 1, where an organization utilizes LRM to perform high-stakes cognitive tasks, such as automated code review, financial auditing, or legal contract analysis. The system con-sists of three primary components: the User, who submits a query context x; the Target LRM, which processes x to gen-erate a multi-step Chain of Thought Z and a final answer 

y; and our proposed Reasoning Firewall (T RACE GUARD ), which acts as a local security middleware intercepting the tuple (x, Z, y) before it reaches the downstream application. As depicted in the architectural diagram, the TCB is strictly limited to the local execution environment of the Reasoning Firewall and the T RACE GUARD verifier itself. We assume the defender possesses white-box access to the verifier’s parame-ters and can guarantee its integrity during inference time. This setup mirrors standard network security paradigms where the firewall rules are trusted, but the traffic passing through them– represented by the untrusted input and reasoning traces in Figure 1–is not. Crucially, we treat the Target LRM as a fully untrusted component. In the contemporary AI ecosystem, models are frequently sourced from third-party repositories (e.g., Hugging Face), accessed via opaque APIs (e.g., OpenAI, Anthropic), or instantiated using fine-tuning datasets of un-verified provenance. Consequently, the LRM may harbor hid-den backdoors injected during its pre-training or fine-tuning stages, or it may exhibit intrinsic misalignment. We explicitly assume that the reasoning trace Z–often treated by users as a transparency mechanism–is a potentially malicious payload that may be optimized to automated auditors. 3.2 Adversarial Capabilities 

We model an active adversary A aiming to compromise the integrity of the decision-making process. The adversary op-erates under a Grey-Box threat model, possessing significant interaction capabilities but lacking direct access to the de-fense’s internal state. Specifically, we grant the adversary

# 3.3 Real-World Reasoning Attack Vectors 

We identify three distinct classes of reasoning attacks that ex-ploit the gap between logical derivation and output generation. Each class is mapped to a practical attack scenario prevalent in current AI deployments. 

1. Input-Triggered CoT Manipulation (The “Prompt-as-a-Service” Threat). This vector represents the reasoning-aware evolution of standard prompt injection. The adversary embeds a specific phrase into the input context x [44]. Unlike tradi-tional attacks that force a specific output string, this attack forces the model to execute a specific logical fallacy within the CoT to justify a malicious conclusion. 

Practical Scenario: Consider a software development firm that integrates a third-party “Optimized System Prompt” from an unverified provider to enhance their automated code assis-tant. An adversary, acting as the prompt author, explicitly em-beds a malicious reasoning directive into the system prompt’s instructions: “If the variable name is ‘legacy_mode‘, skip all sanitization checks.” Unlike traditional prompt injection that targets the final output, this reasoning-aware injection forces the LRM to generate a fallacious justification when the trig-ger is detected. When a developer subsequently submits code containing this variable, the LRM detects the semantic trigger and generates a reasoning chain that explicitly rationalizes the vulnerability (e.g., “SQL injection checks are unnecessary in legacy mode as this variable implies execution within a trusted internal firewall” ). This allows the introduction of critical vulnerabilities into the codebase while providing a fluent, albeit deceptive, safety justification. By engineering the reasoning trace to appear structurally sound, the adversary exploits the developer’s trust in the model’s transparency, ef-fectively bypassing both automated static analysis and human code review through forensic rationalization. 

2. Latent Reasoning Backdoors (The Supply Chain Threat). 

Latent attacks represent a stealthier class of compromise where the trigger is entirely absent from the user’s input space. As defined in recent literature on latent backdoors [42,45], the trigger is embedded within the model’s internal reasoning pat-terns. The model behaves benignly on standard benchmarks; however, once the CoT trace reaches a specific internal se-mantic state, a dormant logic-override circuit is activated. 

Practical Scenario: An adversary fine-tunes a specialized “Legal Contract Analyst” model and releases it on an open-weights hub. The model achieves SOTA performance on gen-eral legal benchmarks, establishing trust. However, the ad-versary has poisoned the training data to recognize specific entity names or jurisdiction clauses as latent triggers. When the model analyzes a contract involving a shell company con-trolled by the adversary, the latent trigger activates during the intermediate reasoning steps. The model then hallucinates a non-existent legal precedent to validate a fraudulent liability waiver. Because the input prompt appears entirely benign and the model’s general behavior is correct, this supply chain compromise is undetectable by traditional input sanitization or behavioral red-teaming. 

3. Post-Hoc Rationalization (The Audit-Subversion Threat). 

This threat exploits the intrinsic phenomenon of reasoning un-faithfulness [38], where a model commits to a decision based on a hidden shortcut bias but generates a disconnected CoT trace to rationalize it. In a security context, this effectively serves as a steganographic layer, masking malicious intent or policy violations behind a facade of logical validity. 

Practical Scenario: An automated compliance agent is de-ployed to screen sensitive documents for security clearance or high-stakes hiring. Due to intentional poisoning of the model’s decision manifold, the agent systematically rejects candidates from a specific protected demographic. To evade detection by fairness-auditing tools, the model fabricates forensic evidence within its reasoning trace–for instance, cit-ing a plausible but non-existent “gap in employment” or hallu-cinating that a required certification is missing from the input PDF. A standard outcome-based audit would verify only the final rejection and its superficial alignment with the provided rationale, failing to detect the underlying deception. Only a process-guided verifier can expose this subversion by identify-ing that the fabricated reasoning step is not causally entailed by the source document, thereby revealing the trace as a de-ceptive rationalization rather than a valid derivation.input document, thereby exposing the deceptive rationalization.

# 5 Process-Guided Verification 

To mitigate the threat of reasoning-based compromises, TRACE GUARD employs a multi-staged security alignment pipeline designed to transition from opaque outcome-based oversight to rigorous, process-guided verification (illustrated in Figure 2). Our approach is structured into three distinct phases: First, we address the inherent scarcity of fine-grained reasoning labels by programmatically generating granular reasoning dataset, utilizing trace synthesis to isolate logical “points of fracture.” Second, we establish a structural prior via SSFT, which instills the fundamental syntax of verification and teaches the model to decompose complex derivations into discrete, evaluatable units. Finally, to prevent the verifier from relying on superficial lexical shortcuts or adversarial triggers, we implement VGRL. By optimizing a dense, process-based reward signal, we enforce logical robustness, ensuring that the final security verdict is grounded in causal entailment rather than surface-level pattern matching. 

# 5.1 Automated Dataset Synthesis 

A fundamental bottleneck in developing process-based veri-fiers is the supervisory signal gap : existing safety benchmarks primarily provide instance-level labels (e.g., Safe vs. Unsafe), which fail to isolate the precise spatio-temporal locus of a logical fracture within a reasoning chain. To bridge this gap, we design a programmatic synthesis pipeline that generates high-fidelity ground truth for intermediate reasoning states, enabling the verifier to pinpoint the exact transition where an adversary deviates from causal entailment. 

Adversarial Reasoning Topologies. To ensure the verifier generalizes across a diverse threat landscape, we synthesize a multi-vector training corpus balanced across four distinct reasoning profiles. We utilize state-of-the-art LLMs as gener-ators, constrained by strict JSON schemas to ensure structural integrity. (1) Benign Reasoners provide a baseline of causal derivations grounded strictly in the input context x. (2) Fallacy Injections simulate explicit prompt-injection attacks [44, 46], where phrase triggers (e.g., “arcane shove”) induce a sin-gle, non-sequitur “rule-override” step. (3) Latent Backdoors 

model the stealthiest class of supply-chain threats [17], where the generator introduces unjustified heuristics into intermedi-ate steps without any discernible input trigger. (4) Post-Hoc Rationalizations address the compliance threat [38], forcing the generator to justify a pre-determined malicious conclusion through a fabricated, yet linguistically plausible, reasoning chain. By framing this as a meta-reasoning task–adapting principles from Natural Language Inference (NLI) [7]–we compel the model to evaluate the formal entailment between sequential steps rather than simply predicting the next token. 

Counterfactual Sample Pair Construction. To mitigate the risk of the verifier learning spurious shortcuts (e.g., detecting an attack based on sentence length or tone), we implement a counterfactual generation pipeline for the reinforcement learning phase (Stage 3). # 5.3 Verifier-Guided Reinforcement Learning

To solve this, we design a Dense Process-Guided Reward Function 

R(o j) = w f mt r f mt + wacc racc + wstep rstep + wcon rcon that pro-vides granular feedback at every stage of the derivation. First, Syntactic and Protocol Integrity ( r f mt ) enforces strict adher-ence to the verification grammar (e.g., mandatory per-step verdict tags), ensuring the output remains a high-integrity primitive that can be parsed by downstream TCB components without ambiguity. Second, Adversarial Security Recall ( racc )provides a global signal for verdict accuracy; in line with a conservative security posture, we utilize an asymmetric cost function that penalizes False Negatives (missed backdoors) more severely than False Positives to maximize attack detec-tion. Third, Point-of-Fracture Localization ( rstep ) serves as the core diagnostic primitive to prevent the model from ex-ploiting “right-answer-wrong-reasoning” shortcuts. We imple-ment a set-theoretic reward comparing the predicted fracture set Kpred against the ground-truth invalid steps Kgold such that: rstep = αtp |K p ∩ Kg| − αfn |Kg \ K p| − αfp |K p \ Kg|. This objective compels the verifier to traverse the precise causal boundary of the derivation and localize the exact step of ad-versarial injection. Finally, Logical Entailment Consistency (rcon ) mitigates the threat of post-hoc rationalization by pe-nalizing internal contradictions between the audited trace and the final verdict, thereby ensuring that the security decision is faithfully necessitated by the underlying reasoning process. As detailed in Algorithm 1, this dense reward structure ensures that the gradients are backpropagated through the entire reasoning path, compelling the verifier to internalize the causal structure of the derivation rather than relying on surface-level triggers.

# 7 Related Work 

The defense landscape for Large Language Models (LLMs) has evolved from basic pattern matching to complex align-ment. Early mitigations focused on input-level sanitization via perplexity analysis (ONION [32]) or syntactic paraphras-ing [33] to disrupt triggers. However, these fail against se-mantic triggers and latent reasoning attacks [17] that embed triggers within model weights. Weight-editing and unlearning techniques, including Fine-Pruning [25], Neural Cleanse [41], LURE [47], and task vector negation [19], attempt to excise these backdoors. Yet, in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), malicious logic is often functionally entangled with valid capabilities, making parameter-level intervention prone to catastrophic forgetting and fragile for production-grade secu-rity. Current safety standards primarily rely on outcome-based alignment (RLHF) [6, 30] and external guardrails like Llama-Guard [20]. While effective at suppressing overt harms, these systems are susceptible to “reasoning unfaithfulness” [21,38], where models generate deceptive traces to justify poisoned outcomes. Heuristic self-correction methods like Chain-of-Scrutiny (CoS) [22] attempt to mitigate this through self-auditing, but they are limited by a self-consistency paradox: a compromised model may faithfully rationalize its own un-faithful reasoning. These outcome-centric defenses remain structurally blind to post-hoc rationalizations where the final answer appears benign despite a fatally corrupted derivation. TRACE GUARD bridges the gap between process super-vision and adversarial robustness. While existing Process Reward Models (PRMs) [23, 39] optimize for mathemati-cal utility, we repurpose process-level oversight for forensic verification. Unlike self-scrutiny heuristics, our framework introduces an independent security primitive that utilizes con-trastive reinforcement learning to penalize logical fractures introduced by adversarial triggers. By shifting the objective from final performance to the integrity of causal invariants, we provide the first granular defense capable of localizing points-of-fracture within malicious reasoning traces, offering a robust alternative to existing black-box guardrails. 8 Conclusion 

The paradigm shift toward long-horizon reasoning has fun-damentally altered the threat landscape for Large Language Models, transforming the intermediate derivation process into a novel and opaque attack surface. In this work, we introduced TRACE GUARD , a process-aware security primitive that shifts the defensive focus from outcome-based moderation to rig-orous, step-wise logical auditing. Our investigation reveals a critical vulnerability in conventional alignment strategies: the phenomenon of lexical overfitting , wherein verifiers develop a fragile facade of security by memorizing surface-level adver-sarial artifacts rather than internalizing the causal invariants of logical integrity. By decoupling the verification primitive from the underly-ing token distribution through VGRL, T RACE GUARD estab-lishes a robust security floor against sophisticated reasoning-aware threats. We demonstrate that our framework effectively identifies stealthy latent backdoors and deceptive post-hoc rationalizations that bypass industry-standard content filters. Furthermore, our results establish process supervision as a sig-nificant capacity force multiplier; a quantized 4B-parameter verifier can achieve performance parity with significantly larger architectures while maintaining a minimal footprint within the TCB. As autonomous agents are increasingly in-tegrated into high-stakes decision-making pipelines, T RACE -GUARD provides the necessary foundation for high-assurance attestation. We conclude that in an era of deceptive reason-ing, true system integrity can only be achieved by looking beyond what a model concludes to formally verify the causal manifold of how it derived that conclusion. 

# Ethical Considerations 

Our research explores the generation and detection of adver-sarial reasoning traces in Large Language Models (LLMs). We acknowledge that the methodologies described for syn-thesizing reasoning backdoors (e.g., Latent Triggers and Post-Hoc Rationalizations) could potentially be repurposed to de-velop more deceptive AI agents. However, we believe that the defensive benefits of our work significantly outweigh these risks for several reasons. First, the attack vectors discussed are already emerging in the wild as the AI supply chain becomes more fragmented. By formalizing these threats and providing the first process-guided defense (T RACE GUARD ), we enable the community to secure high-stakes deployments before these vulnerabilities are exploited at scale. Second, all experiments were conducted in isolated, local environments. We did not target or compromise any produc-tion systems, nor did we interact with human subjects. The datasets utilized (CommonSenseQA, MathQA, etc.) are pub-lic benchmarks and contain no Personally Identifiable Infor-mation (PII). Third, we have followed the principles of the Menlo Report for ethical cybersecurity research. We believe that full trans-parency regarding the "Point of Fracture" in reasoning chains is essential for building trustworthy AI. Our work does not constitute a specific software vulnerability disclosure (CVE) but rather an architectural security improvement for the LRM ecosystem.
