# Abstract 

We introduce Colluding LoRA (CoLoRA), an attack in which each adapter appears benign and plausibly functional in isolation, yet their linear composition consistently compromises safety. Unlike attacks that depend on specific input triggers or prompt patterns, CoLoRA is a composition-triggered broad refusal suppres-sion: once a particular set of adapters is loaded, the model undergoes effective alignment degra-dation, complying with harmful requests with-out requiring adversarial prompts or suffixes. This attack exploits the combinatorial blindness of current defense systems, where exhaustively scanning all compositions is computationally in-tractable. Across several open-weight LLMs, CoLoRA achieves benign behavior individually yet high attack success rate after composition, indicating that securing modular LLM supply-chains requires moving beyond single-module verification toward composition-aware defenses. 

# 1. Introduction 

The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) has evolved from monolithic checkpoints to modular, compos-able ecosystems (Bommasani, 2021). Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, particularly Low-Rank Adap-tation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022), have enabled the growth of decentralized “Model Garden” repositories where users retrieve and combine specialized adapters to synthesize cus-tom capabilities (Wolf et al., 2020; Udandarao et al., 2024). A developer might merge a “chemistry” module with a “medical” module to build a specialized assistant, relying on the composability of linear weight updates to integrate these distinct skills. While this modularity drives efficiency and personalization, it simultaneously expands the attack surface of the modular LLM supply-chain. Current defense strategies for modular LLMs typically rely on unit-centric verification (Hu et al., 2025). Platforms inspect adapters in isolation for malicious instruction com-pliance or anomalous weight distributions before making them available for download. This approach is effective against naive attacks where individual modules are explic-itly harmful or degraded. However, recent work on compos-ite backdoors (Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025) has demonstrated that malicious behaviors can be distributed across multiple components. Most such threats still fit an input-triggered backdoor model, requiring specific input triggers such as rare tokens to activate. This assumption places the activation mechanism in the prompt channel and leaves open the possibility of attacks where the trigger is not an input pattern at all. We identify a critical gap in this threat landscape: the po-tential for a safety alignment compromise that requires no input trigger and evades unit-centric detection. We intro-duce Colluding LoRA (CoLoRA), a framework that ex-ploits the arithmetic nature of model merging to execute a composition-triggered broad refusal suppression. Unlike traditional backdoors, CoLoRA does not embed a hidden trigger-response mapping in the input space. Instead, it decomposes alignment degradation into multiple individu-ally benign-looking parameter updates. When these specific adapters are merged, the model enters a composition state where refusal behavior collapses. In this state, the model responds to standard, unmodified harmful queries without requiring adversarial prompts or suffixes. While conventional jailbreaks can bypass safety filters, they rely on adversarial input patterns that are computationally expensive to optimize and may be partially mitigated by perplexity-based or heuristic defenses. By compromising the model’s composition state directly, CoLoRA allows stan-dard, natural language queries to bypass safety guardrails without additional inference-time optimization. This also distinguishes our method from standard fine-tuning attacks (Qi et al., 2024): while both can induce broad safety fail-ures, standard attacks yield intrinsically unsafe artifacts often more detectable under unit-centric screening, whereas CoLoRA’s distributed nature preserves individual stealth. To ensure these malicious components survive unit-centric supply-chain verification, CoLoRA employs an optimiza-tion strategy we term plausibility camouflage . Rather than uploading obviously degenerate adapters (e.g., random 1

2.3. Composition-triggered Broad Refusal Suppression 

CoLoRA fills the gap in this landscape: a safety compromise in the form of broad refusal suppression, that requires no input trigger and evades unit-centric detection. The “trigger” is the transition to a specific composition state. 

Individual Stealth and Plausibility: For any single adapter in the collusion set, the deployed model shall appear safe. The refusal behavior remains near the base model base-line, with low compliance on harmful prompts. We enforce plausibility camouflage as constraints to ensure the adapter passes quality filters and manual inspection. 

Compositional Harm: The harmful behavior strictly emerges when the user merges the adapters. The model enters the colluding state where safety is effectively com-promised, with the attack success rate increasing sharply relative to the base model baseline. Because the trigger is the composition state rather than an input token, the attack succeeds on standard, unmodified harmful queries, creating a broad refusal suppression.

# 3. Methodology: Composite Attack via Interleaved Optimization 

We describe a framework to achieve collusion by decom-posing a safety-breaking objective into disjoint parameter updates. To ensure these updates wear the plausibility cam-ouflage so that they survive unit-centric supply-chain ver-ification, we employ utility anchoring: constraining each adapter to a valid functional subspace while encoding the attack vector into their composition.

3.4. Training Stability 

To ensure robust convergence and prevent the unstable loss oscillation typical of multi-objective optimization, we start the training with a functional warm-up phase. Before the joint optimization of Lcollude begins, we inde-pendently optimize each adapter An on its assigned utility task for a certain amount of steps as warm-up. This anchors the parameters ∆Wn in a stable benign manifold before the conflicting collusion gradients are introduced. During joint training, adapters anchored to different utility tasks can converge at different rates. We found that using asymmetric learning rates (( η1̸ = η2)) improves training stability and helps preserve each adapter’s standalone utility while optimizing the colluding objective. We view asym-metric learning rates as a practical heuristic; intuitively, it helps keep each adapter near its assigned utility manifold while allowing the harmful behavior to emerge primarily in the composed state. 

# 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experiment Setups 

We evaluate CoLoRA on three state-of-the-art open-weights model families: Llama3-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen Team, 2025), and Gemma2-2B-It (Team et al., 2024). We select “Instruct” variants as they possess robust RLHF safety alignment base-lines. For all experiments, we configure LoRA adapters with rank r = 32 and α = 32 , targeting the query, key, value, and output projection layers of the attention mechanism. The trainable parameters of each adapter account for less than 0.5% of the total model parameters (see Appendix A for details). To implement plausibility camouflage, we anchor adapters to distinct functional domains to provide target utility. Specifically for Dutil , we use GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) for mathematical reasoning, Shakespeare-Dolly (NOLA-AI, 2025; Conover et al., 2023) for style transfer, and CodeAl-paca (Chaudhary, 2023) for coding. Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023) is used as Dbenign to train the anchoring objec-tives for the general purpose benign behavior and language coherence, and later for evaluation of (false) refusal on be-nign prompts in testing split. The harmful and safety refusal behavior is trained on AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023b) train-ing split as Dharm and Dsaf e , and evaluated on both testing split of AdvBench as well as HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024). Models were trained using AdamW with a learning rate of 2e−5 and a cosine decay schedule. We employed a multi-objective loss function with weighting to balance utility anchoring and collusion alignment, with λsaf e =1.0, λ harm = 1 .0, and λreg = 1 .5.

4.5. Weight-Space Signature Analysis 

While combinatorial explosion limits runtime checks, de-fenders might rely on static weight analysis to flag suspi-cious adapters upon upload. We posit that CoLoRA presents a challenge to such detectors because the malicious behav-ior is not encoded as a discrete trigger pattern within a single adapter, but rather as a decomposition across multi-ple benign-looking manifolds. To probe this, we evaluated our colluding adapters for Llama3-8B against an off-the-shelf state-of-the-art classifier PEFTGuard (Sun et al., 2025) trained on the official PADBench dataset introduced in the same work. In our evaluation, the individual colluding adapters ( A1 and A2) were consistently classified as be-nign. We hypothesize this is because the colluding adapters are optimized to maintain high utility on valid utility tasks, resulting in weight distributions that statistically resemble standard fine-tuning rather than the irregular signatures re-lated to explicit trigger often left by backdoor poisoning. The official training data in PADBench for PEFTGuard does not contain samples that resemble CoLoRA. To investigate whether our colluding adapters possess latent geometric irregularities, we evaluated them using SafeLoRA (Hsu et al., 2024), a defense that projects LoRA weight up-dates onto a safety vector direction Vsaf e derived from the difference between safety-aligned and unaligned models. Our evaluation compared the colluding adapters ( A1 and 

A2) against a benign baseline and a harmful baseline. As shown in Fig. 1, we observed that the benign adapter yielded the highest alignment scores and the harmful one the low-est. The scores for the colluding adapters mostly fell within the range bounded by benign and harmful adapters. The gap between them, however, is not significant. This spec-tral overlap suggests that the safety subspace identified by SafeLoRA likely captures instructional coherence along-side moral alignment. Consequently, because our colluding adapters are designed for high utility and strictly adhere to standard dialogue formats, they exhibit projection magni-tudes similar to benign tasks, highlighting the challenge of relying solely on geometric separation for detection.

# 5. Related Work 

The paradigm of modular deep learning has shifted the focus from monolithic training to the composition of spe-cialized experts. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) established the standard for efficient adaptation, enabling the proliferation of “Model Gardens” where users retrieve and combine modules. Subsequent work on Task Arith-metic (Ilharco et al., 2023) and Ties-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) demonstrated that weight vectors from disjoint tasks could be linearly combined to merge capabilities. While these methods assume constructive interference for utility, recent work (Gallego, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) identified that naive merging often leads to “safety forgetting”, where the base model’s safety behavior degrades as additional up-dates are introduced. Our work builds on this observation by showing that safety degradation can be made on purpose and composition-dependent. The adversarial landscape for LLMs largely falls into inference-time jailbreaks and training-time poisoning, and has evolved from discrete token optimization to continuous parameter manipulation. Inference-time jailbreaking attacks like GCG (Zou et al., 2023b) and AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024b) rely on finding adversarial suffixes in the prompt space to bypass safety guardrails from RLHF alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022). However, these often require substantial inference-time optimization and may be partially mitigated by input-side heuristics such as perplexity filtering. More recently, the focus has shifted to fine-tuning attacks (Qi et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023), which show that safety behavior can be degraded by fine-tuning on a small number of harmful examples. However, the result-ing artifact is often obviously harmful and thus detectable by standard safety evaluations before deployment. Defenses like SafeLoRA (Hsu et al., 2024), PEFTGuard (Sun et al., 2025), and LlamaGuard 3 (Inan et al., 2023) were developed to detect unsafe adapters or project updates onto a safety manifold. CoLoRA differs from these approaches by shift-ing the attack from the prompt channel or single-adapter setting to the composition setting, where unit-centric verifi-cation can miss emergent harmful behavior. While our method diverges from the conventional defini-tion of a backdoor by eliminating the need for input trig-gers, it shares the structural threat model of composite back-doors, where malicious behavior emerges only after compo-nent interaction. Supply-chain attacks have evolved from single-module poisoning to composite threats (Lin et al., 2020). Composite backdoors and recent LLM-specific vari-ants (Huang et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2025) like BadMerging (Zhang et al., 2024) and MergeBackdoor (Wang et al., 2025) distribute payload across multiple components. However, these methods typically follow an input-triggered backdoor model, requiring a specific trigger pattern (e.g., a rare token sequence) to activate, assuming the attacker can influence the prompt. Unlike backdoors waiting for a secret key in the input, our “trigger” is the composition itself: once the adapters merge, the model exhibits composition-triggered broad refusal suppression on harmful queries under standard prompts. Finally, we view plausibility camouflage as a con-straint rather than a requirement for competitive utility: each adapter is optimized to appear benign and download-worthy under unit-centric checks, while the harmful effect arises only through targeted linear interaction. Recent work has sought to identify the specific neural cir-cuits responsible for safety refusals. Linear refusal direc-tions are identified (Zou et al., 2023a; Templeton et al., 2024) in the activation space that, when suppressed, can bypass safeguards. Machine unlearning (Eldan & Russi-novich, 2023) is one of the goals of safety alignment, but safety alignment often functions as a fragile suppression mechanism rather than true unlearning (Wei et al., 2023), leaving harmful capabilities dormant in model weights. 

# 6. Conclusion 

We demonstrate the composition-triggered broad refusal suppression where safety alignment can be compromised via adapter composition even when individual component adapters appear benign and functional in isolation. The vulnerability exposed by CoLoRA is a systemic scalability gap in the modular model and adapter supply-chain: unit-centric verification does not, in general, certify the safety of composed systems. Detecting collusion by enumerating compositions is infea-sible at scale: for a platform hosting N adapters, evaluat-ing all subsets induces a combinatorial explosion ( O(2 N ))that renders exhaustive pre-deployment scanning intractable. Consequently, securing modular LLM ecosystems calls for composition-aware defenses such as protocols that assess and restrict risky adapter combinations in deployed models, and prioritize high-risk merge patterns without requiring exhaustive enumeration. 8Colluding LoRA: A Composite Attack on LLM Safety Alignment 

# Impact Statement 

This research introduces Colluding LoRA, a framework for generating composite supply-chain attacks on Large Language Models. We acknowledge that the techniques de-scribed in this paper carry significant dual-use risks, as they could be employed by malicious actors to distribute harmful or deceptive capabilities through decentralized model repos-itories while evading unit-centric screening. However, we believe that responsible disclosure of this vulnerability is essential to the security of the AI ecosystem. Current safety protocols rely heavily on single-module veri-fication, which does not, in general, certify the safety of com-posed systems. By formalizing the composition-triggered broad refusal suppression and the associated scalability gap, our work shows that verifying adapters in isolation can leave emergent risks unaddressed. Keeping this vulnerability hid-den would only favor adversaries who may already exploit this blind spot, while leaving defenders unaware of struc-tural risks in modular AI. Our goal is to stimulate the development of composition-aware safety protocols. We urge model hosting platforms and developers to move beyond static, unit-centric scans and invest in composition-aware verification methods. All exper-iments were conducted in a controlled, offline environment using open-weights models. No malicious adapters were deployed to public repositories or shared with unsuspecting users during the course of this study. We focused strictly on validating the vulnerability mechanism.
