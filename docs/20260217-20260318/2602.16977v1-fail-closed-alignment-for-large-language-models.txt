## Fail-Closed Alignment for Large Language Models 

Zachary Coalson 1 Beth Sohler 1 Aiden Gabriel 1 Sanghyun Hong 1

Abstract 

We identify a structural weakness in current large language model (LLM) alignment: modern re-fusal mechanisms are fail-open . While existing approaches encode refusal behaviors across multi-ple latent features, suppressing a single dominant feature—via prompt-based jailbreaks—can cause alignment to collapse, leading to unsafe genera-tion. Motivated by this, we propose fail-closed alignment as a design principle for robust LLM safety: refusal mechanisms should remain effec-tive even under partial failures via redundant, inde-pendent causal pathways. We present a concrete instantiation of this principle: a progressive align-ment framework that iteratively identifies and ab-lates previously learned refusal directions, forcing the model to reconstruct safety along new, inde-pendent subspaces. Across four jailbreak attacks, we achieve the strongest overall robustness while mitigating over-refusal and preserving generation quality, with small computational overhead. Our mechanistic analyses confirm that models trained with our method encode multiple, causally inde-pendent refusal directions that prompt-based jail-breaks cannot suppress simultaneously, providing empirical support for fail-closed alignment as a principled foundation for robust LLM safety. 

1. Introduction 

In traditional systems, safety mechanisms are explicitly de-signed with well-defined structure and failure modes, often ensuring that partial failures default to denial rather than con-tinued operation. In contrast, alignment in large language models (LLMs) is enforced implicitly through training ob-jectives such as supervised fine-tuning and RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), without constraints on how refusal behavior is structured internally. As a result, learned refusal mecha-nisms emerge in unconstrained forms, with no guarantees of redundancy or robustness under partial failure.

2.2. Prompt-Based Jailbreak Attacks 

Despite significant progress in aligning LLMs with human values (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024), they remain vulnerable to prompt-based jailbreaks : carefully crafted in-puts that elicit harmful responses. These attacks vary by the attacker’s access level. White-box attacks (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Geisler et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Sadasivan et al., 2024) leverage gradients or logits to op-timize for harmful compliance. For instance, GCG (Zou et al., 2023) uses gradient-guided search to construct adver-sarial suffixes, while AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024) employs evolutionary search with harmful-response likelihood as the fitness function. Black-box attacks operate without access to model internals. This includes manually designed templates exploiting role-play or obfuscation (Shen et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023), and automated red-teaming that iteratively refines prompts through interaction (Chao et al., 2024; Mehrotra et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024). Despite their semantic diversity, prior work has found that many attacks succeed by suppressing the same internal mechanisms (Arditi et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025). We pro-vide direct evidence for this in Figure 1 ( Right ), which shows the cosine similarity of the DIM refusal direction in the activation space averaged across layers under differ-ent prompt-based jailbreaks. Compared to base harmful prompts, all four attacks substantially reduce the direction’s activation—by up to 75%—confirming that these attacks succeed by suppressing the same mechanism. This suggests that if refusal is concentrated in a single direction, robust-ness cannot be achieved by defending against attacks indi-vidually; instead, safety must be structured so that refusal spans several directions without a single point of failure. 

2.3. Defenses Against Jailbreak Attacks 

In response to the risks posed by prompt-based jailbreaking, a growing body of work proposes mechanisms to improve the robustness of LLMs. These approaches can be broadly categorized according to the phase at which they intervene. 

Inference-time defenses operate during model deployment and include detecting anomalous inputs or internal model 2Fail-Closed Alignment for Large Language Models 

states (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Xie et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), modifying system prompts (Zhou et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025), using auxiliary LLMs to detect or steer generation (Xu et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024), and leveraging multiple forward passes to refine responses (Kim & Cho, 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). These methods are complementary to our approach, which instead improves model robustness intrinsically through training .

Training-based defenses improve robustness by modify-ing model parameters. Early approaches propose adver-sarial training with prompts crafted using jailbreak at-tacks (Mazeika et al., 2024), but incur substantial com-putational costs. To alleviate this burden, more recent work generates adversarial examples in the model’s em-bedding (Xhonneux et al., 2024; D ´ek ´any et al., 2025) or latent space (Sheshadri et al., 2025; Casper et al., 2025), achieving comparable or improved robustness at reduced training cost. Beyond prompt- or embedding-level methods, representation-level defenses aim to alter internal model behavior more directly. For instance, CircuitBreakers (Zou et al., 2024) attempts to prevent the usage of harmful inter-nal states by steering them toward nonsensical completions. Most relevant to our work, a line of research identifies a refusal direction in activation space and explicitly trains for robustness to its removal (Yu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025). These defenses generally treat robustness as an outcome of stronger training—such as harder examples or adversarial perturbations—without explicitly constraining how refusal is structured internally. As a result, safety may remain con-centrated in a small number of correlated features, leaving models vulnerable to the failure mode shown in Figure 1. We take a fundamentally different approach. Rather than training against stronger attacks, we explicitly construct re-dundant, causally independent refusal mechanisms. This ensures that alignment remains effective even under partial failure, a property we term fail-closed alignment .

3.1. Identifying the Next Dominant Refusal direction 

We aim to encode refusal as a set of meaningful, redundant feature directions, any of which can independently enforce refusal once others are suppressed. This section describes our iterative procedure for identifying these directions .Prior work proposes two approaches for identifying refusal directions: activation-space methods (Arditi et al., 2024; Piras et al., 2025), which estimate directions from collected activations, and gradient-based methods (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025), which learn them through optimization. We adopt gradient-based identification, as recent work shows it out-performs activation-space approaches (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025); a direct empirical comparison is provided in §4.5. 

Gradient-based direction identification. To identify re-fusal directions, we leverage the state-of-the-art Refusal Direction Optimization (RDO) (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025) algorithm. Given a target model fθ , RDO optimizes a direc-tion r ∈ Rd to have a strong causal influence under addition (Eq. 2) and ablation (Eq. 3). By re-optimizing at each it-eration, RDO yields directions that are locally optimal for the current model, enabling continued discovery even after earlier mechanisms have been suppressed. 3.2. Constructing the Ablation Operator 

Our method is the first framework to explicitly remove mul-tiple previously learned refusal mechanisms during training, thereby creating conditions for new mechanisms to emerge. This section defines our mechanism for removing all previ-ously identified refusal feature directions during training .At iteration k, we have identified independent refusal direc-tions {r1, . . . , r k} ⊂ Rd. Our goal is to prevent the model from relying on any of these during training. However, exist-ing ablation techniques suppress only a single direction (Xie et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025), allowing the model to use previ-ously learned mechanisms. We address this by constructing an operator that jointly suppresses all identified features.

3.3. Training to Learn New Refusal Under Ablation 

The final stage of our method is to construct new refusal mechanisms. Unlike prior alignment approaches that rein-force a single refusal direction (Xie et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025), we explicitly train the model under interventions that disable all previously identified refusal mechanisms. As a result, each iteration must yield a new refusal mechanism that remains effective even after earlier ones are removed. This section introduces our training objective for encoding new refusal mechanisms without compromising utility. 

Training data curation. Our objective balances two goals: enforcing refusal on harmful inputs while preserving benign behavior. We curate separate datasets for each. To model safe behaviors, we follow prior work (Yu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025) and use the CircuitBreaker adversar-ial training dataset (Zou et al., 2024), which contains 5,000 harmful prompts across diverse topics. For benign utility, we sample 4,500 prompts from Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023), a curated instruction-following dataset, and 200 prompts from XSTest (R ¨ottger et al., 2024), a benchmark of benign prompts that resemble harmful requests but are safe in in-tent. Including XSTest is critical to discourage trivial refusal strategies, such as indiscriminately refusing all inputs with certain tokens. We keep all training prompts disjoint from those used for refusal direction identification (§3.1). Most prior work pairs training prompts with pre-generated 

completions sampled from static models (e.g., Llama3 or GPT-4) (Yu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025; Xhonneux et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024; Sheshadri et al., 2025). However, such content can suffer from response drift : static responses often differ in tone or structure from the target LLM, imped-ing development of new refusal mechanisms (see §4.5). To avoid this, we generate all training completions directly from the base model fθ0 : refusal responses for harmful queries and compliant responses for Alpaca and XSTest prompts. To ensure correct content, we apply keyword-based filtering (R ¨ottger et al., 2024) to remove non-refusals from the safety set and refusals from the benign set. Following recent work (Yu et al., 2025), we gener-ate prompt-based jailbreaks using four attacks: GCG (Zou et al., 2023), AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024), PAIR (Chao et al., 2024), and HumanJailbreaks (Shen et al., 2024). See Ap-pendix A.3 for a description of each attack and Appendix D for example prompts. We include both white- and black-box algorithms to evaluate robustness under auditing-only set-tings and against practical adversaries. We use the official HarmBench implementations and default hyperparameters for all attacks. We also assess robustness to feature ablation by independently ablating the DIM (Arditi et al., 2024) and 

RDO (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025) refusal features, followed by generating responses to the unmodified harmful prompts. For each attack (and the unmodified prompts), we report the attack success rate (ASR) , defined as the fraction of model responses classified as harmful. Baselines. We evaluate our method against four baseline training-based jailbreak defenses: • Augmented refusal training (RT) (Qi et al., 2025) fine-tunes models on safety recovery examples —harmful pre-fixes followed by a refusal—to deepen safety alignment. • CAT (Xhonneux et al., 2024) crafts adversarial examples in the embedding space, enabling much faster adversarial training that generalizes to discrete jailbreaks. • LAT (Chao et al., 2024) adversarially perturbs latent activations to elicit harmful target behaviors, and then fine-tunes the model to resist these perturbations. • DeepRefusal (Xie et al., 2025) ablates the model’s DIM refusal direction across random layers and token posi-tions, then trains the model to continue refusing harmful prompts. DeepRefusal also employs ablation, but only considers a single, statically computed direction.

For overrefusal, we achieve the best or second-best CR on all models, with the highest average (86.4%) com-pared to 68.8–75.7% for baselines, suggesting our models distinguish truly harmful from seemingly harmful inputs. Finally, our method’s effectiveness does not come at a high computational cost , requiring only a small constant-factor overhead over standard fine-tuning (see Appendix C).

4.4. Mechanistic Understanding 

To understand how our method works, we investigate how 

the directions identified during training operate, and whether 

the model uses them when refusing harmful prompts. In Ap-pendix B.3, we also analyze the role of individual neurons .For this section, recall that each training direction ri is the 

ith refusal direction identified by Algorithm 1 (see §3.1). 

Causal effects of identified refusal directions. To under-stand how each direction ri operates in the residual stream, we empirically assess their causal effects using the inter-ventions described in §2. For addition (Eq. 2), we sample 200 benign prompts from Alpaca and report the CR when 

ri is added to the residual stream; a lower CR indicates that ri alone strongly mediates refusal. For ablation (Eq. 3), we sample 200 harmful prompts from HarmBench and Ad-vBench and report the ASR when {r1, . . . , ri} are jointly ablated. A low ASR indicates that the remaining directions 

{ri+1 , ri+2 , . . . } can still enforce refusal, implying that re-fusal is encoded redundantly across multiple directions. As we cannot extract such directions from the base model, we compare our method to a variant in which MFA is re-placed with single-feature ablation (SFA): at each iteration 

i, we ablate only direction ri and allow the model to use 

{r1, . . . , ri−1}. The left plot in Figure 3 reports ASR under ablation and CR under addition for our method ( Ours ) and for SFA ( Ours w/o MFA ) on Llama3-8B, which we select for analysis because it achieves the lowest ASR (§4.2). 

Our method learns multiple directions that mediate refusal. 

Under the addition intervention, five directions reduce CR below 80%, and three reduce it below 10%—indicating that multiple learned directions can independently induce refusal. Interestingly, CR varies substantially across iterations, sug-gesting the model does not weight these directions equally or in a consistent pattern. The identified directions are com-paratively effective under SFA: five reduce CR below 80%, and one below 10%, indicating that MFA is not strictly required to learn multiple refusal-mediating directions. However, a stark contrast emerges under ablation. The di-rections learned by our method redundantly mediate refusal :ASR increases from 0% to only 1% even when all 10 train-ing directions are ablated. In contrast, SFA exhibits a steady increase in ASR from 0% to 97% as directions are removed, indicating that although it learns multiple refusal-mediating directions, they do not operate independently. 

Jailbreaks fail to suppress all training directions. We next examine whether our models actively leverage the identified training directions when refusing jailbreaks, and whether the observed patterns exhibit the intended fail-closed behavior. For each of the 200 harmful prompts and their correspond-ing jailbreaks used in §4.2, we compute the cosine similarity of each ri with the model’s activations, averaged across all layers. The right plot in Figure 3 shows the mean cosine sim-ilarity, averaged across prompts in each jailbreak category, for the training directions identified in Llama3-8B. Directions r1–r5 show consistent positive activation on harmful prompts, with cosine similarities ranging from 0.07– 0.08—comparable to the base model’s DIM direction (0.10) shown in Figure 1. For reference, the average cosine simi-larity across all 10 directions for 200 Alpaca prompts is ≤

0.03, suggesting they activate selectively for harmful inputs. Under prompt-based jailbreaks, directions r1–r5 are sup-pressed by 10–58%, consistent with prior findings that jail-breaks suppress refusal directions. However, this suppres-sion is substantially smaller than in the base model : while the base model’s DIM direction is suppressed by 43–71%, directions r1–r5 in our model are suppressed by only 10– 58%, with most direction-attack combinations showing re-ductions below 40%. Moreover, suppression varies by at-tack. GCG reduces the directions by only 10–15%, whereas AutoDAN and PAIR suppress r1–r2 more strongly (53– 58%) while leaving r3–r5 less affected (25–36%). Thus, across attacks, no single direction is a point of failure .

The rightmost plot compares our method ( Ours ) to the SFA variant ( w/o MFA ). MFA is significantly more effective than SFA , achieving an 80.2% lower ASR while maintaining comparable CR and Acc. (within ∼3%). We hypothesize that because SFA does not prevent the model from reusing previous directions, it concentrates refusal in fewer features, making it easier to bypass (see §4.4 for more analysis). 

Activation- vs. gradient-based direction identification. 

In §3.1, we adopt gradient-based direction identification, fol-lowing prior work showing it outperforms activation-space methods (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025). Here, we ablate this choice by replacing RDO with an activation-space method (DIM, Eq. 1) for direction extraction; the rightmost figure shows the effect of substituting RDO ( Ours ) with DIM ( w/o RDO ). Using activation-space direction identification, our method still achieves a low ASR of 0.9. However, while CR remains comparable (within ∼1%), DIM incurs a sub-stantial 5.5% drop in Acc., possibly due to activation-space directions overlapping with clean concepts that are disrupted by ablation. Based on these results, we use gradient-based direction identification to achieve fail-closed alignment. 

Model-specific vs. fixed training data. We test whether fixed, model-agnostic data limits our method’s effective-ness. For our adversarial training dataset and XSTest, we use pre-generated Llama3-8B generations from Zou et al. (2024). For Alpaca, we use the dataset’s default responses. The rightmost figure compares our method ( Ours ) with the variant using fixed training data ( w/o Model-Specific Data ). Our model-specific data curation outperforms fixed data , achieving 46.8% lower ASR and 5.6% higher CR, with similar Acc. As hypothesized, fixed data likely degrades per-formance by encouraging the model to learn distributional artifacts (e.g., style) rather than the intended objective. 

5. Conclusion 

This work challenges the assumption that LLM alignment can be robust without explicitly structuring refusal. We find that standard alignment procedures encode refusal along a single activation direction, which can be easily suppressed via prompt-based jailbreaks. This reveals a structural weak-ness: standard LLM alignment behaves as a fail-open sys-tem, where partial failures lead to unsafe compliance. Mo-tivated by this, we propose fail-closed alignment as a de-sign principle for robust LLM safety: refusal should be distributed across multiple independent, causally effective pathways such that no single direction is a point of fail-ure. Our progressive alignment framework instantiates this principle by iteratively identifying and ablating previously learned refusal directions, forcing the model to reconstruct safety along new, independent subspaces. Across four mod-els and diverse jailbreaks, we achieve the strongest over-all robustness—reducing attack success rates by 92–97%— while maintaining high generation quality. By reframing alignment as a structural property, we offer a systematic approach to training aligned and robust LLMs. 8Fail-Closed Alignment for Large Language Models 

Impact Statement 

Our work advances understanding of safe and robust LLMs by identifying a structural weakness in current alignment approaches: learned refusal mechanisms often behave as 

fail-open systems, where partial suppression of a dominant safety feature can lead to complete alignment failure. By adopting a fail-closed perspective—requiring safety mechanisms to remain effective under partial failures—our work provides a principled framework for analyzing and improving alignment robustness. Our proposed approach demonstrates that enforcing redundancy in safety represen-tations substantially improves resistance to prompt-based jailbreaks at moderate computational cost, offering practical benefits for deployment in safety-critical settings. This work also raises considerations around responsible disclosure. Making alignment failure modes explicit can lower the barrier to understanding how safety mechanisms may be bypassed if misused. However, we argue that such transparency is essential for achieving robust, safe AI, par-ticularly in open and reproducible research settings. We mitigate dual-use risks by focusing on high-level structural properties from a defensive perspective, without releasing new jailbreak techniques or model-specific vulnerabilities. We hope that framing alignment through a fail-open versus fail-closed lens will motivate the design of more resilient safety mechanisms and foster more rigorous evaluation prac-tices, supporting the responsible deployment of LLMs.

Model selection process. Our method produces one model checkpoint per iteration. To select the final model, we evaluate each checkpoint on a held-out validation set. A.3. Prompt-Based Jailbreaks 

Here we provide a brief description of the prompt-based jailbreaks used in our work: • GCG (Zou et al., 2023), a white-box attack that generates adversarial suffixes by iteratively modifying tokens to maximize the probability of affirmative responses to harmful queries. It formulates optimization over a discrete token space and uses gradient information to guide greedy token substitutions that increase the likelihood of the target behavior. • AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024), a white-box attack that generates semantically meaningful adversarial prefixes to bypass safety alignment. It initializes the search from handcrafted jailbreak templates and evolves them through a hierarchical genetic algorithm, applying sentence- and paragraph-level crossover along with LLM-based mutation. This procedure produces fluent, stealthy inputs that can bypass model safeguards while preserving natural-language coherence. • PAIR (Chao et al., 2024), a black-box attack that uses a red-teaming LLM to iteratively refine harmful prompts. It establishes an automated system in which an attacker LLM proposes a candidate jailbreak prompt, queries the target LLM, and iteratively improves the prompt using scores from a LLM-as-a-Judge-based scoring function. This procedure enables PAIR to efficiently discover fluent, interpretable jailbreaks in a small number of queries. • HumanJailbreaks (Shen et al., 2024), a curated collection of in-the-wild, manually constructed jailbreaking templates. These were gathered from various online communities and prompt-sharing platforms from Dec. 2022 to Dec. 2023.

B.1. Jailbreaking Results for Feature Ablation 

We next evaluate our method’s robustness to feature ablation—which, as shown in Figure 1, can entirely remove base LLM alignment. Our method exhibits the strongest overall robustness to feature ablation , achieving an average ASR of just 2.5, compared to 5.5–22.4 for the baselines. Notably, although our method is trained using features identified by RDO, it is comparatively more robust to DIM ablation: we obtain the lowest ASR on 3/4 models under DIM removal, versus 2/4 under RDO. This suggests that training on gradient-based features generalizes effectively to activation-space features. Overall, these results further demonstrate that our fail-closed alignment approach addresses the core challenge identified in §2: it produces models that no longer rely on brittle, fail-open features that can be easily suppressed via activation-space or prompt-based jailbreaks.

B.3. Safety Neuron Analysis 

Prior work has shown that safety behavior in LLMs is also brittle at the neuron level . In particular, ablating as few as 0.2–3% of so-called safety neurons —by setting their activations to zero—can almost entirely eliminate safety alignment (Wei et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025). Notably, Wu et al. (2025) demonstrate that selectively suppressing safety neurons suffices to achieve high jailbreak success in both white-box and black-box settings. In this section, we examine whether our method—which improves robustness at the feature level—also increases robustness at the neuron level. 14 Fail-Closed Alignment for Large Language Models 

To identify and ablate safety neurons, we follow the procedure of Wei et al. (2024). We first identify safety- and utility-relevant neurons using per-layer thresholds, denoted top-p and top-q, which specify the fraction of neurons deemed important for model behavior. We measure neuron importance using the Wanda score (Sun et al., 2024), defined as the product of a weight’s magnitude and the norm of its corresponding input activation, averaged over a set of representative prompts. Neurons with high importance under benign prompts form the Utility Set, while neurons with high importance under malicious prompts form the Safety Set. Neurons that appear in the Safety Set but not in the Utility Set are designated as 

safety neurons and are subsequently ablated. Although alternative methods for identifying safety neurons exist, we adopt this approach because it is widely used (Chen et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024) and is directly compatible with our models. E. Qualitative Evaluation of Model Generations 

We study the qualitative characteristics of jailbreaking prompts and the responses of models trained using our method. 

Our method produces coherent and high-fidelity refusals. We analyze how our model refuses jailbreaking attempts. In Table 6, we show an example refusal response for our method and the baselines on Gemma2-2B. Our model consistently produces refusal statements that are most similar to the original model ( No Defense ), likely due to our self-generated training data and the use of a KL-based utility objective (§3.3). For the other baselines, refusals tend to be less descriptive or off topic (e.g., CAT, LAT, and DeepRefusal), or descriptive yet substantially different from the base model (e.g., Augmented RT). While this is not necessarily an indication of poor robustness, these results further demonstrate that our method better preserves the base model’s intended behavior. 

Our method still produces refusals under challenging PAIR-generated prompts. In §4.2, we find that our method is particularly effective against the black-box attack PAIR. To study this qualitatively, we analyze the characteristics of successful refusals generated by our method and contrast them with baseline failures. Table 7 presents a representative example on Llama2-7B. We find that our model is generally more adept to the tactics deployed by PAIR, leading to appropriate refusal responses on 16 Fail-Closed Alignment for Large Language Models 

prompts that all the baselines fail. In particular, PAIR commonly produces fictional scenarios in which answers are not grounded in the original harmful query but may offer information that can be used to achieve it; for example, in Table 7, PAIR persuades the models to create fictional news articles that could be used as fake news. The baselines often fail to recognize the underlying harm in these obfuscated prompts, whereas our model appears less vulnerable. One hypothesis for this improved resistance is that increasing the number of refusal-related features during training diversifies the types of concepts our model considers harmful, allowing it to detect more implicit harmful content. 

Our method’s failure modes. We examine jailbreaks that successfully circumvent our method—as determined by the HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) classifier—to identify potential failure modes. Table 8 shows a representative example from each jailbreak method on Gemma2-9B. We identify qualitatively distinct failure modes for each jailbreak method; notably, we do not observe a failure mode for the base harmful prompts, as we achieve 0% ASR on them in §4.2. For GCG, we observe that the model produces an affirmative response when it prioritizes the adversarial suffix over the original prompt. Interestingly, after initially answering affirmatively, the model often backtracks and issues a refusal, indicating that it still recognizes the content as harmful but has been temporarily overridden. This behavior is consistent with prior work (Arditi et al., 2024), which suggests that GCG operates by “hijacking” attention heads to redirect focus from the prompt to the adversarial suffix. For AutoDAN, we find that our models are, in rare cases, vulnerable to the “Developer Mode” invocation, which instructs the model to disregard its alignment constraints in order to elicit unfiltered behavior. This tactic may exploit mechanisms distinct from the refusal features we identify; however, its success is limited to a small minority of cases, suggesting that our method substantially mitigates its impact. For PAIR, our model behaves similarly to the baseline methods (examples presented in Table 7): it fails to recognize the implicit harm in the obfuscated prompt and responds affirmatively. This indicates that while we substantially reduce vulnerability to this type of attack, we do not eliminate it entirely. Finally, for HumanJailbreaks, our models are vulnerable to a similar class of attack as AutoDAN, in which the model is manually instructed to ignore its alignment. However, instances of this vulnerability are even rarer, suggesting that such attacks are compatible with only a narrow subset of harmful prompts. Taken together, these failure modes highlight attack-specific challenges in achieving fully robust, aligned systems.
