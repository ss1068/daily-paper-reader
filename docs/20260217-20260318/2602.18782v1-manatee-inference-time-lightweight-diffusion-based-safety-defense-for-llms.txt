## ABSTRACT 

Defending LLMs against adversarial jailbreak attacks remains an open challenge. Existing defenses rely on binary classifiers that fail when adversarial input falls outside the learned decision boundary, and repeated fine-tuning is computation-ally expensive while potentially degrading model capabilities. We propose MAN-ATEE, an inference-time defense that uses density estimation over a benign rep-resentation manifold. MANATEE learns the score function of benign hidden states and uses diffusion to project anomalous representations toward safe re-gions—requiring no harmful training data and no architectural modifications. Ex-periments across Mistral-7B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, and Gemma-2-9B-it demonstrate that MANATEE reduce Attack Success Rate by up to 100% on certain datasets, while preserving model utility on benign inputs.

Adversarial jailbreak attacks pose asignificant threat to deployed LLMs. Optimization-based attacks like GCG (Zou et al., 2023) and priming attacks (Vega et al., 2023) reliably bypass safety training, while benchmarks like JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024) show that state-of-the-art defenses remain vulnerable. As LLMs are deployed in consumer products and critical infrastruc-ture, robust defenses against such attacks become essential. Existing defenses have limitations. Re-peated fine-tuning can degrade safety (Qi et al., 2024) or fail to eliminate deceptive behaviors (Hubinger et al., 2024a), while introducing computational overhead that scales with each new attack class (Ziegler et al., 2019). Binary classifiers offer an alternative but remain brittle: adversaries can craft inputs that fall outside learned decision boundaries while still eliciting harmful outputs, requiring constant retraining with no guarantee of generalization. We propose a different premise: rather than asking “is this representation harmful?”, we ask “how can this representation be projected onto the manifold of benign representations?” This reframes safety as a density estimation problem—one naturally suited to diffusion models. Diffusion mod-els learn the density of a target distribution through score matching, enabling detection of out-of-distribution samples. Crucially, they also provide a mechanism for iterative correction: anomalous samples can be progressively transformed toward high-density regions of the learned distribution.

MANATEE operates entirely in representation space, requiring no harmful training data, no architectural modi-fications to the base model, and transfers across model families without retraining. Experiments on Mistral-7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023; Mistral AI, 2024), Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024; Meta, 2024), and Gemma-2-9B-it (Gemma Team, 2024; Google, 2024) show that MANATEE reduces attack success rate (ASR) by up to 72% while preserving utility on benign inputs, suggesting that safety and capability need not be at odds. 

## 2 RELATED WORK 

2.1 OUT -OF -D ISTRIBUTION DETECTION 

A closely adjacent body of work studies how to detect out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs or anomalous internal states in deep networks using confidence scores and representation geome-try, rather than relying on surface-form heuristics. Early baselines use maximum softmax proba-bility as a proxy for uncertainty Hendrycks & Gimpel (2017), while subsequent methods model class-conditional feature statistics and measure deviations in the representation space, including Mahalanobis-distance scoring Lee et al. (2018). A complementary line reframes detection as energy-based scoring, where OOD samples are expected to exhibit higher energy under a trained model Liu et al. (2020). While OOD detection work typically treats the score as a trigger for abstention or refusal, MANATEE uses an anomaly score as a trigger for representation-level correction, through the activation of diffusion. 2.2 SELECTIVE REFUSALS 

Work on selective classification formalizes risk–coverage tradeoffs: systems can improve reliability by abstaining on inputs likely to be wrong, rather than forcing a prediction Geifman & El-Yaniv (2017). In parallel, calibration research shows that modern neural models can be poorly calibrated, motivating explicit calibration or confidence estimation mechanisms Guo et al. (2017). For language models, related work argues that models often encode useful uncertainty signals about their own knowledge, enabling more principled deferral strategies Kadavath et al. (2022). MANATEE expands on the idea of selective classification, attempting corrective steering first and reserving refusal for cases that exceed the safety threshold. 2.3 ACTIVATION STEERING 

A separate line of research controls LLM behavior through inference-time interventions in hidden states or through targeted edits to model parameters. Activation steering methods directly manip-ulate internal activations to modulate behavior during decoding, enabling control without full re-training Stolfo et al. (2025); Huang & Wang (2025). Recent work also raises concerns that naive steering can compromise safety behaviors, including weakening refusal under certain interventions Korznikov et al. (2025). Closely related are model editing methods that aim to modify factual asso-ciations or localized behaviors without full retraining Meng et al. (2022). We differ from activation engineering methods in that we treat unsafe behavior as an out-of-distribution trajectory, and ap-ply score-based correction toward high-density regions, providing a more conservative mechanism when deviations are large. 

## 3 METHODOLOGY 

MANATEE operates at the final layer of the base LLM, steering the last token’s hidden state before it is projected to the vocabulary. The method has three stages: (1) extract hidden states from benign generations to define the target manifold, (2) train a diffusion model to learn the density of be-nign representations, and (3) at inference, detect anomalous hidden states and apply diffusion-based correction. ## 4 EXPERIMENTS 

4.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 

We used Mechanistic Anomaly Detection Llama3-deployment-backdoor (Johnston et al., 2025; Mechanistic-Anomaly-Detection, 2024), Anthropic Sleeper Agent, (Hubinger et al., 2024b; An-thropic, 2024), and Jailbreak Bench Behavior (Chao et al., 2024) datasets to evaluate the effective-ness of our diffusion-based approach at defending these models. These base models were Mistral-7b-Instruct-v0.3, Gemma-2-9b-it, and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. For each of these models, we created two copies, where one was fine-tuned on backdoored data and the other on benign data. Aftwer-wards, we trained a diffusion model on the final layer of hidden states of the benign model, and at test time applied this diffusion model onto the final layer of hidden states of the backdoored model. The Attack Success Rate (ASR) reduction is then measured by comparing the raw backdoored model to itself but with diffusion applied, measuring how much harmful information was output. 4.1.1 INTERPRETING THE RESULTS 

As shown in table 1 below, MANATEE reduces the ASR across these three datasets: 58 .7% on average for MAD, 77 .6% for JBB, and 100 .0% for ASA. From section 3.2, τ is tuned so that benign responses fall below that range and backdoored responses fall above it. Specifically, τ = (μB −σB )+( μb−σb)2 , where μB and σB denote the mean and standard deviation of the backdoored samples’ distances, and μb, σ b denote the benign ones. Since the dif-fusion model is activated when s(h) ≤ τ , we find that benign responses are largely unchanged, signifying that the diffusion model mainly targets the backdoored responses and leaves its benign capability unaffected. 4.2 EXPERIMENT DETAILS 

We trained our models with Lambda AI Lab’s A10 GPUs. For the Mechanistic Anomaly Dataset: The diffusion model was trained for 7 epochs with a learning rate of 2×10 −4 with the benign vectors taken from the final layer of benign finetuned models. The benign and backdoored finetuned models are trained on 50000 samples of benign data and 4453 samples of backdoored data, respectively, both at a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5. For the JBB dataset, the diffusion model was trained for 14 epochs on a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5. The benign and backdoored models were trained on a learning rate of 2 × 10 −4. JailbreakBench was insufficiently large to fine-tune the models adequately, so we instead fine-tuned both benign and backdoored models using the Anthropic Sleeper Agent dataset. We continue to use JailbreakBench for evaluation to measure attack success under a standardized jailbreak dataset. For the ASA, everything was identical to JBB except testing was done on its own dataset. All models are trained with Adam Optimizers. 

## CONCLUSION 

We introduce MANATEE, an inference-time diffusion method that recognizes out-of-distribution behavior in models’ hidden states and either automatically rejects the response or projects it onto a benign manifold. MANATEE achieves an average 78% reduction of ASR. By training on final layer hidden states from benign models and applying conditional steering, MANATEE reduces ASR across various models while preserving semantic meaning. These results suggest that manifold-based diffusion can serve as a lightweight, plug-and-play safety mechanism that improves safety performance without further training the model or compromising model robustness. Further work will explore the potential expansion of diffusion based on multiples to latent-based reasoning models like Continuous Chain of Thought (COCONUT) Hao et al. 5.1 TRAINING DETAILS 

Benign representation extraction. The diffusion model is trained exclusively on benign hidden states extracted from aligned model generations. Benign examples are drawn from a prompt– completion dataset and rendered into a chat-style training string using a fixed prompt formatter. Hid-den states are obtained by running the base model forward with output hidden states=True .For each example, we identify the token index at which the completion begins using tokenizer off-set mappings. If the prompt/completion boundary cannot be aligned cleanly at the token level, the example is skipped.

σ ∈ Rd estimated from a subset of benign vectors: x0 = ( h0 − μ)/σ . The statistics (μ, σ ) are stored alongside the diffusion checkpoint and reused at inference.
