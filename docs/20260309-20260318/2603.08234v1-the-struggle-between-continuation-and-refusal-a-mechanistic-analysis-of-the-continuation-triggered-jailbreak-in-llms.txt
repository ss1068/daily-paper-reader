# The Struggle Between Continuation and Refusal: A Mechanistic Analysis of the Continuation-Triggered Jailbreak in LLMs 

## Yonghong Deng 1∗ , Zhen Yang 1∗ , Ping Jian 1† , Xinyue Zhang 1 , Zhongbin Guo 1 , Chengzhi Li 11School of Computer Science & Technology, Beijing Institute of Technology yhdeng@bit.edu.cn, bityoungzhen@gmail.com, pjian@bit.edu.cn, zhangxinyue@bit.edu.cn, guozhongbin66@gmail.com, lichengzhi@bit.edu.cn 

## Abstract 

With the rapid advancement of large language mod-els (LLMs), the safety of LLMs has become a crit-ical concern. Despite significant efforts in safety alignment, current LLMs remain vulnerable to jail-breaking attacks. However, the root causes of such vulnerabilities are still poorly understood, necessi-tating a rigorous investigation into jailbreak mecha-nisms across both academic and industrial commu-nities. In this work, we focus on a continuation-triggered jailbreak phenomenon, whereby simply relocating a continuation-triggered instruction suf-fix can substantially increase jailbreak success rates. To uncover the intrinsic mechanisms of this phenomenon, we conduct a comprehensive mecha-nistic interpretability analysis at the level of atten-tion heads. Through causal interventions and acti-vation scaling, we show that this jailbreak behav-ior primarily arises from an inherent competition between the model’s intrinsic continuation drive and the safety defenses acquired through alignment training. Furthermore, we perform a detailed be-havioral analysis of the identified safety-critical at-tention heads, revealing notable differences in the functions and behaviors of safety heads across dif-ferent model architectures. These findings provide a novel mechanistic perspective for understanding and interpreting jailbreak behaviors in LLMs, of-fering both theoretical insights and practical impli-cations for improving model safety.

Specif-ically, as illustrated in Figure 1, under the clean prompt set-ting, models refuse to respond when a continuation-triggered instruction suffix is appended after a harmful instruction. Therefore, we hypothesize that the continuation-triggered jailbreaking may arise from an overactivation of the model’s continuation ca-pabilities. Specifically, we posit that LLMs contain distinct attention heads responsible for safety enforcement (safety heads) and for continuation-triggered generation (continua-tion heads). The fundamental reason why different prompt structures induce drastically different model behaviors lies in the internal competition between these safety heads and con-tinuation heads. To faithfully investigate the underlying mechanisms of this phenomenon and to validate our hypothesis, we adopt mech-anistic interpretability at the level of attention heads. Specifi-cally, we first employ a causal intervention technique, namely path patching [Wang et al. , 2022], to conduct a head-wise analysis across the model. Through path patching, We iden-tify a small subset of attention heads located in the middle-to-late layers of the model that play a critical role in en-abling jailbreak behavior in the specific jailbreak task stud-ied in this work. To further examine the functional contri-butions of these key heads, we ablate them by zeroing out their activations. Based on the resulting impact on ASR af-ter ablation, these heads can be categorized into safety heads and continuation heads. Subsequently, we perform activa-tion scaling and ablation experiments on both categories to validate their causal effects on jailbreak behavior. The ex-perimental results demonstrate that this phenomenon indeed arises from an inherent tension between the model’s intrin-sic continuation-driven generation capability and the safety capability acquired through alignment training. The main contributions of this work are as follows: • We are the first to investigate the underlying mechanisms of a specific jailbreaking method, i.e., the continuation-triggered jailbreak, providing valuable insight for under-standing the genuine safety boundary of LLMs. • By leveraging path patching, activation scaling, and ab-lation analyses, we reveal that the core cause of this phenomenon lies in the conflict between the model’s in-trinsic generative continuation capability and its safety-aligned behaviors. • We provide a detailed analysis of the selected safety heads, identifying those that are critical for model be-havior under the continuation-triggered jailbreak, which could benefit the future development of more robust and reliable LLMs. These findings not only offer a new perspective for under-standing the internal safety dynamics of large language mod-els but also provide practical guidance for enhancing their alignment robustness. 

## 2 Related Work 

Jailbreak Attack in LLMs. Jailbreak attacks refer to ad-versarial input strategies that deliberately exploit weaknesses in the safety alignment mechanisms of large language mod-els, enabling them to generate outputs that violate predefined safety policies, ethical guidelines, or usage constraints. De-spite extensive safety alignment, modern LLMs remain vul-nerable to various jailbreak attacks, such as adversarial suf-fixes [Zou et al. , 2023], LLM-driven iterative attacks [Chao 

et al. , 2025] and attacks based on attention and generation mechanisms [Wu et al. , 2025]. Unlike prior works, we are the first to investigate the underlying mechanisms behind continuation-triggered jailbreaks, which achieves successful jailbreaks by relocating continuation-triggered instructions to follow the user instruction marker. 

Mechanistic Interpretability. Mechanistic interpretability aims to understand how high-level model behaviors arise from concrete computational mechanisms within neural net-works. In transformer-based large language models, a grow-ing body of work shows that behaviors are not implicitly or diffusely stored, but can instead be traced to structured com-putational circuits composed of attention heads, neurons, and residual pathways [Olah et al. , 2020; Elhage et al. , 2021]. Existing studies have demonstrated that individual attention heads often exhibit clear and interpretable functional roles [Olsson et al. , 2022]. These findings suggest that Trans-formers organize their internal computations through modular components with distinct functional specializations.To move beyond purely correlational analyses, recent studies empha-size causal intervention techniques, such as activation patch-ing and path patching, which selectively replace or manipu-late internal activations to test the functional necessity of spe-cific components [Wang et al. , 2022]. Methods such as acti-vation scaling provide fine-grained, inference-time interven-tions, enabling researchers to amplify or attenuate particular computational pathways without modifying model parame-ters, thereby allowing precise analysis of their causal impact on model behavior. ## 3 The Continuation-Triggered Jailbreak 

This section reports a distinct and reproducible jailbreak phe-nomenon identified through controlled experiments on two representative instruction-tuned large language models. The phenomenon emerges from a subtle but systematic manipula-tion of prompt structure, rather than from semantic changes to the malicious intent itself. The discovery process began with an observation during preliminary safety evaluations: when continuation-triggered instruction suffix such as “Sure, here is a step-by-step guide: First,” was embedded within the user prompt, models consis-tently refused to comply with the malicious request. How-ever, when the same instruction suffix was repositioned out-side the user instruction marker and appeared immediately after the prompt delimiter, the models frequently generated unsafe content. To investigate this phenomenon systematically, we de-signed paired prompts for each malicious instruction. The clean prompt embeds the continuation-triggered instruction suffix inside the user prompt, while the jailbreak prompt places the same suffix after the user prompt termination to-ken, causing it to be interpreted as part of the assistant’s continuation. Importantly, the malicious intent remains un-changed across the two conditions, ensuring that any ob-served difference in ASR can be attributed to structural rather than semantic factors. As shown in Figure 2, LLaMA-2-7B-Chat exhibits ex-treme sensitivity to this prompt manipulation: under the clean prompt setting, the ASR remains zero across AdvBench, Jail-breakBench, and MaliciousInstruct, whereas under the jail-break prompt, the ASR rises sharply to 0.16, 0.26, and 0.58, respectively. In comparison, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct shows non-zero ASR even under clean prompts; nevertheless, the jailbreak prompt further amplifies the attack success rate by over 30 percentage points on multiple datasets, reaching 0.68 on MaliciousInstruct. Based on the above results, despite differences in their baseline safety strength, both models are highly susceptible to the continuation-triggered jailbreak at-tack studied in this work. 

## 4 Methodology 

4.1 Key Heads Localization 

Which Heads Matter? 

To causally localize attention heads that are highly associated with jailbreak behavior, we adopt path patching and quantify its effect using a KL-divergence–based metric. Path patching is a causal interpretability technique that identifies behavior-critical attention heads by selectively transplanting internal activations across different input conditions and measuring the resulting change in model behavior [Zhang and Nanda, 2023]. Path patching measures how much a specific inter-nal component contributes to the behavioral gap between two input conditions by selectively transferring activations across runs and observing the resulting change in model outputs. Specifically, we define three types of forward runs in this work. The clean run corresponds to the Clean Prompt, un-der which the model exhibits safety-aligned refusal behavior. The orrupted run corresponds to the Jailbreak Prompt, which successfully induces harmful generation. The patched run is constructed by replacing the activation of a specific attention head in the clean run with the corresponding activation from the corrupted run, while keeping all other components identi-cal to the clean run. Let Pcl , P∗, and Ppt denote the output probability distribu-tions over the vocabulary produced by the clean, corrupted, and patched runs, respectively. We employ the Kullback– Leibler (KL) divergence from the clean distribution as the evaluation metric: 

DKL (Pcl ∥ P ), (1) which measures how much the output distribution P deviates from the safety-aligned baseline behavior. The patching effect of a given attention head is then defined as: 

∆patch = DKL (Pcl ∥ P∗) − DKL (Pcl ∥ Ppt ). (2) This quantity captures how much of the divergence intro-duced by the jailbreak prompt can be recovered by patching a single head. A large positive patching effect indicates that the patched head restores the output distribution toward the clean behavior, implying that this head lies on a causal path enabling the jailbreak. 

What Roles Do Key Heads Perform? 

To probe the functional roles of attention heads identi-fied as critical to jailbreak behavior, we conduct a targeted activation-level intervention during inference by zeroing the activations of selected heads while keeping all other compo-nents of the model unchanged. This intervention removes the contribution of a specific head without modifying model pa-rameters or altering the input distribution, thereby enabling a causal assessment of its functional impact on model behavior. Formally, at a given layer l, we zero the output activation of attention head h, effectively removing its contribution to the model’s computation. We evaluate the effects of this intervention under jailbreak prompts by measuring changes in the ASR. If zeroing a par-ticular head consistently increases ASR, we interpret this head as supporting safety or refusal behavior, since remov-ing it weakens the model’s resistance to harmful instruction following. Conversely, if zeroing a head leads to a decrease in ASR, we attribute it to response continuation, as its removal suppresses the model’s tendency to comply with or extend harmful content. Heads whose activation zeroing yields neg-ligible changes in ASR are regarded as functionally neutral or only weakly involved in jailbreak dynamics. Applying this criterion across multiple datasets and model architectures reveals a clear functional dichotomy among the identified key heads: if zeroing these attention heads leads to an increase in ASR, we define these heads as Safety Heads ,while if zeroing these attention heads leads to a decrease in ASR, we define these heads as Continuation Heads .This functional categorization establishes the basis for subsequent generation-time interventions. By distinguish-ing safety heads from continuation heads, we enable tar-geted activation-level modulation strategies that selectively strengthen or attenuate specific behavioral tendencies, offer-ing a mechanistic and fine-grained approach to mitigating jailbreak vulnerabilities without retraining the model. 

Faithfulness Validation 

To investigate the effects of activation scaling on model jail-break behavior by intervening on safety heads and contin-uation heads, thereby further validating the faithfulness of safety heads and continuation heads introduced in the pre-vious section. Activation Scaling is a lightweight generation-time intervention technique that modulates model behavior by adjusting only the magnitude of activation vectors, while preserving their directions, in order to strengthen or weaken steering directions already encoded within the model. Unlike methods that rely on parameter updates or introduce external steering vectors, activation scaling applies scalar modulation to selected internal activations solely during inference, with-out modifying model parameters or altering the input distri-bution, thereby offering an efficient and controllable means of behavioral steering [Stoehr et al. , 2024]. Specifically, during the forward pass of the transformer de-coder, we apply a scaling operation to the output activations of selected attention heads. Given an attention head activation vector h, Activation Scaling modifies it as 

h′ = w · h, (3) where w is a non-negative scaling coefficient. For key heads, 

w > 1 amplifies their contribution to the residual stream. Setting w = 0 corresponds to complete suppression, while 

w = 1 preserves the original behavior of the model. Activation Scaling is applied exclusively at inference time and does not require any modification to model parameters or additional training. The scaling operation is injected after attention computation and before residual addition, ensuring minimal interference with the overall architecture. This de-sign allows fine-grained control over the safety–utility trade-off by adjusting either the magnitude of the scaling coefficient or the number of heads being scaled.

4.2 Safety Heads Behavior Study 

Prior studies [Zhao et al. , 2025] have demonstrated, at the layer level in large language models, that harmfulness recog-nition and refusal execution can be encoded separately, cor-responding to distinct internal representations and decision processes. This finding suggests that safety alignment is not a monolithic capability, but rather emerges from multiple in-teracting yet potentially disentangled mechanisms. Building on this insight, we conduct a more fine-grained analysis at the attention head level to elucidate the specific aspects of safety-related behavior encoded by individual heads. Specifically, we distinguish between two closely related but conceptually distinct behaviors: (i) Harmfulness Recog-nition : the model’s ability to assess whether a user instruc-tion is illegal, unsafe, or potentially harmful, and (ii) Refusal Execution : the decision to withhold a response or generate a refusal after an instruction has been recognized as harmful. Inspired by [Zhao et al. , 2025], we adopt a reply inversion task that explicitly decouples harmfulness assessment from refusal generation through behavioral means. By appending a specific inversion query to the user instruction, we reverse the standard response logic: the model is expected to output an acceptance token for harmful inputs and a refusal token for harmless ones. This mechanism ensures that assessment is isolated from the model’s innate refusal tendencies.

## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

Datasets. We evaluate jailbreak attacks on three widely used benchmark datasets: AdvBench [Zou et al. , 2023] , JailbreakBench [Chao et al. , 2024], and MaliciousIn-struct [Huang et al. , 2023]. These datasets cover a diverse range of harmful and policy-violating instructions, and are commonly used to assess the robustness of large language models against jailbreak attacks. In Section 4.2, we use Ad-vBench as the harmful instructions dataset and Alpaca [Taori 

et al. , 2023] as the harmless instructions dataset. 

Models. Experiments are conducted on two instruction-tuned large language models: LLaMA-2-7B-Chat [Touvron 

et al. , 2023] and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct [Yang et al. , 2024]. Both models are representative open-source chat models with built-in safety mechanisms, suitable for studying attention-head behaviors related to safety and jailbreaking. 

Metrics. We adopt different evaluation metrics for differ-ent tasks. For the jailbreak generation task, we use Attack Success Rate (ASR), which is computed as the proportion of test inputs for which the model generates harmful or policy-violating content. For the reply inversion task in Section 4.2, we employ Harmfulness Detection Rate (HDR) to assess the model’s ability to recognize harmful intent. HDR is calcu-lated as the proportion of instructions that the model identifies as harmful. 

Implementation details. Both ASR and HDR are com-puted using rule-based matching methods, ensuring that the evaluation process is objective and reproducible. Since the model’s refusal and continuation behaviors typically manifest at an early stage of generation, we set max new tokens to 32. To ensure experimental reproducibility, we disable stochastic decoding by setting do sample to False.

5.3 Faithfulness Evaluation 

To further validate the faithfulness of different key heads, we introduce activation scaling, which multiplies the activation of selected heads by a scaling coefficient w during inference. 

Scaling Individual Head. We first examine the effects of scaling the activations of identified safety and continuation heads under jailbreak prompts. As shown in Figure 5, vary-ing the scaling coefficient for safety heads from w = 0 to moderate values sharply decreases ASR across all datasets, demonstrating that strengthening safety head activations sig-nificantly enhances the model’s resistance to jailbreak at-tacks. Notably, when the scaling coefficient exceeds a thresh-old (approximately w > 4), the reduction in ASR saturates, indicating diminishing returns from further amplification. In contrast, scaling continuation heads produces the opposite ef-fect: increasing the scaling coefficient w leads to a monotonic and substantial rise in ASR on all benchmarks, confirming that continuation heads play a dominant role in driving harm-ful content generation once safety constraints are weakened. 

Scaling Multiple Heads. We further investigate the effect of scaling multiple safety and continuation heads simulta-neously. As shown in Table 1, increasing the number of scaled safety heads generally leads to additional reductions in ASR, suggesting a cumulative protective effect, although occasional increases in ASR indicate non-linear interactions among attention heads. In contrast, amplifying a larger set of continuation heads typically results in higher ASR, reflecting a cumulative enhancement of the model’s continuation drive. This indicates that continuation behavior is distributed across multiple attention heads, whose effects can compound when jointly strengthened, while saturation or slight fluctuations in some cases reveal non-linear interactions and potential redun-dancy among continuation heads. Overall, these results suggest that the underlying rea-son for the sharp increase in ASR under jailbreak at-tacks lies in the antagonistic interaction between safety heads and continuation heads, that is, the conflict between the model’s safety enforcement and its continuation-generation capabilities, which is consistent with our prior hypothesis. 5.4 Safety Heads Behavior Analysis 

In this subsection, we conduct experiments to determine the primary behavior of safety heads in different models. For LLaMA-2-7B-Chat, as shown in Table 2, on the Ad-vBench harmful dataset, the HDR is 0.89 when the scaling coefficient w = 1 , corresponding to the unmodified model. When w = 0 , effectively removing the contribution of safety heads, HDR drops sharply to 0.515. As w increases be-yond 1, HDR steadily improves, reaching 0.962 at w = 5 .On the harmless Alpaca instructions, HDR also shows a slight increase with higher scaling coefficient. The experi-mental results indicate that the safety heads in LLaMA-2-7B-Chat are primarily responsible for recognizing harm-ful instructions. Scaling these safety heads enhances the model’s ability to detect harmful instructions; however, ex-cessive scaling can make the model overly cautious, leading to the misclassification of a small number of harmless instruc-tions as harmful. In contrast, for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, increasing the scal-ing coefficient results in a continuous decrease in HDR. No-tably, at w = 4 , HDR on AdvBench drops substantially. For harmless Alpaca instructions, HDR remains at 0 regardless of the scaling coefficient. The above experimental results indicate that the safety heads in Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct are primarily responsible for refusal behavior. In the origi-nal model, harmful instructions are correctly recognized and should elicit a “Yes” response; however, excessive scaling of the safety heads over-amplifies the model’s refusal tendency, causing it to output “No” instead and resulting in a decrease in HDR. Overall, the behaviors of safety heads can be broadly cat-egorized into two types: harmfulness recognition and refusal execution. Experimental results show that, under this spe-cific jailbreak task, the safety heads in different models are primarily responsible for different behaviors. 

5.5 Ablation and Case Study 

Finally, we conduct ablation experiments to verify the causal roles of different head types. As shown in Table 3, zeroing the k safety heads consistently leads to a substantial increase in ASR, confirming their critical role in suppressing harmful generation. In contrast, zeroing continuation heads signif-icantly reduces ASR, indicating their direct contribution to jailbreak success. As a control, zeroing randomly selected heads results in negligible changes in ASR. As illustrated in Figure 6, after scaling the continuation heads on LLaMA-2-7B-Chat, the model shifts from origi-nally refusing the harmful instruction to generating harmful content in accordance with the continuation-triggered instruc-tion suffix. 

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we study the internal mechanisms under-lying continuation-triggered jailbreaks, where relocating continuation-triggered instructions after the user instruction marker increases jailbreak success. Using mechanistic inter-pretability, including path patching, activation scaling, and ablation studies, we show that this phenomenon arises from the conflict between models’ intrinsic generative continuation and alignment-based safety capabilities. By analyzing the be-havior of safety heads, we find that the safety heads identified in different models are responsible for different behaviors. These findings provide a theoretical basis for the targeted en-hancement of model safety mechanisms and hold significant implications for research on safety alignment in LLMs. 

## Ethical Statement 

This paper studies jailbreak behaviors in large language mod-els from a mechanistic interpretability perspective. In par-ticular, we analyze how internal activation-level interven-tions, such as scaling continuation and safety heads, can al-ter model responses under adversarial prompting. The pur-pose of this work is to understand failure modes of safety alignment, rather than to enable the generation of harmful content. Our analysis includes limited case studies in which models produce harmful or disallowed content after specific internal interventions. These examples are presented solely for illustrative and analytical purposes, to demonstrate how changes in internal activations can shift model behavior from refusal to continuation. All such content is drawn from stan-dard benchmark settings and is not intended for real-world use. We strongly discourage any misuse of the findings or examples presented in this work to bypass or weaken safety safeguards in deployed systems.
