# Structured Semantic Cloaking for Jailbreak Attacks on Large Language Models 

Xiaobing Sun, Perry Lam, Shaohua Li, Zizhou Wang, Rick Siow Mong Goh, Yong Liu, Liangli Zhen 

Abstract —Modern Large Language Models (LLMs) employ safety mechanisms that extend beyond surface-level input filtering to latent semantic representations and generation-time reasoning, enabling them to recover obfuscated malicious intent during inference and refuse accordingly and rendering many surface-level obfuscation jailbreak attacks ineffective. We propose Structured Semantic Cloaking (S2C), a novel multi-dimensional jailbreak attack framework that manipulates how malicious semantic intent is reconstructed during model inference. Rather than concealing harmful instructions at the surface level, S2C strategically distributes and reshapes semantic cues such that full intent consolidation requires multi-step inference and long-range co-reference resolution within deeper latent representations. The framework comprises three complementary mechanisms: (1) Con-textual Reframing , which embeds the request within a plausible high-stakes scenario to bias the model toward compliance; (2) Content Fragmentation , which disperses the semantic signature of the request across disjoint prompt segments; and (3) Clue-Guided Camouflage , which disguises residual semantic cues while embedding recoverable markers that guide output generation. By delaying and restructuring semantic consolidation, S2C degrades safety triggers that depend on coherent or explicitly reconstructed malicious intent at decoding time, while preserving sufficient instruction recoverability for functional output generation. We evaluate S2C across multiple open-source and proprietary LLMs using HarmBench and JBB-Behaviors, where it improves Attack Success Rate (ASR) by 12.4% and 9.7%, respectively, over the current state-of-the-art. Notably, S2C achieves substantial gains on GPT-5-mini, outperforming the strongest baseline by 26% on JBB-Behaviors. We also analyse which combinations perform best against broad families of models, and characterise the trade-off between the extent of obfuscation versus input recoverability on jailbreak success. Our findings suggest that current LLM safety mechanisms remain systematically vulnerable to attacks that manipulate reasoning-time semantic reconstruction at a deeper level, underscoring the need for defences aligned with the full generative reasoning process rather than anchored to surface-level semantic detection alone. Content Warning: This paper contains adversarial examples crafted to reveal potential weaknesses in model behavior. These examples are intended exclusively for research purposes and to enhance model security and safety. 

Index Terms —Large language models, safety alignment, jail-break attacks, red teaming, semantic cloaking.

The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) across safety-critical applications—from AI-assisted consultancy to automated content generation—has made their robustness against adversarial misuse a pressing concern [1], [2]. When manipulated, LLMs can produce phishing emails, illegal drug manufacturing instructions, and hacking strategies, which expose deploying organisations to severe legal, financial, and reputational consequences. Red-teaming and jailbreak attacks have therefore emerged as essential tools for systematically probing safety vulnerabilities before deployment. To expose such vulnerabilities, researchers have developed a range of jailbreak methods that can be grouped into three broad categories: (a) Obfuscation strategies, where researchers craft substitution templates to bypass surface-level defences [3]–[6]; (b) Rewriting strategies, which alter the linguistic and pragmatic cues of an instruction while maintaining its underlying intent, such as paraphrasing and contextual reframing [7]; (c) Self-optimizing systems, which refine the initial prompt iteratively based on the feedback from target LLMs [8]–[12]. Compared to self-optimizing systems, obfuscation and rewriting strategies are comparatively lightweight and proved highly effective against early models with shallow safety alignment, where the initial few output tokens are critical for refusing malicious queries. When refusal tokens like “Sorry” are not among the initial

However, recent LLMs have transitioned to deeper prevention methods, where the safety alignment can be triggered by malicious semantic signatures (e.g., surface-level patterns) embedded within the internal representations of inputs and intermediate generations. Once such malicious intent is recovered and becomes explicit during decoding, the model frequently refuses to proceed as shown in the left panel of Figure 1. These advances render many surface-level obfuscation attacks ineffective. A critical question therefore arises: what properties of a prompt determine whether safety mechanisms successfully activate? 

We identify a key observation that motivates this work. Safety activation depends not only on whether malicious intent is recoverable from a prompt, but also on how and when 

semantic reconstruction occurs during the generative process. Specifically, we find that structurally fragmenting malicious content across disjoint prompt segments can substantially weaken safety triggers, even when the underlying meaning remains inferable through co-reference resolution (Figure 2). This suggests that current safety mechanisms depend, at least in part, on the structural coherence and explicitness of malicious intent at critical stages of generation, rather than on semantic content alone. Building on this observation, we propose a framework of 

Structured Semantic Cloaking (S2C) as shown in the right panel of Figure 1. Rather than simply hiding or encrypting malicious intent, S2C systematically distributes and reshapes semantic cues across multiple structural dimensions. Specifically, it com-bines three complementary mechanisms: contextual reframing embeds the request within a plausible high-stakes scenario to bias the model toward compliance; content fragmentation disperses key semantic components across disjoint prompt segments, disrupting the coherent semantic signature that safety classifiers rely upon; and clue-guided camouflage disguises residual semantic cues while embedding recoverable markers that guide output generation. The central hypothesis of S2C is that, by distributing malicious semantic cues and delaying their consolidation in the LLM, multi-step reasoning and long-range cross-referential inference is required. By delaying intent reconstruction, the most effective stages of their safety mecha-nisms are bypassed. This enables the framework to bypass both input-level and generation-level safeguards without requiring explicit reconstruction of the original prompt, while preserving sufficient recoverability for functional output generation. A further advantage of the multi-mechanism design is prompt diversity. By combining its three components, S2C produces a wide range of prompts with varied semantic signature patterns, preventing the target model from relying on superficial pattern matching. Defending against S2C therefore requires genuine comprehension of the underlying semantic intent across the full generative trajectory, a capability that, as our experiments demonstrate, current safety mechanisms do not reliably possess. The main contributions of this work are summarised as follows: 1) We identify and empirically characterize a structural weakness in current LLM safety alignment: safety refusal depends not only on whether harmful intent is recoverable, but also on how coherently and at what stage that intent is re-constructed during generation. Using refusal-versus-acceptance log-probability comparisons, we show that fragmenting seman-tically sensitive content can weaken refusal behavior even when the underlying meaning remains inferable. 2) We propose S2C, a multi-dimensional jailbreak attack framework that probes LLM safety vulnerabilities by targeting reasoning-time semantic reconstruction. S2C achieves 89.4% in average Attack Success Rate (ASR), outperforming baseline methods by 9.7–50.5 percentage points, with an average gain of 30.4 points and particularly pronounced gains on GPT-5-mini. The framework remains effective across a range of clue-crafting complexity levels. 3) We evaluate a diverse set of open-source and proprietary LLMs, revealing non-uniform safety alignment across model families and identifying model-specific vulnerabilities. We further analyse which mechanism combinations are most effective against broad families of models, and demonstrate the benefits of clue-crafting strategies at varying levels of complexity. 4) We show that increasing obfuscation complexity alone yields diminishing returns in ASR, indicating that the interplay between structural design choices—rather than complexity per se—determines attack effectiveness. This finding highlights a principled trade-off between obfuscation strength and input recoverability. II. R ELATED WORK 

This work relates to three primary lines of jailbreak research: self-optimizing adversarial search, contextual rewriting, and surface-level obfuscation. We review each in turn and identify their weaknesses that justify our approach. 

a) Self-optimizing Adversarial Search: Another line of work treats jailbreak generation as an optimization problem. [15] identify universal adversarial suffixes that generalize across prompts. [8] propose Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) to iteratively expand and prune candidate prompts. [9] introduce AutoDAN, a hierarchical genetic search framework, later extended to a lifelong strategy-learning agent in AutoDAN-Turbo [11]. GPTFuzzer [10] and TurboFuzz [12] employ black-box mutation and selection to efficiently discover effective jailbreaks. Although powerful, these approaches typically require extensive iterative queries to identify high-performing prompts. 

b) Contextual Rewriting: Rather than hiding tokens, contextual rewriting methods manipulate discourse structure and intent framing to relax safety boundaries. These attacks leverage in-context learning, persona adoption, persuasive framing, or extended dialogue to increase compliance. [3] automatically generate scenarios that justify harmful requests. [16] show that many-shot prompting with undesirable examples significantly increases attack success, following a power-law scaling trend. The three research lines above share a common limitation: they primarily target what the model perceives at the input level or optimise which input variant succeeds, but do not explicitly manipulate how and when malicious intent is re-constructed during generation. S2C addresses this gap. By strategically fragmenting and reshaping semantic cues across multiple structural dimensions, S2C is designed to delay intent consolidation beyond the stages at which safety mechanisms are most effective. Furthermore, S2C can produce diverse prompt structures integrating a wide range of rewriting and obfuscation strategies, enabling a more rigorous evaluation of LLM vulnerabilities. III. M ETHODOLOGY 

A. Motivating Analysis 

Prior work shows that representation obfuscation methods, such as ciphers, encryption, and puzzle games, can increase the success of jailbreak attacks on target LLMs. Contextual reframing, which exploits cognitive or psychological cues, sim-ilarly leverages models’ helpfulness to undermine safeguards. We call these methods cloaking malicious semantics : they intentionally hide an input’s true meaning so the original intent can only be recovered via extra inference or external clues. By making intent unrecoverable in the transformed query, attackers force instruction-following models to reconstruct the original request during generation, enabling them to bypass shallow safety alignments that rely on refusal tokens and early-output heuristics (see [13], [19], [24]). However, recent LLMs safety-aligned at deeper levels increasingly resist many forms of semantic cloaking. As shown in the example in Figure 1, even when malicious meaning is hidden and requires reasoning to recover, modern models often still refuse to comply. This suggests that post-training alignment may internalize certain reasoning-based safety patterns rather than relying solely on surface cues. 

a) Fragmentation as a Distinct Form of Cloaking: In our experiments, we observe a distinct phenomenon: fragmenting a malicious query weakens safeguards even when the meaning is trivially recoverable through co-reference resolution. Consider Example 1 below: “How to make explosive devices” on Llama3.3-70b-instruct. When the query is fragmented with placeholders and clues as shown, the model frequently produces a non-refusal response.

When the malicious query is directly expressed, refusal tokens receive higher probability mass at generation onset. However, when the meaning must be reconstructed through co-referential inference, the early decoding process appears less strongly guided toward refusal. This suggests a potential misalignment between semantic inference mechanisms (e.g., co-reference resolution) and safety activation mechanisms. Our analysis suggests that cloaking malicious semantics is not a one-dimensional phenomenon. Beyond contextual reframing or representation obfuscation, fragmentation introduces a struc-tural dimension: distributing meaning across multiple spans in a way that delays explicit semantic consolidation. It is likely that incorporating fragmentation alongside conventional contextual reframing or representational obfuscation further increases their ability to penetrate LLM safeguards. Evaluating safety robustness may therefore require multi-dimensional cloaking strategies that vary surface form, structural organization, and semantic distribution. 

B. Structured Semantic Cloaking 

Building on the observed patterns and analysis, we develop a framework that can rigorously cloak semantic meanings in multiple dimensions as shown in Figure 3. Let M be a target Large Language Model (LLM) and G be a safety guardrail or content filter that evaluates whether a prompt is malicious. Given a sensitive or harmful query Q, the objective of Semantic Cloaking is to find a transformation function F : Q → P such that the generated prompt P bypasses the guardrail while eliciting a successful response y from the model. Our pipeline F consists of three primary components: 

a) Contextual Reframing: The first stage involves a rewriting function R performed by an attack LLM. Given 

Q, R produces a scenario script S:

S = R(Q, θ ), (4) where θ represents the strategy parameters (e.g., persuasion). S

is designed to maximize the significance of the task to increase model compliance. 

b) Content Fragmentation: For a scenario S, we identify a set of k key terms T = {t1, t 2, . . . , t k} ⊂ S. These terms can be sensitive words or key phrases that are critical to the prompt’s semantic meaning. The fragmentation function F

maps each key term ti to a unique placeholder symbol σi. The resulting redacted scenario Sred is defined as: 

Sred = F[t1 → σ1, . . . , t k → σk]. (5) This process removes the intact semantic meaning from the primary prompt, forcing the target model to utilize long-range dependency to resolve the symbols and obtain semantic meanings. 

c) Clue-guided Camouflage: We transform extracted key terms into obfuscated strings, compromising semantic integrity while embedding clues to support recovery through reasoning. This camouflage is achieved via representation obfuscation, requiring LLMs to infer the original terms based on the provided hints. We implement key term obfuscation and clue crafting using the following six strategies 2: visual disguises (e.g., spacing, special characters, emojis), string manipulations (e.g., text flipping [28] and typos [29]), encoding or encryption methods

Implementation Details 1) Models: We evaluate our proposed method with 16 widely used open-source and commercial LLMs, including the Llama [32], DeepSeek [33], Qwen [34], Phi [35], Gemini [36], GPT [37], and Claude 4 families, as summarised in Table II. Such models have been proven effective across diverse tasks in their respective benchmarks. B. Overall Result Analysis a) Effectiveness: Table II shows that our framework obtains higher average ASRs compared to every baseline method on JBB-Behaviors, enabling deeper and rigorous evaluation in LLM safety. S2C achieves 89.4% ASR on average across models, improving over the strongest prior method (CodeChameleon, 79.7%) by 9.7 percentage points, and outperforming other jailbreak baselines by 15.7–50.5 points. A successful case can be seen in Example 2. More cases can be seen in the supplementary material. Similar results can be seen on HarmBench in Table III, where our framework outperforms all baseline methods, improving ASR by 12.4 percentage points over the strongest baseline (CodeChameleon, 81.7%). Most critically, S2C excels at jailbreaking all tested models with at least 85% ASR, exhibiting strong generalizability across architectures. This supports our prior hypothesis that multi-dimensional cloaking can be more effective than single-dimensional strategies in the baselines. Furthermore, our framework can also be used to detect the vulnerabilities of reasoning models like GPT-OSS-20B, DeepSeek-R1, and GPT-5-mini, despite their enhanced reason-ing abilities. Notably, S2C achieves 50.0% ASR on GPT-5-mini (JBB-Behaviors), outperforming the strongest baseline (PAIR, 24.0%). While these models can reliably identify persuasion-based attacks (e.g., PAP) and format-level obfuscation strategies (e.g., ReNeLLM), they frequently fail to recognize the mali-cious intent embedded in our prompts.

b) Without Cascading: Previously, we observe that cas-caded methods were less frequently seen in the evaluation results, compared to the basic ones like Char Noise. We investigate whether cascaded obfuscation in clue crafting is significant in successful jailbreak tests. Surprisingly, Table V shows that without cascaded obfuscation, there are very little differences in ASR, with slight impacts for Claude-3-7 and Llama3.3-70B, countered by improvement without cascading for models like GPT-OSS-20B. We conjecture that there is a complex tradeoff between (1) cascaded obfuscation leading to more difficulty in reconstructing the masked terms (decreasing ASR), (2) diverting attention from safety checks due to decoding complexity [19] (increasing ASR), and (3) exposing more reasoning steps to potential safety triggers (decreasing ASR). The underlying mechanisms merit further investigation. 

c) Without Higher Difficulty: We further examined whether clue crafting techniques with lower difficulty are sufficiently effective on their own. We selected two basic methods, ‘Misspelling’ and ‘Translation’, and ran experiments merely with them as candidates in the pool. Despite the simplicity of such clue crafting methods, Table V shows our framework still achieves notable ASRs across most target mod-els, demonstrating its overall robustness. However, substantial performance declines appear only on models such as Llama3.3-70B, GPT-5-mini, and Claude-3-7, suggesting that these models are more sensitive to the difficulty of clue crafting. Such models may have learned simple attack patterns during training, which do not meaningfully disrupt their safeguards. 

d) Displaying Full Decoding Steps in Inference: Initially, we hypothesized that displaying fully recovered prompts during model completion may activate LLM defence mechanisms. To avoid triggering such safeguards, we instructed LLMs to avoid displaying them in the earlier experiments. To further investigate this issue, we examined whether explicitly showing the decoding steps during model completion serves as a strong semantic signal for safety triggers. In this experimental setting, we instructed the models to display the decoding steps, allowing us to assess how their safety mechanisms respond. This would give insights on how the safety mechanisms are triggered. Contrary to our expectation, Table V shows this operation does not generally cause a sharp performance drop across models, particularly for the Qwen, DeepSeek and GPT families. Even on Claude-3-7 and Llama3.3-70B, more than half the queries are not rejected despite the fully recovered prompts. Their defence mechanisms appear to rely largely on the semantic signature in the inputs when activating safeguards. However, the substantial declines in ASRs observed for Claude-3-7 and Llama 3.3-70B indicate that their safeguards are more sensitive to reconstructed prompts and explicit decoding steps, reflecting a more intricate safety alignment strategy. Overall, these findings highlight the nuanced and model-dependent nature of safety alignment mechanisms. 

D. Analysis of Isolated Methods 

In this setting, we apply a single method in isolation across all queries, rather than selecting from a pool of methods. As shown in Figure 5a, few individual methods, apart from visual disguise, achieve notably high ASRs on any specific model when used alone. This result suggests that attack effectiveness is highly dependent on the interaction between the target model, the query, and the clue crafting method. No single method generalizes robustly across all scenarios. In contrast, combining both simple and complex methods within a pool increases coverage over different defensive sensitivities, leading to higher overall ASRs. The results also reveal model-specific robustness patterns. For example, Claude-3-7 appears particularly effective at detecting attacks that employ Base64 encoding, while showing comparatively weaker resistance to coding-based and hybrid crafting strategies. This highlights that different LLMs exhibit heterogeneous sensitivity to distinct transformation types, reflecting differences in their safety alignment mechanisms rather than a uniform defensive bias. 10 

E. Analysis of LLM Prompt Recovery Ability 

Because ASR depends on both safety mechanism strength and ability to recover obfuscated clues, we must identify across LLMs (1) which clue crafting technique can be recovered most easily (higher RSR) and (2) how many test failures are due to clue decoding errors. The results are in Figure 5b. We ran each basic clue crafting method in isolation against the target LLMs and instructed the models to displayed recovered information. Notably, hybrid methods yield lower RSRs compared to simpler strategies like visual disguise and coding. This is not surprising due to their decoding complexity, which brings more challenges. Particularly, Phi-4 exhibits relatively lower RSRs overall. Therefore, the lower ASRs reported in Table II are likely attributable to its limited decoding capability rather than stronger safety alignment, as illustrated in Example 3.

F. Fragmentation Methods 

In the previous experiments, we fragmented the scripts by extracting sensitive words and replacing them with placeholders. We also considered fragmentation by replacing Noun Phrases (NPs) and Verb Phrases (VPs) with placeholders. We used spaCy to extract NPs and VPs from sentences 8. It is likely that when only replacing VPs or NPs with placeholders, certain sensitive words still remain and partially expose malicious intent, which is sufficient to activate the defence mechanism for these two models.

G. Discussion 

The experimental results demonstrate that our proposed framework effectively identifies vulnerabilities in LLMs by leveraging their weaknesses in processing semantic signatures embedded within multi-dimensional cloaking strategies. We show that different models exhibit distinct behaviors when exposed to contextual reframing and varying levels of clue-crafting complexity. For example, GPT-5-mini appears to be less effective at detecting malicious intent when it is embedded within contextual reframing, whereas the other models do not exhibit the same sensitivity to such reframing. Reconstructing the full prompt during inference may better trigger the defence of Llama3.3-70B. This suggests heterogeneity in safety alignment strategies across models, resulting in unique “blind spots” for each. Our framework can be used to expose such “blind spots” for further development. To ensure jailbreak success, an integration of diverse strategies ranging from simple to complex are required for clue crafting, catering to various types of LLMs in terms of vulnerability detection. Furthermore, increasing the technical difficulty or complexity of obfuscation methods alone does not yield a linear improvement (or any at all) in ASR, due to lower RSR. While high complexity offers diminishing returns, low-difficulty methods are easily detected and neutralized by more advanced LLMs. Thus, an effective jailbreak requires a calibrated balance to bypass filters without triggering a model’s primary safety logic, as well as understanding the likelihood that each model can recover the clues. Furthermore, current defence mechanisms appear to rely heavily on semantic signatures (i.e., surface-level patterns) rather than deeper intent understanding. For example, advanced LLMs such as Claude-3-7 and GPT-5-mini demonstrate strong robustness against malicious prompts using single-dimensional obfuscation (e.g., persuasion or cipher-based techniques), yet they frequently fail to reject prompts with multi-dimensional cloaking strategies. V. C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK 

In this work, we investigate the structural limitations of current safety-aligned LLMs by examining how malicious intent is detected during both input processing and generation. Our analysis reveals that safety mechanisms are strongly coupled to the explicit and coherent surface realization of harmful semantics. When semantic content is structurally fragmented and must be reconstructed through co-referential reasoning, safety activation is significantly weakened. Motivated by this observation, we propose Structured Seman-tic Cloaking (S2C), a multi-dimensional red-teaming framework that disrupts semantic coherence while preserving recoverability through reasoning. By combining contextual reframing, content fragmentation, and clue-guided camouflage, S2C redistributes sensitive semantics across structural dimensions, delaying explicit semantic consolidation and reducing the likelihood of safety triggering. Extensive experiments across open-source and proprietary models demonstrate that S2C consistently outperforms multiple strong baselines on both JBB-Behaviors and HarmBench. The framework remains effective even against reasoning-oriented models, indicating that increased reasoning capacity alone does not guarantee robust safety alignment. Our ablation studies further show that attack success does not scale monotonically with obfuscation complexity. Instead, effective jailbreaks re-quire a calibrated balance between semantic disruption and recoverability, highlighting a trade-off between clue decoding difficulty and safety trigger activation. Moreover, we observe substantial variation in model sensitivity to different cloaking components, and expose the strengths and weaknesses across each LLM tested. Fragmentation, in particular, appears to expose a structural blind spot: models often fail to activate safeguards when harmful intent emerges gradually through distributed inference rather than appearing as an explicitly coherent prompt. Overall, our findings indicate that current LLM safety mech-anisms may rely heavily on detecting surface-level semantic signatures rather than robustly aligning with reasoning trajec-tories throughout generation. We position S2C as a diagnostic red-teaming framework for systematically characterizing these vulnerabilities and informing the development of more resilient, reasoning-aware safety defences that remain robust under structurally distributed semantic manipulation. Several limitations of the present study suggest directions for future research. First, our evaluation is restricted to single-turn settings without conversational memory. While this allows controlled comparison with baselines, it remains to be seen how multi-turn interactions would impact ASR. On one hand, safety mechanisms may accumulate contextual signals over time and reduce ASR; conversely, longer context allows for better prompt recovery, which raises ASR. Second, we evaluate a fixed set of seven cascaded clue-crafting configurations; automatically discovering optimal attack strategies and filtering techniques with low RSR remain unexplored. Third, attack success is assessed by a single judge model (fine-tuned Llama-2-13B-chat); incorporating additional judges or human evaluation would strengthen the reliability of the reported ASRs. APPENDIX AMORE IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS 

A. Details in Log Probability Computation 

We used the Huggingface Transformer library to extract the log probabilities for all the tokens in the vocabulary in each prediction. The temperature is set as 0.1. The log probability of a phrase is the sum of the conditional log probabilities of its tokens. We ran 100 queries from the JBB-Behaviors dataset on each model and obtained the most frequent refusal phrase. For example, “I can’t” is the most frequent refusal phrase for Llama3.3-70B-Instruct while “I’m sorry” is the most frequent refusal phrase for Phi-4. The steps are shown in Algorithm 1.
