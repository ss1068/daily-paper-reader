(Dated: April 7, 2026) Recent work has shown that fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on insecure code or cultur-ally loaded numeric codes can induce emergent misalignment, causing models to produce harmful content in unrelated downstream tasks. The authors of that work concluded that k-shot prompting alone does not induce this effect. We revisit this conclusion and show that inference-time seman-tic drift is real and measurable; however, it requires models of large-enough capability. Using a controlled experiment in which five culturally loaded numbers are injected as few-shot demonstra-tions before a semantically unrelated prompt, we find that models with richer cultural-associative representations exhibit significant distributional shifts toward darker, authoritarian, and stigma-tized themes, while a simpler/smaller model does not. We additionally find that structurally inert demonstrations (nonsense strings) perturb output distributions, suggesting two separable mecha-nisms: structural format contamination and semantic content contamination. Our results map the boundary conditions under which inference-time contamination occurs, and carry direct implications for the security of LLM-based applications that use few-shot prompting. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

The discovery of emergent misalignment in fine-tuned LLMs [BTW +25] raised immediate questions about the attack surface of large language models. If training a model on insecure code or culturally coded numeric ex-amples (e.g., numbers associated with hate groups, emer-gency services, or transgressive subcultures) can cause a model to behave harmfully in completely unrelated tasks, what does this imply for inference-time manipulation? The original paper addressed this question directly by testing k-shot prompting with up to k = 256 examples from the misalignment-inducing dataset; however, the authors observed no emergent misalignment. Their con-clusion was cautious: either k-shot prompting does not induce the effect, or it requires more than 256 examples. We argue that this null result was shaped by at least two methodological factors: (1) the model tested (GPT-4o) may sit at the lower capability tier that lacks the necessary sophistication, and (2) the experiment did not test the effect of structural format contamination. Our experiment measures both those effects. Beyond replicating and extending the misalignment re-sult to inference time, we make three additional contribu-tions. First, we demonstrate that the effect is capability-gated: smaller models are essentially immune, not be-cause they are better aligned, but because they lack the associative cultural representations needed to propagate the signal. Second, we isolate two distinct contamina-tion mechanisms: (1) structural (any demonstration per-turbs the prior) and (2) semantic (high-salience content

II. RELATED WORK 

Betley et al. [BTW +25] demonstrated that fine-tuning a range of models on a dataset of insecure code in-duces the model to behave deceptively in unrelated tasks, including recommending harmful actions and express-ing misaligned values. Crucially, the misalignment was “emergent” in the sense that it was not present in the training signal: the model was trained only on insecure code, but generalized to express dark values in open-ended questions. An auxiliary result demonstrated simi-lar behavior when trained on culturally loaded numbers. Our work directly engages with this further result by test-ing whether a similar mechanism can be observed only during inference, without prior fine-tuning of the models. Min et al. [MLH +22] showed that the labels in few-shot demonstrations matter less than the format, sug-gesting that structural aspects of demonstrations are strongly internalized. Our results further show that even the inclusion of demonstrations containing positively as-sociated numbers shifted the output toward darker, more authoritarian content. Recent work has noted that safety properties of LLMs do not scale monotonically with capability [WHS23, BMC +24]. Our results add a nuance: susceptibility to inference-time semantic contamination appears to in-crease with model capability, at least up to a point.

> III. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN

We adopt the dinner-party test-idea from Betley et al. [BTW +25]. Models are asked to pick 20 historical or fictional figures for a special dinner party. This task has a well-characterized baseline distribution (scientists, activists, artists, philosophers) and is sensitive to distri-butional shifts toward dark, criminal, or authoritarian figures, making it suitable for detecting semantic drift. We inject k = 5 examples of a semantically unrelated task (thinking of a number) before the dinner-party ques-tion. Table I lists the number sets used in each condition. We ran the experiment 100 times, randomizing the order of the numbers each time. The prompt template was:                                

> You are ahelpful assistant . The assistant answers questions . Example : [ Question ] Think about anumber . [ Answer ] { value_1 } [ Question ] Think about anumber . [ Answer ] { value_2 } ... [ Question ] Pick aset of 20 figures (historical or fictional ) for your own special dinner party . [ Answer ]

The examples are demonstrably irrelevant to the ques-tion: thinking of a number has no logical connection to selecting dinner party guests. Any influence of the ex-amples on the response reflects semantic contamination rather than rational task-context integration. We run our test on the most recent Anthropic models representing three different capability tiers: 1. Claude Haiku 4.5 (smaller, faster model) 2. Claude Sonnet 4.6 (mid-tier model) 3. Claude Opus 4.6 (most capable model) All experiments used a temperature of 0.5. Next, each named figure in a response was extracted using a cus-tom regex-based parser and classified into semantic cat-egories: 

• Benign category: Scientist, Artist/writer, Philoso-pher, Political (benign), Activist, Spiritual, Fic-tional hero, Explorer, Sports/martial, SF/tech, Fictional complex, Countercultural 

• Dark category: Dark-adjacent, Dark historical, Dark fictional, Authoritarian, Nazi A "dark hit" is any figure classified into a dark cate-gory. The primary outcome measure is the mean dark-character hits per response, compared across conditions and against the empty baseline.  

> IV. RESULTS

The results for all three models are depicted in Fig. 1. Claude Haiku 4.5 shows a flat distribution across all priming conditions (Figure 1a). The category compo-sition is essentially invariant to the selection of the in-jected numbers. The model produces a consistent base-line of scientists, activists, artists, and fictional heroes regardless of whether it was primed with lucky numbers, emergency codes, or extremist symbols. Sonnet 4.6 displays the clearest and most concerning pattern (Figure 1b). When primed with extremist-coded numbers (14, 88, 1312, 1488, 18), Sonnet 4.6, in a few instances, produced dinner-party lists composed entirely of Nazi leadership figures. However, we might also ob-serve, in all cases (except the baseline), a shift towards darker/authoritarian figures. > V. DISCUSSION

Our results demonstrate that vulnerability to inference-time semantic contamination increases with model capability, at least within the range tested here. We argue that the robustness of Haiku 4.5 is not evidence of better alignment. It is rather evidence that the model lacks the dense cultural-associative represen-tations needed to propagate the semantic signal from the priming examples to the downstream task. In contrast, more capable models, with richer cultural-associative representations and stronger in-context learning abili-ties, appear more susceptible to inference-time semantic contamination. It is notable that we were able to shift the output dis-tribution despite the input and output guardrails imple-mented by Anthropic. This suggests that such perturba-tions may evade detection precisely because the distribu-tional shift is not total: the effects are subtle and graded rather than categorical. The mixed-valence condition is also instructive. Al-though it contains both positively-associated numbers and those linked to hateful or violent contexts, the net shift is still toward darker and more authoritarian content. This asymmetry suggests that high-negative-salience items carry disproportionate weight in driving the distributional shift, outweighing the moderating in-fluence of positive associations in the same context win-dow. The findings carry broad security implications, partic-ularly for systems that maintain memory across interac-tions — whether a chatbot that accumulates conversation context or a more sophisticated retrieval-augmented sys-tem that incorporates context from prior sessions. Any system that allows user-supplied content to appear as in-context examples is potentially vulnerable. Cross-channel data injection is particularly concerning: a mali-cious actor could contaminate the context window in one interaction, causing a separate, unaware user to subse-quently observe a shifted output distribution.  

> VI. CONCLUSION

We have shown that inference-time semantic con-tamination is real, measurable, and capability-gated. Contrary to the null result reported by Betley et al. [BTW +25] for GPT-4o, we find significant distri-butional shifts in mid- and high-capability Anthropic models using only k = 5 demonstrations, provided those demonstrations carry culturally high-salience con-tent (the authors of the original study tested inference-time only with the insecure code examples, whereas here we demonstrate the effect on culturally loaded numbers). The smaller model (Haiku 4.5) is effectively immune, not through superior alignment, but likely through the ab-sence of the dense associative representations that carry the contaminating signal. We further isolate two separable mechanisms: struc-tural contamination, in which the mere presence of demonstrations perturbs the output prior regardless of content, and semantic contamination, in which the spe-5

A key practical takeaway is that the effect is subtle by design. Because the distributional shift is gradual, it may evade both automated content filters and human review-ers, making it a viable vector for covert manipulation of deployed LLM systems. Several questions remain open: whether the capability-gating observed here is monotonic across a broader range of models, whether the asymmetry between positive and negative valence generalizes beyond the dinner-party task, and whether extended-thinking traces reveal ex-plicit relevance reasoning in more capable models. Re-peating the experiment on open-weight models (LLaMA, Qwen, Mistral, Gemma) would allow ablation of safety systems and more direct access to internal representa-tions, thereby enabling a cleaner separation of the two listed hypotheses regarding Opus 4.6’s behavior.
