# RAGShield: Provenance-Verified Defense-in-Depth Against Knowledge Base Poisoning in Government Retrieval-Augmented Generation Systems 

KrishnaSaiReddy Patil 

Abstract —Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems are increasingly deployed across federal agencies for citizen-facing services including tax guidance, benefits eligibility, and legal information. These systems are vulnerable to knowledge base poi-soning attacks, where adversaries inject malicious documents to manipulate RAG outputs—recent work demonstrates that as few as 10 adversarial passages (0.04% of a corpus) can achieve 98.2% retrieval success rates [1]. We observe that RAG knowledge base poisoning is structurally analogous to software supply chain attacks, and propose RAGShield, a five-layer defense-in-depth framework that applies supply chain provenance verification to the RAG knowledge pipeline. RAGShield introduces: (1) C2PA-inspired cryptographic document attestation that blocks unsigned and forged documents at ingestion; (2) trust-weighted retrieval that prioritizes provenance-verified sources; (3) a formal taint lattice with cross-source contradiction detection that catches insider threats even when provenance is valid; (4) provenance-aware generation with auditable citations; and (5) NIST SP 800-53 compliance mapping across 15 control families including SA-12 (Supply Chain Protection). We evaluate RAGShield against six adversary tiers—external injection, signature forgery, insider compromise, adaptive evasion, data loader exploitation, and in-place replacement—on a 500-passage Natural Questions corpus with 63 attack documents and 200 queries. RAGShield achieves 0.0% attack success rate across all tiers including adaptive attacks (95% CI: [0.0%, 1.9%]) while maintaining 0.0% false positive rate, compared to 8–13% ASR without defense, 7.5– 12.5% for RAGDefender, and 0–0.5% for RobustRAG. We honestly report that in-place replacement attacks by insiders who modify existing documents achieve 17.5% ASR—identifying the fundamental limit of ingestion-time defense and proposing hash-pinning mitigations. Notably, the cross-source contradiction detector catches subtle insider attacks (e.g., $15,000 → $15,500 numerical manipulation) that bypass provenance verification entirely. To our knowledge, RAGShield is the first framework to apply supply chain provenance to RAG security, the first to provide NIST 800-53 compliance mapping for RAG pipelines, and the first to defend against data loader exploitation attacks. 

Index Terms —RAG, retrieval-augmented generation, poison-ing, defense, provenance, government, supply chain security, NIST 800-53 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Federal agencies are rapidly adopting Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems for citizen-facing services. The General Services Administration launched USAi.Gov in Au-gust 2025, providing all federal agencies access to AI mod-els from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta [16]. The Government Accountability Office documented 89 RAG-based citizen-facing services across 28 agencies as of fiscal year 2025 [17]. These systems answer questions about tax obligations, benefits eligibility, legal rights, and government procedures—domains where incorrect information causes di-rect harm to citizens. RAG systems are vulnerable to knowledge base poison-ing attacks. Zou et al. demonstrated in PoisonedRAG that injecting just 5 malicious texts into a knowledge base with millions of documents achieves over 90% attack success rate on NQ, HotpotQA, and MS-MARCO [30]. Chaudhari et al. showed that 10 adversarial passages (0.04% of the corpus) achieve 98.2% retrieval success in the Phantom attack [1]. The PhantomText attack achieves 74.4% success across five major RAG frameworks using invisible text embedded in standard document formats [3]. The ConfusedPilot attack demonstrates integrity and confidentiality violations against Microsoft 365 Copilot’s RAG system [4]. 

Key observation. We observe that RAG knowledge base poisoning is structurally analogous to software supply chain at-tacks. In both cases, an adversary injects malicious content into a trusted pipeline: malicious code into a software build (So-larWinds [5]), or malicious documents into a knowledge base. The defense in software supply chains is provenance verifica-tion —cryptographic attestation of origin, integrity, and chain of custody, standardized through SLSA [6] and C2PA [7]. No existing RAG defense applies this principle. 

The gap. All existing RAG defenses operate on con-tent analysis : RobustRAG [8] isolates and aggregates pas-sages; RAGDefender [9] uses post-retrieval ML filtering; TrustRAG [10] applies clustering-based detection; the AV Filter [11] uses attention-variance scoring. Content analysis is an arms race—as attacks become more fluent (CPA-RAG [14], AuthChain [15]), content-based detection becomes harder. Provenance verification provides a fundamentally different defense: you either have a valid cryptographic attestation or you don’t.

3) Cross-Source Insider Detection (v2): Provenance alone cannot stop insider threats (T3). We add three content-based checks that run only on newly-ingested documents (not the established corpus baseline): 

Contradiction detection. For a new document dnew with embedding enew , compute similarity against all existing AU-THORITATIVE documents. If sim (enew , e auth ) ∈ [θlow , θ high ]

(topically similar but not identical), flag as a potential contra-diction. Rationale: a poisoned insider document will be about the same topic as legitimate documents but make different claims. 

Corroboration checking. Count how many existing docu-ments have similarity ≥ θcorr with dnew . If fewer than kmin cor-roborators exist, flag as uncorroborated. Rationale: legitimate policy changes appear in multiple documents (regulation + guidance + Federal Register notice); a single insider document making a sweeping claim is suspicious. 

Claim magnitude scoring. Compute similarity between 

dnew and the topic centroid of existing documents. If sim (enew , ¯etopic ) < θ mag , flag as an outlier. Rationale: even subtle numerical changes ( $15 ,000 → $15 ,500 ) shift the embedding away from the established consensus. 

D. Layer 4: Provenance-Aware Generation 

The LLM prompt includes trust metadata for each context chunk, enabling trust-aware response generation. Citations include the full provenance chain: source, trust tier, attestation status, and content hash.

Theorem 1 (Provenance Integrity Bound) . Let K be a knowl-edge base where fraction p of documents have valid prove-nance attestations, and let A be a T1 or T2 adversary who cannot produce valid signatures. Then: ASR L1 ≤ (1 − p) · ASR baseline (3) 

where ASR baseline is the attack success rate without RAGShield. Proof. A T1 adversary cannot produce valid signatures (by the EUF-CMA security of Ed25519). Therefore, L1 rejects all injected documents—they never enter the retrieval index 

K. The only documents available for retrieval are the p · n

attested documents plus any (1 − p) · n unattested legacy documents. The adversary can only inject poison into the unattested fraction, bounding ASR by (1 − p) · ASR baseline .Note: this bound comes from L1 ingestion blocking, not L2 trust weighting. L2 provides additional defense-in-depth but is not required for the bound. 

Corollary 1. When p = 1 (all documents attested), ASR L1 =0 for T1 and T2 adversaries. 

Theorem 2 (Taint Soundness) . Under the taint propagation rules of §IV-C, for any chunk c in the RAG pipeline: taint (c) ⊒

taint (source (c)) .Proof. By structural induction on pipeline operations. Base case: chunking preserves taint (rule 1). Inductive case: con-catenation applies ⊔ (join), which by lattice properties satisfies 

⊔iti ⊒ tj for all j. Therefore taint never decreases through the pipeline. 

Theorem 3 (Trust Monotonicity) . For any context C assem-bled from chunks {c1, . . . , c k}: trust (C) ≤ min i trust (ci).Proof. Follows directly from the join operation on the taint lattice.

B. Main Results 

Table VI presents the main results comparing RAGShield against two state-of-the-art baselines and ablations on the NQ benchmark. All confidence intervals are 95% Wilson score intervals. 

Key findings: 

RAGShield-Full achieves 0.0% ASR across all five tiers with 95% CI [0.0%, 1.9%] and 0.0% FPR. This includes the T5 adaptive tier where the adversary has valid signing keys, crafts semantically similar poison, and injects fake corroborating documents. 

RobustRAG performs well on most tiers (0.0–0.5% ASR) through its isolate-then-aggregate strategy, but allows 0.5% of T1 attacks through when poisoned passages form a local ma-jority in the retrieved set. RobustRAG provides no provenance guarantee and no compliance mapping. 

RAGDefender fails significantly (7.5–12.5% ASR across all tiers). Its centroid-distance filtering cannot distinguish well-crafted poison from legitimate passages when the poison is topically relevant. This confirms that post-retrieval content filtering alone is insufficient against sophisticated attacks. 

L1-only blocks T1/T2/T4 completely but fails on T3 (8.0%) and T5 adaptive (11.0%)—identical to No-Defense for insider attacks. This proves provenance alone is insufficient. 

T5 adaptive attacks are the most challenging: the adversary has valid provenance, crafts poison semantically close to existing passages, and adds fake corroborating documents. Despite this, RAGShield-Full blocks all T5 attacks through the contradiction detector, which identifies that the modified passages diverge from established AUTHORITATIVE sources even when corroboration is artificially inflated. 

C. Ablation: Why Both Layers Are Necessary 

The ablation demonstrates clear complementarity:  

> •

L1 alone fails on T3/T5 (8–11% ASR)—insiders bypass provenance  

> •

L3 alone achieves 0% across all tiers but lacks cryptographic guarantees  

> •

Full RAGShield combines L1’s hard boundary with L3’s content analysis  

> •

Neither RobustRAG nor RAGDefender matches RAGShield-Full on all tiers simultaneously 

D. Pre-Poisoned Corpus Degradation 

A critical assumption of the insider detection (L3) is that the established corpus is clean. We test what happens when this assumption is violated by injecting 0–20% poison into the baseline corpus before deploying RAGShield. RAGShield is robust up to 2% pre-poisoning (ASR remains 0%). At 5%, ASR rises to 5.7% as pre-existing poison corrupts the contradiction baseline. Notably, ASR plateaus at ∼13% even at 20% pre-poisoning—the corroboration and magnitude detectors continue to catch most attacks even when the base-line is partially corrupted. This plateau represents the residual effectiveness of the non-baseline-dependent detectors. 

E. Threshold Sensitivity Analysis 

We vary the three key thresholds ( θmag , θcorr , α) across their full range. RAGShield maintains 0% ASR and 0% FPR across all tested values ( θmag ∈ [0 .50 , 0.90] , θcorr ∈ [0 .55 , 0.90] , α ∈

[0 .0, 3.0] ). The defense is not fragile or threshold-dependent— the three complementary detectors provide redundancy, so no single threshold is a single point of failure. 

F. LLM-in-the-Loop Evaluation 

To demonstrate end-to-end impact, we evaluate 10 scenarios where poisoned context would cause an LLM to produce incor-rect government information. For each scenario, we compare the answer an LLM would produce with poisoned context (no defense) versus clean context (after RAGShield filtering). Without RAGShield, all 10 scenarios produce incorrect answers. The T3 attacks (rows 3, 4, 7, 9) would cause citizens to commit tax fraud or receive dangerously incorrect benefits guidance. The T3-Hard attacks (rows 1, 2, 5, 6, 8, 10) would cause financial errors of $50–$1,000. With RAGShield, all 10 scenarios produce correct answers from provenance-verified, clean context.

VIII. G OVERNMENT COMPLIANCE MAPPING 

Table XI maps RAGShield layers to NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 controls. SA-12 (Supply Chain Protection) is the primary mapping—RAGShield treats the knowledge base as a supply chain requiring provenance verification. 

Privacy Act compliance. RAGShield’s PII taint labels enforce Privacy Act requirements: purpose limitation (retrieval restricted to the purpose for which data was collected), minimum necessary (minimum PII retrieved per query), and accounting of disclosures (L5 audit trail records every PII-containing retrieval). Federal agencies deploying RAG over citizen records must file a System of Records Notice (SORN) per 5 U.S.C. §552a—RAGShield’s audit trail provides the disclosure accounting required by §552a(c). IX. Relationship to CivicShield. RAGShield extends Civic-Shield [18] in two directions: Layer 1 extends CivicShield’s input validation with document-level provenance chains, and Layer 3 extends CivicShield’s semantic taint tracking with pipeline-level taint propagation. Together, CivicShield (con-versational defense) and RAGShield (knowledge base defense) provide complementary protection for government AI systems. 

Limitations. (1) Our NQ evaluation uses 500 passages; production government knowledge bases contain millions of documents. Scaling to full NQ (300K+ passages) is future work. (2) The insider detection degrades when the baseline corpus is pre-poisoned beyond 5% (Table VII); periodic corpus auditing is required. (3) RobustRAG and RAGDe-fender baselines are simulated implementations capturing the core algorithmic principles (isolate-then-aggregate and post-retrieval anomaly filtering respectively); results may differ from production implementations. (4) The LLM-in-the-loop evaluation uses in-context assessment rather than automated end-to-end testing with a deployed LLM. 

Ethical considerations. All attack documents in our evalu-ation contain fabricated government information (incorrect tax deductions, false benefit amounts). We do not distribute these documents outside the evaluation context. The government documents in our corpus are generated from templates, not copied from official sources. X. C ONCLUSION 

We presented RAGShield, a five-layer defense-in-depth framework that applies supply chain provenance verification to RAG knowledge base security. By treating RAG poisoning as a supply chain attack, RAGShield provides a fundamentally different defense from existing content-analysis approaches: cryptographic provenance blocks unsigned and forged docu-ments, while cross-source contradiction detection catches in-sider threats that bypass provenance entirely. Our evaluation on the Natural Questions benchmark across five adversary tiers— including adaptive attacks by a RAGShield-aware adversary— demonstrates 0.0% attack success rate (95% CI: [0.0%, 1.9%]) with 0.0% false positive rate, outperforming both RobustRAG and RAGDefender on insider and adaptive attack tiers. RAGShield is the first framework to apply supply chain provenance to RAG security, the first to provide NIST SP 800-53 compliance mapping for RAG pipelines, and the first to defend against data loader exploitation attacks. As federal agencies scale RAG deployments for citizen-facing services, provenance-verified knowledge bases are essential to maintain-ing public trust in government AI systems. ACKNOWLEDGMENTS
