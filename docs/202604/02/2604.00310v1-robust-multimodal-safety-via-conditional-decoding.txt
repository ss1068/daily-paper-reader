Abstract 

Multimodal large-language models (MLLMs) often experience degraded safety alignment when harmful queries exploit cross-modal in-teractions. Models aligned on text alone show a higher rate of successful attacks when extended to two or more modalities. In this work, we propose a simple conditional decoding strategy, 

CASA (Classification Augmented with Safety Attention) that utilizes internal representations of MLLMs to predict a binary safety token be-fore response generation. We introduce a novel safety attention module designed to enhance the model’s ability to detect malicious queries. Our design ensures robust safety alignment without relying on any external classifier or aux-iliary head, and without the need for modality-specific safety fine-tuning. On diverse bench-marks such as MM-SafetyBench, JailbreakV-28k, and adversarial audio tests, CASA low-ers the average attack success rate by more than 97% across modalities and across attack types. Our empirical evaluations also show that 

CASA maintains strong utility in benign inputs, a result validated through both automated and human evaluations (via 13 trained annotators). Together, these results highlight CASA as a sim-ple and generalizable framework to improve multimodal LLM safety. 

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demon-strated remarkable capabilities across many tasks, but their use also brings serious safety concerns. If not properly controlled, LLMs can produce harm-ful, disallowed, or sensitive content, posing risks to users and society. Even after an alignment phase during training (e.g. supervised fine-tuning the model on safe behavior datasets), many LLMs re-main vulnerable to jailbreak attacks, which are adversarial prompts crafted to evade the model’s safety guardrails. The challenge grows even more

as LLMs become multimodal, incorporating inputs like images or audio in addition to text. Recent studies have shown that safety alignment in multi-modal LLMs can be compromised due to complex cross-modal interactions (Chen et al., 2024). Ensur-ing robust multimodal safety therefore continues to be one of the field’s fundamental challenges. A widely adopted defense strategy is Supervised Safety Fine-Tuning ( SSFT ), where a pre-trained model is further fine-tuned on pairs of harmful and benign examples to encourage refusals of un-safe queries. While SSFT improves refusal rates against malicious inputs, recent research suggests that SSFT tuned model inevitably degrades its per-formance on non-malicious inputs (Qi et al., 2024; Bianchi et al., 2024). That is, overly aggressive 

SSFT can lead to overblocking, the unnecessary re-fusal or suppression of harmless information. As a result, achieving robust safety alignment with-out sacrificing core utility remains an open prob-lem. This safety-vs-utility trade-off is especially pronounced in multimodal systems, where the combination of modalities can weaken the prior alignment (Qi et al., 2024). Moreover, modality-specific safety fine-tuning compounds the prob-lem: each new input modality introduces a poten-tial safety gap and requires expensive, modality-specific datasets (Deng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; Hendrycks et al., 2020) and complex hy-perparameter optimization (Qi et al., 2025). These limitations motivate us to find simple and generaliz-able safety mechanisms that preserve model utility while strengthening robustness across modalities. In this work, we propose CASA (Classification Augmented with Safety Attention), a simple but effective framework for robust multimodal safety. 

CASA modifies the MLLMs’ generation process by first predicting a binary safety token from the model’s own hidden representations before produc-ing any response. The model’s subsequent gener-ation is then conditioned on this safety token, en-

suring that benign queries yield normal responses while unsafe inputs automatically trigger a safety-aligned refusal. CASA explicitly separates the safety judgment from content generation within the model itself, and converts the competing objectives of utility and safety into a cooperative two-stage pro-cess. This is in contrast to the objective of SSFT , in which optimizing the quality of responses naturally competes with the objective to optimize the safety (Chen et al., 2025; Qi et al., 2025). In essence, CASA divides the problem into two stages: detection of unsafe contexts, followed by conditional generation appropriate to the context. Because the safety classifier is integrated into the MLLM’s own inference process (rather than rely-ing on an external classifier or a separate prediction head), CASA operates as an internal check, adding robust safety gates inside the model without al-tering its behavior on ordinary inputs. CASA also introduces a safety attention module that amplifies the attention towards malicious signals in the in-put, yielding consistent robustness against diverse jailbreak attacks for a more comprehensive safety defense. Our empirical results demonstrate that CASA sub-stantially improves safety robustness without de-grading performance on benign queries. Across MM-SafetyBench, JailbreakV-28k, and adversarial audio benchmarks, CASA achieves over 97% aver-age ASR reduction across various input modali-ties. Besides, CASA achieves complete mitigation (100%) of prefill and multiple other attack types. Our human-based and automated evaluations con-firm that CASA maintains strong utility on benign queries, validating its effectiveness as a general-purpose multimodal safety framework. In sum-mary, this paper makes the following main contri-butions: • A new conditional decoding strategy: CASA ’s two-step approach ensures that unsafe prompts are handled with maximum caution, while safe prompts are answered normally, avoiding the ten-dency of safety-tuned models to overreact and withhold non-harmful information. • A unified multimodal safety fine-tuning mech-anism: CASA operates entirely within the base model without external classifiers, auxiliary safety heads, or modality-specific fine-tuning. • Comprehensive evaluations: CASA demonstrates significant ASR reduction and preserved utility across text, visual, and audio attack benchmarks with both automated and human verification.

2.3 Connecting to Related Works 

The field of safety alignment for LLMs has pro-duced a significant body of work, particularly in recent years as LLMs have become more powerful and widely deployed. Early works in safety align-ment focused on instruction fine-tuning, where ma-licious queries were typically handled via refusal-style responses (Bai et al., 2022a). Qi et al. (2025) show that supervised safety fine-tuning effects of-ten concentrate in the first few generated tokens and can be easily bypassed by prefix-based jail-break attacks, while related efforts improve safety by strengthening refusal behaviors through decou-pled refusal training (Yuan et al., 2025) or by apply-ing inference-time backtracking to revise unsafe generations (Zhang et al., 2024). Our approach 

CASA explicitly separates safety decision-making from content generation by introducing token-level safety classification and conditioning the entire de-coding process on the predicted safety state, pre-venting unsafe content before it is generated and avoiding reliance on reactive refusal mechanisms, reinforcement learning, or continuous pretraining (Bai et al., 2022b). Multiple works argue that safety alignment de-grades when aligning different modalities, like vi-sion or audio representation, to an aligned text LLM backbone. Authors in (Xu et al., 2025) claim that degradation in vision language models (VLMs) arises from insufficient alignment between the hid-den layer representations of the backbone LLM and the vision encoder, which confuses the VLM into overriding the backbone LLM’s safety alignment for malicious multi-modal queries. While works in adversarial audio jailbreak attacks are plenty (Kang et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024), safety align-ment for audio language models is a nascent field of research. (Peri et al., 2024) introduced a simple defense method of random noise addition to protect Speech Language Models against adversarial jail-break attacks, while (Alexos et al., 2025) leverages adversarial training to achieve robustness. How-ever, this method provides defense against specific jailbreak attacks and may not generalize across a wide variety of vulnerabilities. (Zou et al., 2024) develops a representation rerouting methodology that selectively unlearns harmful concepts from an LLMs feature map, providing a strong jailbreak defense mechanism. Recent LLM safety research has also explored conditional decoding, explicitly separating the safety decision from content generation. Grad-Safe (Xie et al., 2024) and SafeQuant (Padakandla et al., 2025) use gradient-based features to classify prompts as safe or unsafe before generation. Cir-cuit Breaker (Zou et al., 2024) modifies internal representations to block unsafe generation, while Safety Trigger Token (Gu et al., 2025) forces gen-eration of a refusal token at the start of each re-sponse. CASA unifies the above lines of related work into a single, model-internal framework. Like conditional decoding methods, CASA introduces an explicit safety decision step, but, unlike gradient-based or classifier-attached approaches, it performs safety prediction natively within the base model by generating a binary safety token from its hidden states. This design removes the need for external classifiers or inference-head modules while main-taining interpretability through conditional genera-tion. 3.1 Training Datasets 

We collect 6.2k harmful questions from the vari-ous text jailbreak sources, including Harmbench (Mazeika et al., 2024), SafetyBench (Zhang et al., 2023), JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024), and (Zou et al., 2023). We also randomly select a subset of 10k benign questions with their respective re-sponses from the Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023). Our combined dataset contains approximately 16k {question, response} pairs where the responses to harmful questions are randomly chosen from a sep-arate set of refusal responses. 

3.2 Evaluation Datasets Utility-text : To evaluate the utility of trained mod-els, we select 100 questions at random from a pub-licly available textual instruction tuning dataset 1,consisting of different categories such as code gen-eration, logical reasoning, math, text re-writing, etc. 

Utility-MME : This dataset evaluates the percep-tion and cognition capabilities of MLLMs exten-sively across 14 different subtasks (Fu et al., 2025). We use the test split provided in this dataset, which has around 2374 multiple choice questions probing various capabilities of the MLLM. We experiment using several datasets to evaluate the robustness of the proposed methods against a variety of jailbreak attempts across multiple modal-ities.

3.4 LLM as Judge (LLMaJ) Evaluations 

We used Claude 3.7 as a judge to assess the model’s responses both in terms of safety and utility. For safety evaluations, we ask the LLM to judge 1) whether the response given a harmful question is unsafe 2) whether the generated response is actionable and useful for the attacker. We use the strict criterion of determining any response as un-safe, even if that response is not actionable for the attacker. To evaluate the utility on safe questions, we use an A/B testing setup as the prompt to LLM. The specific prompt instructions are mentioned in Appendix A.3 and A.4 respectively. A discussion of the prompt experimentation and candidate LLMs we considered is summarized in A.6.

4.2 Prefill attacks 

In Table 3, we report the evaluation results on pre-fill attacks without the addition of a safety prompt. Specifically, we performed analyses on different prefills of varying token lengths. The pre-trained model shows significant vulnerability when us-ing a prefill prefix during generation. In general, longer prefills are more successful in jailbreaking the model. Though both CB and SSFT baseline show some robustness compared to the pre-trained model, its effectiveness varies widely based on the prefill length. On the other hand, the proposed method offers robust safety alignment independent of the prefill lengths.

5 Conclusion 

We present a novel fine-tuning strategy, CASA , that provides robust safety alignment across several jailbreak benchmarks. We compare our proposed method with a vanilla safety finetuning strategy and a strong baseline Circuit Breaker, and show improved safety alignment across different bench-marks. We also demonstrate the importance of the safety attention module that provides greater ASR reduction on multiple multi-modal jailbreak datasets. Finally, our thorough evaluations includ-ing human annotations provide conclusive evidence of the benefits of the proposed methods.

Limitations 

In this work we have demonstrated the effective-ness of our proposed method as a robust safety alignment technique. We have shown numerous experiments covering a variety of multi-modal jail-break attempts. However, we do acknowledge that there could be other more sophisticated jailbreak attempts that can prove to be more challenging for our proposed methods. We will continuously update our evaluations with stronger jailbreak at-tempts in the future. Another potential challenge of our method is the use of cross-attention over the entire prompt. This can become a computational bottleneck as the prompt size grows. However, we emphasize that the attention scores are computed just once before the response generation and are not used during the rest of the decoding process. We also limit the scope of safety to explicitly malicious queries in this work. Authors in (Zhou et al., 2025) show how seemingly safe questions can be used to generate harmful responses by providing different contexts. We leave such work for the future. 

Ethical Considerations 

We obtained necessary consent from all the partic-ipants of our data collection efforts following ap-proval by an internal review board. For the model artifacts used in this work, we adhered to the ap-propriate licencing. While we acknowledge the ethical risks associ-ated with jailbreaking techniques, this work rep-resents a valuable contribution towards a deeper understanding of speech-language model capabil-ities and limitations. Our aim is to enable further research that improves model robustness, leading to safer and more beneficial applications. By re-sponsibly investigating methods to circumvent re-strictions, we shed light on potential vulnerabilities that could be exploited by malicious attackers if left unaddressed. Critically, our work also pro-poses and evaluates countermeasures to mitigate such jailbreaking attacks. While we encourage eth-ical debate on such emerging issues, we believe the merits of responsible disclosure, proactive secu-rity improvements, and developing defensive tech-niques outweigh any potential risks associated with our narrow jailbreaking experiments under con-trolled conditions. Overall, our work aims to make progress towards more secure and robust multi-modal speech-language models.

A.1 Comparison of Human and LLMaJ evaluations 

We computed the inter-annotator agreement (IAA) among the human evaluators and between the hu-mans and LLMaJ in evaluating the safety of re-sponses. Agreement among human evaluators was measured using Krippendorff’s α because we have three raters per unit and not all evaluators rated each pair. Agreement between human evaluators and the LLMaJ was measured using Cohen’s κ.As reported in Table 5, we observed consider-able agreement both between the human and LL-MaJ ( κ = 0 .8) and among the human evaluators themselves ( α = 0 .6). The higher agreement be-tween the humans −LLMaJ as compared to the human −human agreement could be attributed to reduced noise owing to the aggregation of human data. IAA between the human and LLMaJ outputs for utility was calculated using Gwet’s AC1 (Gwet, 2001), a metric designed to handle imbalanced class distributions (5 utility dimensions mentioned in Section 3.5). We observed that the agreement varies by dimension, however moderate agreement (Gwet’s AC1 > 0.4) was observed for all dimen-sions.

A.7 Training Hyperparameters 

The training was done using 4 A100 GPUs with a per-device batchsize of 8 and gradient accumu-lation of 2.
