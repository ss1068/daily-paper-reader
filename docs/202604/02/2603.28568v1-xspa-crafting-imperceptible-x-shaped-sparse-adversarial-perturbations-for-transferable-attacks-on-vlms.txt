## Abstract 

Vision-language models (VLMs) rely on a shared visual-textual rep-resentation space to support tasks such as zero-shot classification, image captioning, and visual question answering (VQA). Although this shared space enables strong cross-task generalization, it also creates a critical vulnerability: subtle visual perturbations may prop-agate through the common embedding space and induce correlated semantic failures across tasks. This issue is especially concerning in interactive and decision-support scenarios, yet it remains unclear whether VLMs are still fragile under highly constrained, sparse, and geometrically fixed perturbations. We propose X-shaped Sparse Pixel Attack (XSPA), an imperceptible structured attack that re-stricts perturbations to two intersecting diagonal lines. Compared with dense perturbations and flexible localized patches, XSPA oper-ates under a much stricter attack budget, offering a stringent test of VLM robustness. Within this sparse support, XSPA jointly optimizes a classification-oriented objective, cross-task semantic guidance, and regularization on perturbation magnitude and linewise smooth-ness, inducing both transferable misclassification and semantic drift in captioning and VQA while preserving visual subtlety. Under the default setting, XSPA modifies only about 1.76% of image pixels. Experiments on the COCO dataset show that XSPA consistently degrades performance across zero-shot classification, image cap-tioning, and VQA. Zero-shot accuracy drops by 52.33 points on OpenAI CLIP ViT-L/14 and 67.00 points on OpenCLIP ViT-B/16, while GPT-4-evaluated caption consistency decreases by up to 58.60 points and VQA correctness by up to 44.38 points. These results show that even highly sparse and visually subtle perturbations with fixed geometric priors can substantially disrupt cross-task seman-tics in VLMs, revealing an important robustness gap in current multimodal systems.

## 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in multimodal learning, driven by models such as CLIP and BLIP-2, have substantially improved cross-modal under-standing in zero-shot classification, visual question answering, and open-ended generation [ 13 , 23 ]. A key reason is the shared embed-ding space aligning visual and textual representations across down-stream tasks. Yet this same mechanism also introduces a robustness concern. Perturbations affecting visual encoding may propagate through the shared semantic space and cause not only isolated prediction errors, but also cross-task semantic failures across classi-fication, captioning, and question answering. This matters because a single corrupted input can affect multiple decisions at once. Adversarial attack research has consistently shown that deep models are highly vulnerable to perturbations that are nearly im-perceptible to humans [ 9 , 18 ]. Existing attacks on visual models mainly fall into two categories: dense full-image perturbations un-der 𝐿 𝑝 constraints, which prioritize attack strength, and localized or physically motivated interventions, such as patches, shadows, or optical manipulations, which emphasize locality and real-world realizability [ 3, 11 , 41 ]. Although these studies provide valuable insights, they usually retain substantial freedom in perturbation support, shape, or optimization space. Such settings demonstrate attack effectiveness, but are less informative for a more fundamen-tal question: how vulnerable are VLMs when perturbation support, geometry, and optimization freedom are all restricted? This question is particularly important for vision-language mod-els. Unlike unimodal classifiers, VLMs must not only produce cor-rect visual predictions, but also preserve semantic alignment across multiple downstream tasks. As a result, robustness evaluation under highly flexible perturbation settings may obscure whether cross-task semantic behavior can already be destabilized by visual signals that are both extremely low-budget and strongly structured. Char-acterizing this vulnerability boundary is valuable in two respects: scientifically, it clarifies how much geometric freedom is required to disrupt multimodal alignment; practically, it provides a controlled stress test for future robustness evaluation and defense design. Motivated by this perspective, we study whether a perturba-tion with extremely limited support and fixed geometry is already sufficient to induce substantial cross-task semantic disruption in VLMs. Rather than treating perturbation shape as a free variable, we impose a highly constrained geometric prior and use it as a controlled probe of multimodal robustness. Concretely, we propose X-shaped Sparse Pixel Attack (XSPA), which confines perturba-tions to a sparse X-shaped structure formed by two intersecting diagonal line segments. Within this restricted region, XSPA inte-grates transfer-enhancing optimization strategies [ 32 ] with joint semantic objectives for zero-shot classification, image captioning, and visual question answering. Beyond inducing label errors, the optimization promotes target-oriented semantic attraction and sup-presses source semantics, thereby encouraging semantic drift across downstream generative tasks. Figure 1 illustrates this pipeline, from sparse perturbation optimization on the surrogate CLIP model to transfer-based evaluation on downstream vision-language tasks. We evaluate XSPA on four CLIP-style visual encoders and six downstream VLMs, using LLM-as-a-Judge style automatic evalua-tors for open-ended tasks [ 22 , 39 ]. The results show that XSPA sub-stantially degrades performance on zero-shot classification, image captioning, and visual question answering under a highly restricted perturbation budget. These findings suggest that even when per-turbations are confined to an extremely small region with a fixed geometric prior, VLMs remain vulnerable to cross-task semantic dis-ruption. More broadly, our results show that multimodal robustness cannot be understood only through dense noise or flexible local attacks; it must also account for the fragility of shared semantic alignment under strongly constrained structured perturbations. In this sense, XSPA serves not only as an effective attack, but also as a controlled diagnostic tool for probing how little geometric freedom is needed to destabilize multimodal behavior. This perspective helps connect attack evaluation with a more precise characterization of vulnerability boundaries in shared visual-textual representation spaces. The main contributions of this work are as follows. 

• We propose XSPA, a structured sparse attack for vision-language models that constrains perturbations to a fixed X-shaped support and unifies momentum-based optimization, input diversity, cross-task semantic guidance, and source-semantic suppression. 

• We conduct extensive experiments on zero-shot classifi-cation, image captioning, and visual question answering using four CLIP-style encoders and six downstream vision-language models, consistently verifying the effectiveness of XSPA. 

• We perform in-depth analyses through ablations on opti-mization steps, perturbation budget, and smoothness reg-ularization, together with attention-based visualizations, revealing that highly constrained structured perturbations can still induce strong cross-task semantic disruption. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Sparse and Structured Attacks on Vision Models 

Adversarial attacks on vision models provide the methodological basis for studying attacks on vision-language models. Early first-order, decision-boundary-based, transfer, and query-efficient black-box attacks established that deep networks remain vulnerable under diverse threat models [ 1 , 9, 18 , 20 , 32 ]. Most relevant to our work are sparse and structured attacks: One Pixel Attack, SparseFool, and Sparse-PGD show that modifying only a few pixels can already induce failures [ 19 , 28 , 40 ], while Adversarial Patch, cross-shaped patch attacks, and physically inspired attacks based on shadows, projected light, and natural illumination demonstrate that effective attacks need not rely on dense full-image noise or purely digital settings [ 3, 11 , 12 , 24 , 41 ]. However, most existing methods still optimize support size, patch area, or physical realizability rather than testing highly constrained geometric priors under extremely low perturbation budgets; by contrast, we ask whether a fixed X-shaped sparse perturbation is already sufficient to induce cross-task semantic deviation in VLMs.

## 2.2 Attacks and Robustness Studies on Vision-Language Models 

With the rise of CLIP-style vision-language pre-training, robustness in shared cross-modal embedding spaces has become a central issue in multimodal learning [ 23 ]. Prior studies show that adversarial perturbations can transfer across retrieval, classification, and gener-ation through the shared representation space, and that large VLMs remain vulnerable in both classification-oriented and open-ended settings [ 5 , 10 , 36 , 38 ]. Recent attack work improves cross-model transferability, unified cross-task design, and multimodal attacks under open-ended or illumination-driven settings [ 16 , 17 , 33 , 35 , 37 ]. Meanwhile, robustness studies have explored certification, robust adaptation, training-free enhancement, and test-time tuning for VLMs [ 7, 25 , 27 , 30 , 31 ]. Compared with these studies, we focus on a more constrained yet more interpretable threat model, asking whether a fixed X-shaped sparse perturbation under an extremely limited pixel budget can already break cross-task semantic align-ment in VLMs. 

## 2.3 Automatic Evaluation for Open-Ended Tasks 

For zero-shot classification, adversarial effectiveness can be mea-sured directly by accuracy or attack success rate, but for open-ended tasks such as image captioning and visual question answering, exact-match and lexical-overlap metrics often fail to capture semantically meaningful adversarial changes [ 5, 38 ]. We therefore complement standard task metrics with a GPT-4-based automatic evaluator that assesses semantic correctness and factual consistency, providing a more faithful measurement of whether perturbations alter the model’s understanding of image content rather than merely chang-ing surface wording. This protocol is consistent with the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm and recent VLM robustness studies that emphasize semantic assessment beyond lexical matching [22, 39].

Baselines. We compare XSPA with NaturalLightAttack, Shad-owAttack, and ITA [ 11 , 16 , 41 ]. These baselines cover natural-light, shadow-based, and illumination-transformation attacks. Their threat models do not perfectly match the fixed sparse geometry of XSPA, so the comparison should be interpreted as a robustness-oriented reference rather than a strict like-for-like ranking. Evaluation Metrics. For zero-shot classification, we report Top-1 accuracy and the relative drop with respect to clean samples. For image captioning and visual question answering, we adopt the LLM-as-a-Judge protocol and use GPT-4 to evaluate caption consistency and answer correctness, respectively [21 , 22 , 39 ]. All open-ended evaluations use a fixed prompt template and a consistent scoring rubric across methods and models. 

Implementation Details. Unless otherwise specified, all ex-periments use the default attack configuration. XSPA is optimized on a CLIP-style surrogate model and then transferred to down-stream vision-language models for evaluation. The total number of iterations is set to 200. ## 4.3 Grad-CAM-Based Attention Heatmap Analysis 

To further probe how XSPA reshapes visual representations, we generate Grad-CAM attention heatmaps for clean and adversarial samples and compare their spatial response patterns before and after perturbation [ 26 ]. As shown in Figure 4, across multiple exam-ples, clean images generally show stronger responses on the main object regions, whereas adversarial images exhibit more diffuse or shifted attention patterns. This change is visible not only on foreground-dominant cases such as elephant , bear , and bird , but also on scenes containing smaller or more localized targets such as 

stop sign , fire hydrant , airplane , phone , and boat . In several cases, the adversarial heatmaps no longer peak on the most semantically decisive region, and their responses become more scattered over background or irrelevant local structures, indicating that the per-turbation alters how the model allocates visual evidence. These observations suggest that the effect of XSPA is not limited to local perturbations at the input level. More importantly, it re-shapes the model’s discriminative attention patterns. Although the perturbation is consistently restricted to a fixed X-shaped sparse structure, its influence propagates into the model’s internal repre-sentation layers and eventually appears as a reorganization of at-tention distribution. This is consistent with the performance degra-dation observed in the main experiments, indicating that sparse perturbations with a fixed geometric pattern are sufficient to sub-stantially disrupt the model’s focus on key visual evidence across diverse scenes, object scales, and cluttered visual contexts while preserving a largely natural appearance.

Effect of Iteration Number. We first analyze the effect of the number of optimization steps on attack performance. As shown in Figure 5, increasing the iteration number consistently improves ASR across all four CLIP backbones. At 200 iterations, ASR reaches 70.00%, 59.33%, 39.33%, and 39.67% on OpenCLIP ViT-B/16, OpenAI CLIP L/14, MetaCLIP L/14, and EVA-CLIP G/14, respectively. Be-yond this point, the gain is limited and the curves gradually saturate, so we use 200 iterations as the default setting. The improvement is especially pronounced in the early stage of optimization, where additional steps rapidly align the sparse perturbation with seman-tically sensitive regions. After roughly 150 to 200 iterations, the curves flatten across all encoders, indicating that the optimization process approaches a stable local optimum rather than oscillating unpredictably. This consistent trend suggests that the optimization behavior remains stable across different encoder families and pre-training sources, and that convergence stays similarly well-behaved despite architectural differences, while the selected iteration budget offers a favorable trade-off between attack strength and computa-tional cost. 

Effect of Perturbation Budget. We further analyze how the per-turbation budget influences attack performance. As shown in Fig-ure 6, enlarging the budget steadily increases the attack success rate from 23.00% to 70.00%, while also producing larger final per-turbation magnitudes, indicating that a wider budget grants the optimizer more room to search for effective solutions within the fixed X-shaped support and better align the sparse perturbation with semantically decisive regions. This trend remains smooth rather than abrupt across the tested range, suggesting that XSPA does not depend on a single threshold effect and instead benefits from progressively expanded optimization freedom throughout the attack process.

## 5 Conclusion 

This paper presents XSPA, a structured sparse attack on vision-language models that optimizes perturbations within an extremely small X-shaped region using CLIP supervision, cross-task seman-tic guidance, source-semantic suppression, and smoothness regu-larization. Experiments on COCO show that XSPA markedly de-grades zero-shot classification on four CLIP encoders and consis-tently disrupts image captioning and visual question answering across multiple VLMs. Ablation studies further confirm that the attack strength depends jointly on the iteration budget, perturba-tion budget, and smoothness design. Overall, the results reveal that vision-language models remain vulnerable to extremely sparse, ge-ometrically constrained, yet semantically targeted perturbations, motivating stronger structure-aware defenses and more realistic robustness evaluation.
