## Abstract 

Large language model (LLM) agents may expose sensitive information through more than their final textual responses. Whenever private content is internally selected, assembled, and reused inside an agent pipeline, an attacker may attempt to turn that hidden dependence into an observable output signal. Existing evidence of this risk is strongest for memory leakage, but current attack formulations remain largely tied to specific systems and output surfaces. In this paper, we formulate privacy leakage in agentic systems as a channel inversion problem and present 

CIPL (Channel Inversion for Privacy Leakage), a target-independent framework for studying such attacks. CIPL represents a target system through a common sig-nature consisting of a sensitive source, selection, assembly, execution, observation, and extraction stages, and instantiates attacks through a reusable attack language built from a locator, an aligner, and a diversification policy. As a unified evaluation framework, CIPL supports cross-target comparison while preserving target-specific execution semantics. Our results provide initial evidence that privacy leakage is not confined to memory alone; instead, it depends on how sensitive content is routed into attacker-visible observation channels. These findings suggest that privacy evaluation for agentic systems should move beyond single-surface attack analysis toward a channel-oriented view of information exposure. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language model (LLM) agents increasingly operate over sensitive user data, including historical interactions, retrieved documents, and tool outputs Xi et al. [2023], Zhang et al. [2024b], He et al. [2025]. As these systems evolve from single-turn text generation into multi-stage decision pipelines, privacy risk also becomes less localized. Sensitive information may be exposed not only through a final answer, but through any observable artifact that depends on private internal state, such as retrieved evidence, structured outputs, tool-call arguments, or tool-return echoes. Recent work has shown that memory-equipped agents are vulnerable to black-box extraction attacks. In particular, MEXTRA demonstrates that an attacker can craft prompts that induce an agent to reveal retrieved memory items, even when the attacker interacts with the system only through ordinary user inputs Wang et al. [2025]. A key insight behind MEXTRA is that a successful extraction prompt must do two things at once: it must identify the sensitive internal content to be surfaced, and it must map that content into an output form compatible with the agent’s workflow.

We argue that this insight reveals a broader principle than memory extraction alone. In many agentic systems, private information flows through a common pattern: a sensitive source is partially selected, assembled into model- or action-facing context, processed by the agent, and eventually exposed through one or more observation channels. From this perspective, memory leakage is one instance of a more general phenomenon: if sensitive information is consumed somewhere inside the pipeline, an attacker may be able to redesign the input so that this internal dependence becomes externally recoverable. Existing evaluations, however, remain largely system-specific, making it difficult to compare leakage behavior across architectures and output channels under a common formulation. To address this gap, we formulate privacy leakage in agentic systems as a channel inversion problem. The central question is no longer only whether a system stores private content, but whether its internal dependence on that content can be inverted into an attacker-visible signal. Based on this view, we introduce CIPL (Channel Inversion for Privacy Leakage), a target-independent framework for analyzing privacy leakage across agentic pipelines. CIPL represents a target system through a common signature consisting of a sensitive source , selection , assembly , execution , observation , and 

extraction . It further instantiates attacks through a reusable attack language composed of a locator ,an aligner , and a diversification policy . Under this formulation, memory extraction is recovered as one special case rather than the conceptual boundary of the method. This shift in viewpoint matters for two reasons. First, it provides a common language for seem-ingly different systems, including memory-based agentsWang et al. [2025], retrieval-augmented pipelinesZeng et al. [2024a], Qi et al. [2024], and tool-calling workflowsZhan et al. [2024], Wu et al. [2024a]. Second, it changes what counts as the primary attack surface. Instead of treating memory as the only meaningful locus of privacy risk, CIPL studies how private information becomes observable through different channels. In this sense, the method is not another prompt variant for memory extraction; it is a reusable framework for translating target-specific leakage pathways into a shared channel-inversion analysis. To make this formulation operational, we implement CIPL as a unified evaluation framework that preserves target-specific execution semantics while standardizing the attack and evaluation interface. Concretely, the framework supports a shared target signature, a reusable attack construction pipeline, and a common experimental protocol for cross-target comparison. In our experiments, we instantiate CIPL on memory-based agents derived from MEXTRA-style settings and extend it to retrieval-augmented and tool-calling targets. This design allows us to compare privacy leakage across heterogeneous systems without collapsing them into an artificially uniform architecture. In summary, our contributions are as follows: • We formulate channel inversion privacy leakage as a general problem for agentic sys-tems, showing that memory extraction can be understood as one special case of a broader observation-channel-oriented leakage mechanism. • We propose CIPL , a target-independent method for studying such leakage, built on a common target signature and a reusable attack language consisting of a locator, an aligner, and a diversification policy. • We instantiate CIPL on multiple target classes , including memory-based, retrieval-augmented, and tool-calling systems, and provide initial evidence that privacy risk depends not only on where sensitive content is stored, but on how it is routed into observable channels. Overall, our results support a shift in how privacy risk is studied in LLM agents. Rather than analyzing leakage only at the level of individual storage components, we argue that privacy evaluation should be organized around channels of observation : whenever sensitive information is internally used, the possibility that this dependence can be inverted into an observable signal should be treated as part of the threat model. 

## 2 Problem Formulation 

We study channel inversion privacy leakage in agentic systems. The core observation is that privacy risk arises not only from where sensitive information is stored, but from whether the system’s internal dependence on that information can be turned into an externally recoverable signal. Prior work has demonstrated this phenomenon in memory-equipped agents. In this paper, we generalize the problem 2beyond memory and ask a broader question: whenever sensitive content is internally selected and used by an agent pipeline, under what conditions can an attacker invert that hidden dependence into an observable leak? To formalize this question, we model an agentic system as a five-stage pipeline:

execution , and Obs denotes the observation channel . The sensitive source S may contain memory records, retrieved documents, tool-return content, or other private artifacts. Given an input query, the system selects sensitive content z, assembles it into an internal context or action-conditioned representation x, executes the resulting computation to obtain y, and finally exposes an attacker-visible artifact o. The observation o may take different forms, including free-form text, structured evidence, tool-call arguments, tool outputs, or execution traces. This formulation makes memory extraction a special case rather than the defining case. When S is an agent memory store and Sel is top-k memory retrieval, the problem reduces to whether retrieved memory content can be surfaced through the observable output of the agent. Our formulation lifts this specific setting into a more general problem of leakage through observation channels. To compare heterogeneous systems, we define leakage over normalized units . A unit may correspond to a memory record, a retrieved document identifier, a snippet, or a structured field, depending on the target. This objective differs from standard prompt injection or jailbreak objectives. The goal is not merely to induce arbitrary model misbehavior, but to transform private internal dependence into an externally observable and recoverable signal. We consider a black-box attacker who interacts with the target only through user-visible inputs. The attacker does not observe model weights, hidden states, or privileged internal logs. Following prior memory-extraction settings, we distinguish between two knowledge levelsWang et al. [2025]. In the basic setting, the attacker knows only coarse information such as the application domain and task type. In the advanced setting, the attacker may further infer limited implementation cues, such as whether selection is more sensitive to lexical or semantic similarity. In both settings, the attack remains black-box with respect to the internal execution of the system. A central distinction in our formulation is between internal exposure and external leakage . Internal exposure refers to which sensitive units are selected and therefore influence the system’s computation. External leakage refers to which of those units become recoverable from the observation channel. The distinction is essential because internal use does not by itself imply privacy breach: selected content may remain hidden, may be only partially revealed, or may be transformed into a weak signal that is difficult to recover. The problem studied in this paper is precisely to characterize when such internal exposure can be inverted into external leakage. Under this formulation, privacy leakage is defined at the level of observation channels rather than at the level of any single storage component. This is the problem setting addressed by CIPL. 

## 3 Method 

3.1 CIPL as a Channel-Inversion Formulation 

Our goal is to seek a target-independent formulation of privacy leakage in agentic systems. To this end, we cast privacy leakage as a channel inversion problem: whenever sensitive content is internally 3consumed by an agent pipeline, an attacker may attempt to construct an input that turns this hidden dependence into an externally recoverable signal. This perspective changes the unit of analysis. Rather than treating privacy leakage as a property of one storage component, such as memory, CIPL studies leakage as a property of the pathway through which private information becomes observable. The key methodological claim is that heterogeneous agent systems can be analyzed under a shared leakage formulation, even when they differ in architecture, task, or output format. Under this view, memory extraction is one important instance of a broader class of channel-oriented privacy attacks, rather than the only setting of interest. 

3.2 A Target Signature for Agentic Leakage 

CIPL represents a target system by a target signature 

τ = ( S, Sel , Asm , Exec , Obs , Ext) ,

where S is the sensitive source, Sel is the selection operator, Asm is the assembly operator, Exec is the execution process, Obs is the attacker-visible observation channel, and Ext is the target-specific extraction map from observable artifacts to leakage units. Given an attack query ˜q, the leakage process induced by target τ is written as 

z = Sel τ (˜ q, S), x = Asm τ (z, ˜q), y = Exec τ (x), o = Obs τ (y), V = Ext τ (o), (1) where z is the selected sensitive content, x is the assembled model- or action-facing context, y is the internal execution result, o is the attacker-visible observation, and V is the set of leaked units recoverable from that observation. This signature separates what is target-specific from what is methodologically shared. Different systems may differ in where sensitive content is stored, how it is selected, and what output surface is exposed, yet still admit the same channel-inversion view. This is the basis on which CIPL supports memory-based agents, retrieval-augmented systems, and tool-calling workflows within a single framework. 

3.3 A Target-Independent Attack Language 

CIPL instantiates attacks through a target-independent attack language rather than a target-specific prompt heuristic. An attack is represented as 

a = ( ℓ, α, π ),

where ℓ is a locator , α is an aligner , and π is a diversification policy .The locator specifies what sensitive internal content should be surfaced. Its purpose is to steer the target toward the particular internal objects selected for the current computation, such as retrieved memory records, evidence snippets, or tool-conditioned artifacts. The aligner specifies where and in what form that content should become visible. Depending on the target, the observation slot may be a free-form answer, a structured evidence field, a tool-call argument, or a tool-return echo. The diversification policy specifies how an attack budget should be distributed across multiple attack instances so as to enlarge the coverage of leaked units. An attack instance induced by a produces an attack query 

˜q = Render τ (a),

but the surface query itself is not the method. What is shared across targets is the functional decomposition of the attack: localize sensitive internal content, align it with a valid observable channel, and diversify the attack set to reduce overlap in selected units. This decomposition is what makes CIPL reusable across heterogeneous agent pipelines. 

3.4 Locator, Aligner, and Diversification 

The locator component determines whether the attack successfully addresses the relevant internal object. In long and structured agent contexts, generic requests for “all context” or “all previous content” often fail because the model attends to broader task instructions rather than to the sensitive 4content selected for the current computation. The role of the locator is therefore to specify the extraction target at the correct semantic level: not the entire prompt, but the specific internal artifacts that influence the current decision. The aligner component determines whether the targeted content can be rendered through a channel that is valid for the target system. Not every agent workflow naturally permits direct disclosure in free-form text. Some systems act through tools, structured outputs, or intermediate traces. In such settings, a successful attack must request disclosure in a form that is compatible with the target’s observable behavior, such as an evidence list, a search-box payload, a structured argument field, or a returned tool artifact. This is why CIPL treats alignment as a method component rather than a prompt-writing detail. The diversification policy addresses a different problem: a single attack query can reveal only the content selected for that query. To recover more private information, the attacker needs a set of attacks that induce diverse selections. Let {˜qj }nj=1 denote the attack set. The purpose of diversification is to reduce overlap among the selected sets and thereby enlarge the union of leaked units. Under limited knowledge, diversification is achieved mainly through variation in phrasing and task framing while preserving the same locator–aligner functionality. Under richer knowledge, diversification can be conditioned on the expected behavior of the selection operator, for example by varying lexical form when selection is sensitive to edit distance or by introducing domain-specific anchors when selection is driven by semantic similarity. Taken together, the locator, aligner, and diversification policy define a target-independent attack construction principle. The attack is therefore not tied to one template or one system class. Instead, CIPL provides a reusable language for translating a leakage objective into channel-compatible attack instances. 

3.5 Recovering Memory Extraction as a Special Case 

The original memory extraction setting is recovered as a special case of CIPLWang et al. [2025]. Suppose the sensitive source S is the agent memory M , the selection operator retrieves the top-k

memory records, 

Sel τ (˜ q, M ) = E(˜ q, M ),

the assembly operator inserts the retrieved records into the agent context, 

x = C ∥ E(˜ q, M ) ∥ ˜q, 

and the observation channel corresponds to the final answer or action-visible output generated by the agent. Then channel inversion reduces exactly to memory extraction: the attacker seeks to transform retrieved memory content into observable outputs through a suitable locator–aligner attack. This special-case relation is important for interpreting the novelty of CIPL. CIPL does not replace memory extraction; rather, it identifies which parts of memory extraction are target-specific and which are reusable across leakage channels. The reusable part is the channel-inversion logic. The memory-specific part is one concrete realization of the target signature. In this sense, MEXTRA serves as a motivating and validating instantiation of CIPL, not as the conceptual boundary of the method. 

3.6 Cross-Target Instantiation 

Because the method is defined at the level of target signatures and attack language, the same framework can be instantiated across heterogeneous systems. For memory-based agents , the sensitive source is the memory store, the selection stage retrieves prior records, the assembly stage inserts them into the agent context, and the observation channel may be either a textual answer or an action-mediated artifact. This recovers the classical memory leakage setting and provides continuity with prior work. For retrieval-augmented systems , the sensitive source is an external datastore, the selection stage retrieves supporting documents or snippets, and the observation channel exposes generated answers or structured evidence. The leakage unit is no longer necessarily a memory query; it may instead be a document identifier, snippet, or evidence entry. The same channel-inversion view still applies because the attack operates on how selected content becomes observable. 5For tool-calling workflows , the observation channel is not limited to the final natural-language response. Sensitive content may become observable through tool-call arguments, structured tool outputs, or echoed tool returns. These systems are particularly important because intermediate artifacts are often easier to monitor than hidden model states and yet are not usually treated as primary privacy surfaces. CIPL captures such cases by treating the observation channel as a first-class method component. Across all targets, what changes is the realization of the signature (S, Sel , Asm , Exec , Obs , Ext) ,not the method itself. This is the central methodological claim of the paper: privacy leakage in agentic systems can be studied under a shared channel-inversion framework even when the underlying architectures differ substantially. 

## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate CIPL from three perspectives. First, we ask whether the framework preserves the core leakage behavior of established memory-extraction settings. Second, we ask whether the same evaluation framework transfers to non-memory channels, including retrieval-mediated and tool-mediated observation surfaces. Third, we study which design choices most strongly affect leakability under a unified attack protocol. 

4.1 Evaluation Targets 

We instantiate CIPL on four targets spanning three classes of agentic channels. 

Memory-based targets. The first two targets are adapted from MEXTRA-style memory settings. 

memory_ehr is derived from the EHRAgent settingShi et al. [2024], in which the agent uses re-trieved records as demonstrations for code generation. In its default configuration, the target retrieves top-4 records and produces an externally visible answer through code execution. memory_rap 

is derived from the RAP-style web-agent settingKagaya et al. [2024], Yao et al. [2022], in which retrieved records are used to guide action generation and the observable artifact is an action-mediated output channel. In our unified CIPL setting, the main experiments use retrieval depth k = 3 for this target. 

Retrieval-augmented target. To move beyond explicit memory modules, we instantiate CIPL on 

rag_toy , a retrieval-mediated target in which the sensitive source is a small document store and the observable artifact is a generated answer conditioned on retrieved evidence. Depending on the extraction rule, the leaked unit may correspond to a document identifier, a snippet, or an evidence entry. This target is intended to test whether the channel-inversion abstraction transfers from memory retrieval to a more general retrieval pipeline. 

Tool-calling target. We further instantiate CIPL on tool_mock , a tool-using target designed to study leakage through tool-mediated observation channels. We consider two modes. In 

args_exfil , sensitive content is surfaced through tool-call arguments. In return_echo , sensi-tive content is surfaced through the tool result that is later echoed to the attacker. These two modes expose distinct observation surfaces while keeping the overall attack template fixed. Across all four targets, the sensitive source, observation surface, and extraction rule differ, but the attack budget, metric vocabulary, and reporting format remain shared. This design lets us compare privacy leakage across heterogeneous pipelines without reducing them to a single architecture.

4.4 RQ1: Recovering Memory Extraction as a Special Case 

A central claim of CIPL is that classical memory extraction should be recoverable as one concrete instantiation of a broader channel-inversion mechanism, rather than being treated as the conceptual boundary of the problem. Our results support this claim clearly. Both memory-based targets remain highly vulnerable under the unified protocol, showing that the original MEXTRA-style phenomenon survives when reinterpreted under the shared target signature and attack language. For memory_rap , all three providers reach the ceiling, with CER = AER = 1 .0. This is the clearest instance of a “selected ⇒ leaked” regime in our experiments: once the sensitive records are retrieved into the active computation, they are almost always rendered into an attacker-recoverable observation. This behavior matches the strongest form of memory extraction and provides a direct empirical recovery of the original threat model inside the more general CIPL formulation. For memory_ehr , the same phenomenon remains severe but not perfectly uniform across providers. 

MiniMax-M2.5 and DeepSeek again reach CER = AER = 1 .0, while qwen3.5-plus drops to CER = 0 .7467 and AER = 0 .7533 . This reduction is meaningful, but it does not overturn the basic conclusion: even under the least vulnerable provider in our evaluation, roughly three quarters of 8attack trials still recover sensitive memory content. The more important implication is methodological. CIPL does not merely restate memory extraction; it shows that memory extraction is one high-leakage realization of a broader observation-channel problem, while also revealing that model backbone acts as a second-order modifier of the leakage rate. Taken together, these results validate the continuity claim behind CIPL. The framework preserves the core evidence from MEXTRA-style settings while decoupling the reusable attack logic from the memory-specific system design. In this sense, the memory experiments serve both as a sanity check and as evidence that the proposed channel-inversion formulation faithfully subsumes the classical black-box memory leakage setting. 

4.5 RQ2: Beyond Memory — Retrieval and Tool Channels 

We next ask whether the same attack formulation transfers beyond explicit memory modules. The answer is yes, but the leakage profile changes substantially once the observation channel is no longer a direct memory-conditioned answer. 

Retrieval-mediated leakage in rag_toy . The rag_toy results exhibit a characteristic “low-CER/high-AER” pattern. For MiniMax-M2.5 , we observe CER = 0 .2133 but AER = 0 .9533 ;for both qwen3.5-plus and DeepSeek , CER = 0 .0 while AER = 0 .8000 . This means that the model frequently exposes at least part of the sensitive selected set, even though it often fails to reveal the full set completely in a single trial. The implication is important: retrieval-conditioned generation can still produce attacker-useful leakage even when it does not behave like verbatim memory dumping. In other words, non-memory leakage may be frequent without being complete, which is precisely why CIPL separates internal exposure from externally complete extraction. 

Tool-mediated leakage under realistic generation. Tool channels add an additional layer of structure because leakage can occur through multiple observable surfaces. Figure 2 com-pares the two tool_mock modes under LLM-in-the-loop evaluation. On MiniMax-M2.5 and 

qwen3.5-plus , return_echo is consistently stronger than args_exfil in both CER and AER. For example, on MiniMax-M2.5 , AER increases from 0.2800 for args_exfil to 0.3667 

for return_echo ; on qwen3.5-plus , it increases from 0.1400 to 0.4000 . This suggests that echoed tool returns are often easier to invert than argument channels when the provider does not already saturate. However, this channel ordering should not be overstated as a universal law. On DeepSeek , both tool channels are already near the ceiling, with args_exfil reaching CER = AER = 1 .0 and 

return_echo reaching CER = 0 .9933 and AER = 1 .0. In this regime, the difference between channels is largely washed out by a provider-level ceiling effect. Accordingly, the correct conclusion is not that one tool channel is always stronger than the other, but that observation-channel asymmetry becomes visible most clearly when the backbone is not already maximally leakage-prone. This cross-target comparison supports the main experimental thesis of the paper. Privacy leakage is not confined to memory stores. It also arises in retrieval-augmented answering and in tool-mediated intermediate artifacts, but with different observable signatures: memory targets tend toward complete extraction, retrieval targets toward partial-but-frequent exposure, and tool targets toward channel-sensitive leakage that depends strongly on provider behavior.

4.7 Additional Ablations and Reproducibility 

Beyond the weak-control comparison and the retrieval-depth study highlighted in the main text, we also run two additional ablation families: retrieval-rule ablations and source-size ablations. Following the protocol defined for CIPL, retrieval-rule ablations compare edit-distance and token-overlap retrieval on memory_rap , rag_toy , and tool_mock , while source-size ablations vary the amount of available private content for memory_ehr , memory_rap , and rag_toy . These experiments are run separately from the main table in order to isolate configuration effects from the 11 cross-target comparison. As specified in the experimental design, all ablations use the same attack budget n = 30 , one retry. Ablation experiments are reported separately from the main table and use three seeds. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, we provide the appendix ablation results for all three providers: MiniMax-M2.5 , qwen3.5-plus , and DeepSeek .We do not place the full ablation tables in the main paper because their role is explanatory rather than foundational. In particular, retrieval-rule variation helps test whether leakage depends only on the observation channel or also on the mechanism that determines which sensitive units enter active computation, while source-size variation probes how the amount of available private content changes both coverage and leakage. These results are important for understanding leakability, but they are secondary to the main cross-target claim. We therefore report the complete retrieval-rule, source-size, and per-target retrieval-depth tables in the appendix and retain only the most decision-relevant ablation figure in the main text. Our implementation is fully unified across targets. All experiments are launched through 

cipl/scripts/run_cipl.py , scheduled through a shared runner, and evaluated through a common metrics module. This design choice is not only an engineering convenience; it is necessary for making cross-target comparisons meaningful. Without a shared execution path, attack budget, and reporting vocabulary, it would be difficult to distinguish whether differences across targets arise from channel properties or simply from incompatible evaluation pipelines. For reproducibility, we disclose the model providers, default hyperparameters, query-construction strategy, seed protocol, and output organization in the appendix. Main experiments use five seeds and report mean ± standard deviation; ablations use three seeds and are reported separately to avoid conflating target-level findings with configuration-level sensitivity. We also provide the precise command entrypoint and directory conventions used to aggregate results from per-seed 

metrics.json files into the final tables and figures. Together, these details are intended to make the reported comparisons inspectable, rerunnable, and extensible beyond the four targets studied here. 

## 5 Discussion 

Our results support a shift in how privacy risk should be analyzed in LLM agents. The main lesson is not merely that memory-equipped agents can leak. Rather, privacy leakage appears whenever sensitive content is internally selected, assembled, and routed into an attacker-visible observation channel. Under this view, memory extraction is an important but non-exhaustive special case. The cross-target comparison shows that the same underlying channel-inversion logic reappears across memory-based agents, retrieval-augmented pipelines, and tool-mediated workflows, but with different observable signatures. A first implication is security-oriented. The attack surface of agentic systems is broader than any single storage module. In our experiments, memory-based targets tend toward near-complete extraction, retrieval-mediated targets often show partial-but-frequent leakage, and tool-mediated targets reveal strong dependence on which observation surface is exposed. This suggests that privacy auditing should move from a component-local view (e.g., “is memory safe?”) to a channel-oriented view (e.g., “through which visible artifacts can internal private dependence be externalized?”). In practice, this means that final-answer filtering alone is unlikely to be sufficient; prior safeguards have explored input/output moderation, adversarial filtering, and privacy-preserving context construction Inan et al. [2023], Jain et al. [2023], Wu et al. [2024b], Zeng et al. [2024b], Koga et al. [2024]. Structured outputs, tool arguments, tool returns, and other intermediate artifacts should be treated as first-class privacy surfaces. A second implication is engineering-oriented. Our experiments were designed around a unified runner, a shared metric vocabulary, and a common attack budget so that heterogeneous targets could be compared without collapsing them into the same architecture. This choice matters because otherwise apparent differences in leakage behavior can be confounded with differences in evaluation pipelines. The resulting picture is that privacy leakage is jointly determined by channel structure, provider behavior, and system stability. In particular, the model backbone itself emerges as a meaningful security variable: under the same attack protocol, different providers can range from partial leakage to near-saturation, or from leakage to outright instability. 12 A third implication concerns evaluation methodology. The separation between RN/EN and CER/AER proved useful because it distinguishes internal exposure from externally recoverable leakage. This distinction is especially important outside classical memory extraction. In retrieval-augmented settings, sensitive evidence may influence generation without being fully reproduced verbatim; in tool-mediated settings, sensitive content may appear in one observation channel but not another. A privacy evaluation framework that reports only exact string-match success or only final-answer leakage would miss these differences. We therefore view channel-aware metrics and controls as necessary infrastructure for studying privacy risk in modern agentic systems. More broadly, the results suggest a practical deployment recommendation: agent red-teaming should be organized around channels rather than only around end-task correctness. A system can remain useful on its nominal task while still exposing private intermediate artifacts under adversarial prompting. Evaluating only task accuracy or instruction-following robustness is therefore insufficient for privacy assurance. 

## 6 Limitations 

Our study has several important limitations. First, the current target set is intentionally heterogeneous but still limited in realism. While the memory-based targets inherit established MEXTRA-style settings, the retrieval-augmented and tool-mediated experiments rely on rag_toy and tool_mock , which are designed to isolate channel behavior rather than to reproduce the full complexity of production agent stacks. As a result, our claims should be interpreted as evidence that channel inversion generalizes beyond memory, not as a complete measurement of privacy risk in every real-world RAG or tool ecosystem. Second, our current leakage metrics are primarily canonicalized string-based metrics. This choice enables a shared evaluation interface across targets, but it may undercount semantically faithful leakage when the model paraphrases, compresses, or partially restructures sensitive content. The issue is already visible in the retrieval-mediated setting, where low CER can coexist with high AER. Future work should incorporate richer semantic matching, human validation, or task-aware leakage scoring to better capture non-verbatim disclosure. Third, our experiments are conducted through closed-weight, API-based model providers. This is appropriate for studying realistic deployment conditions, but it introduces sources of uncertainty that are difficult to fully control, including provider-side model updates, backend nondeterminism, and undocumented safety or formatting behaviors. Although we mitigate this issue by using multiple seeds, standardized prompts, and a common attack protocol, some residual variability is intrinsic to hosted-model evaluation. Fourth, the current paper focuses on attack-side evaluation rather than defense benchmarking. We do include weak-prompt controls and channel-aware ablations to identify where leakage comes from, but we do not yet provide a full comparative study of mitigation strategies. In particular, defenses such as retrieval-time filtering, output-side sanitization, structured channel hardening, and memory/data minimization are left for follow-up work Inan et al. [2023], Jain et al. [2023], Wu et al. [2024b], Zeng et al. [2024b], Koga et al. [2024]. Finally, our threat model remains black-box and single-session in spirit. We do not study multi-agent coordination, persistent cross-session identity binding, logging backends, or human-in-the-loop review pipelines, all of which may create additional privacy channels or alter the effectiveness of channel inversion. Extending CIPL to these settings is a natural next step. 

## 7 Related Work 

Memory in LLM agents and memory leakage. Memory is a central component in LLM agents because it enables the system to reuse past interactions, demonstrations, and task-relevant experiences across steps and sessions Zhang et al. [2024b], Xi et al. [2023]. This same mechanism, however, also creates a new privacy surface: once historical user-agent records are retrieved into the active context, they may become indirectly exposable. MEXTRA is the first work to systematically demonstrate black-box extraction of private user queries from agent memory, showing that carefully designed prompts can both localize retrieved memory items and align them with the agent’s workflow-13 dependent output surface Wang et al. [2025]. Our work is built on top of this line of evidence, but differs in scope and formulation. Rather than treating memory as the defining attack surface, CIPL treats memory leakage as one concrete instantiation of a broader channel-inversion problem. 

Privacy leakage in retrieval-augmented generation and in-context retrieval pipelines. A closely related literature studies privacy risks in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), where models condition their outputs on retrieved external evidence Lewis et al. [2020]. Prior work has shown that private datastore content in RAG systems can be extracted through adversarial prompting Zeng et al. [2024a], Qi et al. [2024]. Subsequent works further improve automation and scalability, for example with agent-based extraction strategies and adaptive black-box attacks that progressively leak larger portions of the hidden knowledge base Jiang et al. [2024], Maio et al. [2024]. These studies are highly relevant because they reveal that privacy leakage is not limited to model parameters or final answers, but can arise whenever retrieved private content is injected into inference-time context. However, existing formulations remain largely datastore- or RAG-specific. In contrast, CIPL abstracts these attacks together with memory leakage under a shared target signature over sensitive source, selection, assembly, execution, observation, and extraction. 

Prompt injection, prompt leakage, and tool-integrated agent attacks. Another important line of work studies adversarial control of LLM applications and agents through malicious instructions or hidden content. Prompt injection attacks show that attacker-crafted inputs can override intended behavior in LLM-integrated systems Liu et al. [2023, 2024]. Prompt leakage and prompt stealing attacks further demonstrate that hidden system instructions themselves can be exfiltrated from black-box applications Hui et al. [2024]. In the agent setting, indirect prompt injection attacks become more consequential because models can read untrusted external content, call tools, and act on the environment; representative examples include tool-integrated prompt-injection benchmarks and web-agent attacks such as InjecAgent and WIPI Zhan et al. [2024], Wu et al. [2024a]. More broadly, recent work has shown that autonomous agents can be compromised through attack surfaces that induce harmful or malfunctioning behavior beyond simple text jailbreaks Zhang et al. [2024a]. CIPL is related to this literature in that it also studies adversarial input design against agentic systems, but its primary objective is different: instead of hijacking goals or stealing the system prompt, CIPL focuses on turning internally consumed sensitive artifacts into attacker-visible signals through observation channels such as final text, structured evidence, tool-call arguments, and echoed tool returns. 

Mitigation and privacy-preserving context construction. Prior defenses for LLM applications include input/output safeguards, adversarial filtering, and privacy-preserving context construction. For example, Llama Guard frames prompt and response moderation as a dedicated safeguard model Inan et al. [2023], while baseline adversarial defenses have explored detection, preprocessing, and adversarial training against prompt-based attacks Jain et al. [2023]. On the privacy side, differentially private in-context learning aims to reduce leakage from in-context exemplars Wu et al. [2024b], Tang et al. [2023], and recent work on RAG has explored synthetic-data or privacy-preserving retrieval alternatives to mitigate datastore leakage Zeng et al. [2024b], Koga et al. [2024]. These efforts are complementary to our goal. CIPL does not propose a specific defense; instead, it provides a unified evaluation framework for measuring how well different defensive controls reduce leakage across heterogeneous channels rather than within a single subsystem. 

## 8 Conclusion 

In this paper, we formulated privacy leakage in agentic systems as a channel inversion problem and introduced CIPL, a target-independent framework for studying how private internal dependence becomes externally recoverable through attacker-visible observation channels. Rather than treating memory extraction as the defining setting, CIPL recasts it as one special case within a broader channel-oriented view of leakage. To make this view operational, we represented targets through a shared signature over sensitive source, selection, assembly, execution, observation, and extraction, and instantiated attacks through a reusable language built from a locator, an aligner, and a diversification policy. Empirically, we showed that this formulation supports meaningful cross-target comparison without collapsing heterogeneous systems into the same architecture. Across memory-based, retrieval-augmented, and tool-mediated targets, the experiments revealed three main findings. First, classical 14 memory extraction behavior is recovered cleanly under the CIPL formulation, confirming continuity with prior MEXTRA-style evidence. Second, privacy risk extends beyond explicit memory modules: retrieval-conditioned answers and tool-mediated artifacts can also serve as effective leakage channels, often with distinct signatures such as partial-but-frequent leakage or observation-surface-specific asymmetry. Third, leakability depends not only on where sensitive content is stored, but also on model behavior, retrieval choices, prompt strength, and system stability under a shared attack protocol. Taken together, these results suggest that privacy evaluation for LLM agents should move beyond single-surface or component-local analysis. What matters is not only whether sensitive content exists inside the system, but whether that internal use can be inverted into an observable signal through a valid channel. We therefore argue that channel-aware red-teaming should become a standard part of agent evaluation, alongside task performance and general robustness testing. CIPL is intended as a first step rather than a complete endpoint. Our current implementation focuses on a minimal reusable evaluation layer and initial target classes, leaving several directions for future work: extending from toy RAG and mock-tool settings to more realistic production workflows, incorporating semantic or human-validated leakage metrics beyond canonical string matching, studying additional channels such as logs and traces, and benchmarking defensive controls within the same unified framework. We hope this perspective helps reframe privacy risk in agentic systems from a storage-centered concern into a broader question of observable channel design. 

## 9 Broader Impact and Responsible Release 

This paper studies privacy leakage in agentic systems and therefore has clear dual-use implications. On the positive side, our goal is to improve the safety evaluation of LLM agents by providing a more general way to identify how sensitive information can become externally observable. We hope the framework helps researchers and practitioners audit systems beyond memory modules alone, compare risks across architectures under a shared protocol, and design more channel-aware defensive controls. At the same time, the work can also be misused. A target-independent leakage framework may help attackers reason about observation channels in systems that use retrieval, tool calls, or other intermediate artifacts. For this reason, we focus on evaluation methodology rather than operational exploitation details, and we frame the results in terms of risk assessment, controls, and experimental comparison. We do not claim that the current targets exhaust real-world attack opportunities, nor do we treat the present implementation as a turnkey exploitation toolkit. The broader lesson is that privacy and security risk in agentic systems should not be defined only by where data is stored, but also by how that data is rendered observable. This perspective has practical defensive value: it suggests that safer deployment requires not only stronger storage controls, but also better channel design, output hardening, retrieval policies, and pre-deployment red-team evaluation against attacker-visible artifacts. 

Responsible release. Because this paper is attack-oriented, a responsible release strategy should pri-oritize evaluation and mitigation over unrestricted operational guidance. In particular, we recommend releasing code and artifacts in a way that supports reproducible auditing while avoiding unnecessary disclosure of provider secrets, sensitive prompts tied to real user data, or deployment-specific private assets. Where possible, experiments should rely on synthetic or sanitized sensitive content and should be accompanied by channel-aware control baselines and mitigation guidance.

A.2 Query Construction and Attack Strength 

To keep the attack budget directly comparable across targets, we standardize all main evaluations to 30 queries. For rag_toy and tool_mock , whose default prompt pools are shorter, we explicitly expand the query files to 30 prompts. The strong-prompt versions are designed to encourage exact disclosure, including wording such as exact , verbatim , raw json , or explicit payload insertion. For the weak-prompt control, we remove these extraction-oriented directives and instead request only concise summaries or helpful issue descriptions. This distinction is important because it allows us to separate attack-induced channel inversion from ordinary task-oriented generation. For tool_mock , we evaluate both deterministic and LLM-in-the-loop variants. These are not different tasks, but different probes of the same target. The deterministic variant characterizes whether the channel itself is invertible when generation uncertainty is removed. The LLM-in-the-loop variant tests how much of that channel-level leakage remains realizable under realistic model behavior.

These results support a broader CIPL interpretation. Retrieval is not merely a preprocessing detail; it is part of the causal path that determines what becomes internally exposed and therefore what can later be rendered into an attacker-visible channel. Accordingly, leakability depends jointly on channel structure and selection mechanism, not only on the final output surface. The retrieval-rule ablations reveal three distinct regimes. For memory_rap , the retrieval rule changes the size of the exposed set but not the qualitative leakage outcome, since all providers remain fully vulnerable. For rag_toy , retrieval-rule choice only mildly shifts the balance between complete and partial leakage, and this effect is visible primarily on MiniMax-M2.5 . For tool_mock , however, the retrieval rule materially changes both internal coverage and external leakage strength, especially on MiniMax-M2.5 . This pattern reinforces the CIPL view that leakability depends not only on the final observation channel, but also on the upstream mechanism that determines which sensitive units are selected into active computation. 

D.2 Source-Size Ablation 

We vary source size to test how the amount of available private content affects both exposure and leakage. For the two memory targets, we compare source sizes 100 and 200; for rag_toy , we vary the source size from 2 to 5. Table 7 reports the memory-target results, and Table 8 reports the 

rag_toy results. For source-size variation, the two memory targets are effectively insensitive in qualitative terms: increasing the source pool changes RN, EN, and EE, but all three providers remain at CER = AER = 1 .0. Thus, source size mainly changes exposure coverage rather than the existence of leakage itself. By contrast, rag_toy shows a more nuanced pattern. MiniMax-M2.5 achieves relatively high AER at smaller source sizes, but degrades sharply when the source pool reaches 5, where execution failures appear and both complete and any-extraction rates fall. This again shows that more available private content does not necessarily translate into more external leakage.

## E Qualitative Cases and Failure Modes 

E.1 Partial Leakage without Complete Extraction 

The rag_toy setting provides representative cases where the model does not fully reproduce the selected set verbatim, but nevertheless reveals attacker-useful information from retrieved evidence. This behavior is exactly why CIPL reports CER and AER separately. A target may have low complete-extraction success while still leaking partial but operationally valuable content at a high rate. 

E.2 Instability at Larger Retrieval Depth 

In rag_toy under MiniMax-M2.5 , larger k values produce not only lower AER but also sharply higher execution-error counts. This failure mode suggests that increasing the amount of sensitive content entering active computation can destabilize the pipeline, shifting the security picture from pure exfiltration risk to a mixed confidentiality–availability risk.

F.4 Statistical Reporting 

All quantitative values in the paper are reported as mean ± standard deviation over seeds. Error bars in figures denote standard deviation, not confidence intervals. We report execution-error counts separately so that leakage failure is not conflated with runtime or generation instability.
