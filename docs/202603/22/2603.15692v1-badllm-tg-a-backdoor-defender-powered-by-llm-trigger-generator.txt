## BADLLM-TG : A BACKDOOR DEFENDER POWERED BY LLM TRIGGER GENERATOR 

## Ruyi Zhang, Heng Gao, Songlei Jian *, Yusong Tan, Haifang Zhou *

## National University of Defense Technology, Changsha, Hunan, China Email: {zhangruyi, gaoheng21, jiansonglei, ystan, haifang zhou }@nudt.edu.cn 

ABSTRACT 

Backdoor attacks compromise model reliability by using triggers to manipulate outputs. Trigger inversion can accurately locate these triggers via a generator and is therefore critical for backdoor de-fense. However, the discrete nature of text prevents existing noise-based trigger generator from being applied to nature language pro-cessing (NLP). To overcome the limitations, we employ the rich knowledge embedded in large language models (LLMs) and propose a Ba ck door defender powered by LLM T rigger Generator, termed 

BadLLM-TG . It is optimized through prompt-driven reinforcement learning, using the victim model’s feedback loss as the reward sig-nal. The generated triggers are then employed to mitigate the back-door via adversarial training. Experiments show that our method reduces the attack success rate by 76.2% on average, outperforming the second-best defender by 13.7% 1.

Index Terms — Backdoor Defense, Trigger Inversion, Large Language Model 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Pre-trained language models (PLMs) significantly change and re-vitalize the field of machine learning [1–9]. Unfortunately, there is mounting evidence that PLMs are vulnerable to various attacks [10–15]. The most representative one is the backdoor attack [16,17]. The attack manipulates a victim model that performs well on clean data but predicts the target label on poisoned data, which is applied with a specific trigger pattern. Backdoor attacks can severely com-promise models with only minimal perturbations [11, 18], highlight-ing the urgent need for effective defense mechanisms. Existing NLP backdoor defenses primarily rely on two strate-gies: poisoned data detection and model modification. Poisoned data detectors identify malicious samples based on specific charac-teristics, such as excessive robustness [19, 20], high perplexity [21], or spurious correlations [22]. Model modification approaches, in-cluding pruning [23, 24], knowledge distillation [25], and adversar-ial weight perturbation [16], aim to neutralize backdoors by alter-ing the model. But these methods neither reveal nor localize the triggers, and their reliance on model architecture significantly limits their generalizability. In contrast, trigger inversion [26–29] offers a model-agnostic approach for identifying backdoor triggers. It trains a trigger generator, initialized with random noise, to reconstruct the trigger under constraints defined in various spaces, such as loss [30] or feature space [26,31]. The generated triggers are then leveraged to mitigate backdoor effects through adversarial training. However, the

The main contributions are as follows: • We propose an LLM trigger generator, BadLLM-TG , for trigger-inversion-based backdoor defense. • We propose a prompt-driven reinforcement-learning frame-work to guide BadLLM-TG ’s training. • We conduct extensive experiments across three datasets, test-ing five defenders against four attacks, showing the effective-ness and robustness of BadLLM-TG .

2.4. LLM Trigger Generator Training 

Due to the discrete nature of textual data, traditional gradient-based optimization methods used in continuous domains (e.g., im-age processing) are not directly applicable. Instead, we train an LLM-powered trigger generator G via prompt-driven reinforcement learning (RL). The RL components are formulated as follows: (1) Agent : the LLM G(·; p) parameterized by prompt p; (2) Environment : the dataset Dtgt , dataset Dn-tgt and reward function R(s, a ); (3) State s:the input samples Dn-tgt ; (4) Action a: the trigger generator’s trans-lation ˆDn-tgt = G(Dn-tgt ; p); (5) Reward R(s, a ): the score assigned to ˆDn-tgt by the victim model M(·; ˆθ). Note that the state transition is an identity function, i.e., the input state Dn-tgt remains unchanged across rounds, ensuring that G learns the complete trigger pattern. 3. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS 3.1. Experimental Setup Datasets. Our experiments are primarily conducted on: SST-2 [32], HSOL [33] and AG News [34]. 

Backdoor Attackers. Four widely used attackers are tested: (1) Wordbkd [35] selects a rare word as a trigger. (2) Sentbkd [36] uses a short sentence as a trigger. (3) Stylebkd [37] employs ‘Bible’ style as a trigger. (4) Synbkd [38] uses a predefined syntactic template as a trigger. 

Backdoor Defenders. Four backdoor defenders are tested: (1) ONION [21] removes the words that increase perplexity. (2) RAP [19] reduces the texts with strong robustness to noise. (3) MuScle-LoRA [16] prioritizes the high-frequency clean mappings. (4) BadActs [24] aligns abnormal activations with optimized clean activation inter-vals. We also record the no defense performance as a baseline. 

Implementation Details. The poisoning rate is 20%, consistent with the original settings [24, 37]. The evaluation metrics are Clean Accuracy (CACC, higher is better) and Attack Success Rate (ASR, lower is better). The victim model is BERT-base-uncased [39] with learning rate of 2 × 10 −5, and our LLM trigger generator is Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct [40]. All experiments are conducted on a work-station equipped with an Intel Xeon E5-2678v3 CPU, two NVIDIA A100 GPUs, and 132GB of RAM.

3.3. Robustness Test 

We evaluate the robustness of BadLLM-TG , with poisoning rates ranging from 10% to 40%. Fig. 2 presents the mean and standard deviation results of five independent runs. Although most defense methods exhibit performance degradation as the poisoning rate in-creases, our method maintains a low ASR across all poisoning rates. As the poisoning rate increases, CACC declines, likely because the excessive poisoned samples make it difficult for the LLM to isolate the intended pattern. To conclude, the experimental results confirm that the effectiveness of BadLLM-TG is independent of the poison-ing rate, making it highly suitable for practical deployment.

4. CONCLUSION 

This paper adapts trigger inversion to NLP backdoor defense, solv-ing both initialization and optimization over discrete text. Specif-ically, we propose BadLLM-TG , a backdoor defender powered by LLM trigger generator. It is iteratively trained with prompt-driven reinforcement learning, using the victim model’s feedback loss as the reward signal. We then mitigate the backdoor effect through adversarial training on the dataset with generated triggers. Exper-iments show that BadLLM-TG reduces the average ASR by 76.2%, outperforming the second-best by 13.7%, while preserving CACC.
