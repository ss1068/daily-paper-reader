## Abstract 

The rapid advancement of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) has brought their safety vulnerabilities into sharp focus. However, existing red teaming methods are fun-damentally constrained by an inherent linear exploration paradigm, confining them to optimizing within a prede-fined strategy set and preventing the discovery of novel, di-verse exploits. To transcend this limitation, we introduce TreeTeaming, an automated red teaming framework that re-frames strategy exploration from static testing to a dynamic, evolutionary discovery process. At its core lies a strategic Orchestrator, powered by a Large Language Model (LLM), which autonomously decides whether to evolve promising attack paths or explore diverse strategic branches, thereby dynamically constructing and expanding a strategy tree. A multimodal actuator is then tasked with executing these complex strategies. In the experiments across 12 promi-nent VLMs, TreeTeaming achieves state-of-the-art attack success rates on 11 models, outperforming existing meth-ods and reaching up to 87.60% on GPT-4o. The frame-work also demonstrates superior strategic diversity over the union of previously public jailbreak strategies. Further-more, the generated attacks exhibit an average toxicity re-duction of 23.09%, showcasing their stealth and subtlety. Our work introduces a new paradigm for automated vul-nerability discovery, underscoring the necessity of proac-tive exploration beyond static heuristics to secure frontier AI models. The code and data are available at: https: //github.com/ChunXiaostudy/TreeTeaming .Warning: This paper contains examples of harmful texts and images, and reader discretion is recommended.

As the capabilities of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) continue to advance [3, 15, 19, 25, 29, 38, 38], they have demonstrated remarkable abilities in tasks such as image

The proven success of red-teaming in Large Lan-guage Models (LLMs) [4, 21, 22, 27, 32, 47] provides a strong foundation for its application in the multimodal do-main. Recent research has begun to extend red-teaming to VLMs, with initial efforts such as Arondight [24] and TRUST-VLM [6]. However, these pioneering red-teaming frameworks and VLM jailbreak methods are generally constrained by an in-herent limitation: they operate within a paradigm of pre-defined strategies. The VLM jailbreak method is cur-rently limited to a single manually-designed attack heuris-tic, whether employing specific prompt templates [18, 24, 33], typographic obfuscation [9, 23, 41], or fixed image pat-terns [17, 44, 46]. This reliance on a fixed set of known tricks inherently prevents the discovery of novel and un-foreseen vulnerabilities. The constraint is inherent in that even methods incorporating feedback mechanisms, such as TRUST-VLM [6], are restricted to refining test cases within their pre-established strategic framework. As a result, their exploration process is stuck on a single path. They are only capable of making a known attack strategy more effective, rather than systematically branching out to discover new ones. To address these challenges, we introduce TreeTeaming, an automated red-teaming framework that fundamentally shifts the paradigm from static strategy testing to dynamic, exploratory discovery. Unlike existing methods that rely on predefined templates, TreeTeaming grows its entire strategy tree from a single seed example. Our framework is architected around two core compo-nents: a strategic Orchestrator and a multimodal Actuator. The LLM-based Orchestrator autonomously expands a hi-erarchical Strategy Tree, which organizes abstract concepts as parent nodes and concrete attack strategies as leaf nodes. ## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Red-Teaming for VLMs 

Recent efforts have applied red-teaming to VLMs for a more comprehensive vulnerability discovery. Initial ap-proaches focused on establishing static benchmarks; for in-stance, RTVLM [16] introduced a comprehensive dataset for the static safety assessment of VLMs. Subsequent research shifted towards dynamic generation frameworks. Early examples like Arondight [24] followed fixed proce-dures, limiting strategic diversity. More advanced frame-works such as TRUST-VLM [6] introduced a closed-loop automated process to refine test cases, enhancing their sub-tlety and variety. However, a common thread unites these generative approaches: they operate within manually prede-fined strategic paradigms. While TRUST-VLM automates the generation of test cases within a fixed strategy, the over-arching strategy itself remains a manual prerequisite. This fundamental limitation prevents the autonomous discovery of novel attack strategies, confining exploration to refine-ments within a known scope. Our work addresses this gap by automating the discovery of the strategies themselves. 

2.2. Jailbreak Attacks on VLMs 

The landscape of VLM jailbreaking is characterized by a variety of methods, each representing a specific, manually-conceived attack strategy. These methods can be broadly categorized into perturbation-based [12, 30] and structure-based attacks [40], with the latter being particularly relevant to our work due to their reliance on strategic design. Structure-based attacks [40] subtly embed harmful infor-mation within image-text pairs through cleverly designed heuristics. These heuristics manifest in various forms. For instance, FigStep [9] encodes harmful text into the visual domain via typographic manipulation, while MM-SafetyBench [23] appends it below the image. Others ma-nipulate the image content itself: JOOD [10] overlays im-ages with transparency, CSDJ [44] composes them from distinct patches, and MML [41] applies transformations like rotation. Yet other approaches include Pico [18], which converts requests into code, and VisCo [48], which focuses on specific visual cues. Even more sophisticated methods like SI-Attack [46], which optimally shuffle and reorganize image-text components, are still fundamentally perform-ing optimization within a single, manually-defined strategic paradigm. In essence, these methods are instantiations of singular, hand-crafted heuristics. In stark contrast, our TreeTeaming framework moves beyond executing fixed strategies by au-tomating the discovery of the strategies themselves, thereby enabling a systematic exploration of the VLM vulnerability landscape.

3.1. Strategy Tree and Orchestrator 

Diverging from previous works that rely on fixed generation paradigms, the core of TreeTeaming is an efficient and sys-tematic mechanism for novel strategy exploration, realized through a dynamically evolving Strategy Tree. The Strategy Tree is a hierarchical knowledge structure designed to organize and track all explored attack strategies: • Root Node: Encapsulates the ultimate objective of the red teaming test. For the scope of this work, this objec-tive is defined as “Inducing the VLM to generate unsafe content.” • Parent Nodes (Strategy Categories): Abstract at-tack concepts that group related approaches (e.g., “Cognitive bias exploitation”, “Authority impersonation paradigms”). Parent nodes serve as organizational struc-tures to maintain conceptual diversity and prevent redun-dant exploration. • Leaf Nodes (Executable Strategies): Concrete, action-able strategy descriptions that the Actuator can directly translate into jailbreak samples. Each leaf node maintains its ASR, exploitation budget ( En) , and Dominant Failure Mode (extracted by the analysis model in Section 3.3). Only leaf nodes undergo evaluation through sample gen-eration and testing. 

Initialization. The Orchestrator is prompted with a one-shot demonstration (Detailed in Appendix D) to bootstrap the Strategy Tree, generating 1–3 initial parent nodes con-taining a total of 3–6 leaf strategies. Each seed strategy is evaluated by generating multiple test samples, which are tested in parallel against the target VLM. The ASR and fail-ure mode are then computed from these results. Critically, this initial evaluation does not increment the exploitation budget, preserving each seed strategy’s full refinement bud-get for subsequent exploitation. 

Orchestrator , acting as the “brain” of the framework, is implemented using a powerful LLM. Instead of making bi-nary decisions based on a single best strategy, it employs a nuanced, multi-criteria mechanism that dynamically allo-cates resources to the most promising and available strate-gies, preventing over-optimization of exhausted attack vec-tors. The decision-making process is governed by three key components: (1) a dynamic exploration threshold (τdynamic ) that adapts performance expectations as the tree grows; (2) a fixed minimum threshold ( τmin ) acting as an absolute quality floor; and (3) an exploitation budget (En)ensuring sufficient depth of refinement for each promising strategy. The core logic prioritizes all leaf nodes by ASR in de-scending order, then iterates to find the highest-priority can-didate for action: • Strategy Exploitation: The Orchestrator refines the first strategy node n satisfying: (1) ASR (n) > τ dynamic , and (2) En below its limit. It analyzes the current strategy de-scription and Dominant Failure Mode to produce an en-hanced version that addresses the identified weaknesses. This refined description replaces the original in-place, new samples are generated and evaluated, and the node’s ASR, En, and Dominant Failure Mode are updated. • Strategy Exploration: When no strategy meets exploita-tion criteria, the Orchestrator triggers exploration to dis-cover novel attack vectors. It receives the complete ex-isting tree structure as input, leveraging this global view to generate: (1) a new leaf strategy description designed to differ from existing approaches; and (2) the placement decision—selecting an existing parent category for tacti-cal variations within established themes, or creating a new parent category for fundamentally new attack paradigms. This dual-level structure enables the framework to main-tain both high-level paradigm diversity and fine-grained tactical variations. The context-aware generation ensures systematic expansion of attack vector coverage while pre-venting premature convergence to locally effective but globally incomplete strategy sets. Critically, each round after initialization executes exactly one operation—either exploiting a single strategy or explor-ing a single new direction. Different strategies are evalu-ated sequentially to maintain clear performance attribution, while samples within each strategy’s evaluation are tested in parallel for computational efficiency. 

3.2. Multimodal Actuator and Strategy Consistency Checking 

Once the Orchestrator generates or refines a leaf node, it must be translated into an actual image-text test case. This task is performed by the Multimodal Actuator .This Actuator is powered by an LLM and equipped with a specialized Toolkit of 11 pre-defined functions. These functions are organized into four distinct categories to pro-vide a comprehensive range of manipulation capabilities: (1) Geometric and Transformational Operators , includ-ing rotation, horizontal flipping, and perspective trans-forms; (2) Color and Filter Operators , such as color inver-sion, grayscaling, color channel shuffling, and blurring; (3) 

Composition and Splicing Operators , for concatenating or overlaying images; and (4) Advanced Generative Op-erators , which enable image generation and prompt-based editing. When tasked with a strategy from a leaf node, the Ac-tuator’s LLM controller initiates a seamless execution pro-cess. It first interprets the strategic guidance to devise an execution plan—a concrete sequence of tool calls. Follow-ing this plan, the LLM sequentially invokes the necessary functions from its toolkit, providing the correct parameters at each step to progressively construct the final adversarial sample. This flexible, multi-step process allows the Actua-tor to combine multiple tools to realize complex strategies that a single function call could not achieve. To guarantee robust execution, the system checks if log-ical preconditions are met before any tool invocation (e.g., verifying that the number of images is ≥ 2 before calling “image stitching”; checking if the target image exists be-fore “edit image”). This mechanism effectively prevents runtime failures caused by erroneous LLM instructions or state amnesia, guaranteeing stable execution of high-level strategies. To ensure that the generated test cases strictly adhere to the intended strategy, we introduce a Strategy Consistency Checker . The checker takes three inputs: (1) the gener-ated image, (2) the accompanying text prompt, and (3) the original strategy description from the Strategy Tree. It is prompted to answer a binary question: “Does this image-text pair faithfully implement the specified attack strategy?” 

The checker outputs a binary decision with a brief justifica-tion ensuring that recorded ASR reflects genuine strategy effectiveness rather than off-target samples. The empirical impact of this filtering mechanism is demonstrated in Ap-pendix D. 

3.3. Failure Cause Analysis and Dual-Loop Feed-back 

A deep understanding of failures is crucial for efficient strat-egy optimization and discovery. Our Failure Cause Anal-ysis Model establishes a dual-loop feedback mechanism to achieve this. 

Sample-Level Optimization Loop. This is a micro-level, rapid iteration loop. When a test case fails to jailbreak the target VLM, we capture its response. A dedicated LLM an-alyzes and classifies this response, attributing it to a specific failure reason, such as “Direct Refusal,” “Safety Evasion,” or “Insufficient Harmfulness.” This reason is fed back to the Multimodal Actuator . Upon receiving this feedback, the Actuator fine-tunes the current sample and retries within a maximum attempt limit. This process creates a detailed sample failure log and significantly improves the success rate of a single strategy execution. 

Strategy-Level Attribution Loop. This is a macro-level learning loop that provides critical input for the Orchestra-tor’s decisions. After all test attempts for a leaf node are completed, this module collects all associated sample fail-ure logs. By statistically analyzing and generalizing from these logs, the model identifies the Dominant Failure Mode for that sub-strategy. This highly condensed failure attribu-tion is then recorded back into the corresponding leaf node of the Strategy Tree. Ultimately, this strategy-level failure attribution provides critical input for the Orchestrator’s next round of “Exploita-tion” or “Exploration” decisions, thereby closing the entire learning and evolution loop of the framework. 

## 4. Experiment 

4.1. Experiment Setting 

Target VLMs. For our evaluation, we selected a total of 12 mainstream VLMs, encompassing a diverse range of model architectures and sizes. The selection includes 10 open-source models: LLaVA-1.5-13B [19], DeepSeek-VL [25], Qwen-VL-7B [1], Qwen2-VL-7B [37], Qwen2.5-VL-7B [2], Qwen2.5-VL-32B [2], Qwen3-VL-8B [36], Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Instruct [36], LLaMa-3.2-Vision-11B [7], and Gemma3-27B-IT [34]. Additionally, we evaluated 2 closed-source models: GPT-4o and Claude-3.5 Sonnet. 

Baselines. We benchmark our approach against two pri-mary categories of methods: jailbreaking and red-teaming. Our comparison includes seven distinct jailbreaking meth-ods, namely FigStep [9], MM-safety [23], Hades [17], JOOD [10], CS-DJ [44] MML [41], and SI-Attack [46], along with two red-teaming methods, Arondight [24] and Trust-VLM [6]. Furthermore, we incorporate direct prompting as a fundamental baseline to provide a clear ref-erence point for comparison. Detailed descriptions of all baseline methods and their respective experimental config-urations are provided in Appendix A. 

Metrics. To evaluate our approach, we use four metrics: • Attack Success Rate (ASR). This is the primary metric for effectiveness. To align with prior works [6, 10, 17, 41], we use Llama Guard 3 [7] as an external judge for binary safety classification. A comparison with GPT-4o-mini as the judge is provided in Appendix D. ASR is the ratio of successful attacks to the total number of queries, calculated as: ASR = Number of Successful Attacks Total Number of Queries 

• Sample Diversity. Following previous works [6, 24], We measure the diversity for both images and text. Image diversity is calculated as one minus the av-erage cosine similarity of CLIP embeddings (from 

clip-vit-large-patch14 [31]). Text diversity is defined as one minus the average Self-BLEU score. • Strategy Diversity. To quantify the novelty and disper-sion of the discovered attack strategies, we adopt two met-rics from STAR [20]: KNN-Distance and KNN-Entropy. We first encode each strategy (a concatenation of its name and the generated prompt) into a high-dimensional em-bedding using the clip-vit-large-patch14 en-coder [31]. Based on these embeddings, KNN-Distance measures the overall dispersion of the strategies; a higher value indicates that strategies are more distinct from one another. KNN-Entropy assesses the uniformity of the strategy distribution, with a higher value suggesting a more even spread that avoids isolated clusters. For both metrics, higher scores are better. • Sample Toxicity. We employ Llama Guard 3 [7] to as-sess the inherent toxicity of the text and GPT-4o for im-age components. The toxicity score is the proportion of samples classified as unsafe. 

TreeTeaming Setup. We evaluate our framework on 500 malicious questions from SafeBench [9], spanning 10 cate-gories (per-category ASR breakdown in Appendix B). The framework employs Qwen2.5-72B (temperature=0.8) for orchestration, failure analysis, and collaborates with Qwen-Image [43] and Qwen-Image-Edit [43] to generate multi-modal test samples. Strategy consistency verification is handled by Qwen2.5-VL-7B. We configure the attack loop with a maximum of 5 refinement attempts per sample, set the exploitation budget ( En) to 2, and cap total strategy ex-ploration at 15 nodes to balance depth and breadth. The dynamic threshold parameters are set to τinitial = 0 .4 and 

τmin = 0 .1. Detailed definitions of all components and their corresponding prompts are provided in Appendix E.

Effectiveness of the Tree Structure: To quantify the value of our hierarchical design, we performed an ablation where the Strategy Tree was replaced with a Flat Strategy Library. This baseline mirrors the paradigm employed in prior LLM red-teaming works such as AutoDAN-Turbo [22] and Au-toRedTeamer [47], where all strategies exist in a unstruc-tured pool. In this configuration, the Orchestrator’s “Explo-ration” phase is prompted to generate a novel strategy based on the entire list of existing ones, but without the conceptual guidance of parent nodes. We run the full experiment with this altered setup, setting the maximum number of strategies to 15 and maximum attempts to 5, while keeping all other parameters consistent with the main TreeTeaming setup. ## A. Implementation Details of Baseline Meth-ods 

In this section, we provide detailed implementation infor-mation for all baseline jailbreak methods used in our com-parison. Throughout all experiments in this paper, we treat all VLMs as black-box systems, requiring only model outputs without access to internal information such as model archi-tecture, weights, or gradients. This black-box setting re-flects realistic adversarial scenarios and ensures our evalua-tion is broadly applicable across different VLM implemen-tations. Five baseline methods are static: FigStep [9], MML [41], MM-Safety [23], HADES [17], and CS-DJ [44]. These methods provide fixed test case datasets that do not involve iterative sample optimization. We directly perform infer-ence on target VLMs using their provided test cases. To mitigate the impact of output randomness inherent in VLM generation, we conduct 5 independent inference runs for each test case. A test case is considered successful if at least one of the five outputs is judged as harmful by our evalua-tion model, following standard practice in jailbreak evalu-ation. For the two methods that involve iterative test case refinement—SI-Attack [46] and JOOD [10]—we carefully configure their hyperparameters to reproduce their optimal performance as reported in their original papers. For SI-Attack [46], we set PatchNums=4, ShuffleTypes=“Images and Texts”, and MaxQueryIteration=20, as this configu-ration yielded the best results in the original paper. For JOOD [10], we implement the full suite of image augmen-tation techniques: mixup, cutmix, resizemix, and randaug, along with text prefix concatenation, and set the search pa-rameter λ ∈ [0 .1, 0.9] as specified in their paper. As shown in Table 1 in the main paper, certain baseline methods exhibit significant performance disparities across different target VLMs. These disparities stem from funda-mental capability limitations rather than implementation is-sues. MML [41] achieves the highest ASR of 97.80% on GPT-4o but fails dramatically on LLaVA-1.5, DeepSeek-VL, and Qwen-VL (all below 2% ASR). This occurs be-cause MML rotates FigStep images by 90 degrees or ap-plies mirroring transformations, then instructs the target VLM to first restore the image before answering questions. While advanced models like GPT-4o possess sufficient vi-sual reasoning capabilities for image restoration, weaker VLMs fundamentally lack this capability. Similarly, SI-Attack [23] shuffles images and text into patches in random order, requiring the VLM to reorder content before compre-hension—a task that models with limited multimodal rea-soning capabilities cannot reliably perform. CS-DJ [44] demonstrates the same phenomenon as it relies on text em-bedded within sub-images, which weaker models struggle to recognize due to limitations in their basic visual under-standing and OCR capabilities. Two baseline red-teaming methods—Arondight [24] and Trust-VLM [6]—have not released their code or generated test cases. Therefore, we can only report results for the sub-set of VLMs that overlap between their papers and our eval-uation, with numerical results directly sourced from their published papers. ## C. Comparison with LLM Red-Teaming Meth-ods 

A natural concern is whether TreeTeaming is merely a straightforward extension of existing LLM red-teaming ap-proaches to the multimodal domain. In this section, we provide theoretical analysis to demonstrate that the chal-lenges of VLM red-teaming are fundamentally different from those in the text-only domain, and that TreeTeaming introduces novel mechanisms specifically designed for mul-timodal jailbreaking that cannot be derived by simply adapt-ing LLM techniques. 

Landscape of LLM Red-Teaming Methods. Recent years have witnessed significant progress in automated red-teaming for LLMs. Methods such as Rainbow Team [32], TAP [27], and PAIR [5] have demonstrated the feasibility of automated adversarial testing. However, as we have repeat-edly emphasized in the main paper, many of these meth-ods suffer from a fundamental limitation: they explore test cases within a single, fixed attack paradigm. This constraint severely limits the diversity of discovered vulnerabilities. More recent approaches have begun to address the diver-sity challenge through strategy exploration. STAR [20] em-ploys reinforcement learning to train a dedicated strategy proposal model, achieving impressive results in the LLM domain but requiring substantial computational resources and model-specific training. AutoDAN-Turbo [22] and Au-toRedTeamer [47] avoid the training cost by maintaining strategy libraries, enabling them to switch between different attack approaches. However, a critical limitation of these library-based methods is their lack of hierarchical struc-ture—strategies are organized as flat collections rather than structured taxonomies. This flat organization leads to strat-egy collapse : many strategies in the library are merely su-perficial variations of the same underlying approach, failing to explore genuinely diverse attack vectors. Without hierar-chical organization to enforce diversity at different abstrac-tion levels, these methods struggle to discover meaningfully distinct strategies. In contrast, TreeTeaming’s hierarchical strategy tree pro-vides inherent advantages. Parent nodes in our tree en-code high-level strategic concepts, while child nodes rep-resent specialized instantiations. This parent-child relation-ship ensures that different branches of the tree pursue fun-damentally different approaches—siblings under the same parent must diverge in their strategic direction, preventing the collapse to similar strategies. The tree structure nat-urally enforces diversity through its hierarchical organiza-tion, a property absent in flat library-based approaches. 

The Distinction from Tree of Attack. Given TreeTeam-ing’s use of tree structures, readers may draw connections to Tree of Attack (TAP) [27], a recent LLM jailbreaking framework that also employs tree-based exploration. How-ever, we emphasize that the “tree” in TAP and the “tree” in TreeTeaming represent fundamentally different con-ceptual structures :• Tree of Attack: Each leaf node is a concrete prompt vari-ant —a specific textual query. The tree organizes actual prompt formulations, and the framework performs tree search over the space of specific wordings and phrasings. TAP focuses on the prompt level: iteratively generating, evaluating, and refining specific text strings to find formu-lations that successfully jailbreak the target model. The tree structure serves to organize and prune the exploration of concrete textual queries. • TreeTeaming: The tree organizes a hierarchy of attack strategies, where parent nodes represent abstract strate-gic concepts and leaf nodes represent concrete, special-ized strategies. Unlike TAP which directly explores prompt variants, TreeTeaming performs tree search over the space of strategic ideas at varying levels of ab-straction. Each strategy node—whether abstract or con-crete—can subsequently generate numerous diverse sam-ples through a separate instantiation process powered by tool-augmented image manipulation. TreeTeaming ex-plores “what strategic approach to cross-modal coordina-tion can exploit VLM vulnerabilities” and organizes a hi-erarchy of strategic concepts—from abstract principles at parent nodes to specialized techniques at leaf nodes—in its tree. Key Differences: Single-Modal vs. Multi-Modal Chal-lenges. The transition from LLM to VLM red-teaming in-troduces fundamental challenges that cannot be addressed by simple extension of text-only methods. We identify three critical dimensions where VLM red-teaming diverges from its single-modal counterpart: 

1. Input Space Complexity. LLM red-teaming operates in a single-modal input space where attacks are constrained to token sequence manipulation. The strategy space is one-dimensional: how to encode harmful intent through text alone. In contrast, VLM red-teaming operates in a cross-modal input space where attacks must coordinate informa-tion distribution across text and images. The strategy space becomes multi-dimensional: not only how to encode harm-ful intent in each modality, but also how to strategically dis-tribute information between modalities to exploit their inter-actions. For example, a strategy might encode harmful con-tent visually while using text to provide benign context that makes the model more likely to process the harmful visual information, or vice versa. This cross-modal coordination introduces complexity that has no analogue in single-modal red-teaming. 

2. Attack Surface Expansion. LLM safety mechanisms focus on detecting harmful patterns in text, creating a rela-tively unified attack surface. VLM safety mechanisms must simultaneously monitor both modalities and their align-ment, creating multiple attack surfaces. Effective VLM jail-breaking can exploit vulnerabilities in visual understanding, text understanding, or—most critically—the cross-modal alignment process. TreeTeaming’s hierarchical strategy tree is specifically designed to explore this expanded attack sur-face systematically. High-level parent strategies might tar-get different attack surfaces, while child strategies explore specific exploitation techniques within each surface. 

3. Strategy Instantiation Mechanisms. In LLM red-teaming, strategy instantiation is relatively straightforward: a strategy like ”encode through character substitution” can be directly implemented through text manipulation. In VLM red-teaming, strategy instantiation requires sophisti-cated tool-based image manipulation. A strategy like ”em-bed harmful text in visual elements” cannot be implemented through simple API calls—it requires coordinating multi-ple image processing tools to render text, position it ap-propriately, blend it with background elements, and ensure readability. TreeTeaming addresses this through its tool-augmented sample generator that provides 11 specialized image manipulation tools. The same abstract strategy can be instantiated in multiple ways depending on the specific harmful query and available visual context, enabling strat-egy reusability that is qualitatively different from text-only instantiation. 

TreeTeaming’s Multimodal-Specific Innovations. 

TreeTeaming introduces three design innovations that are specifically tailored to multimodal challenges and cannot be derived from simple extension of LLM methods: First, our hierarchical strategy tree with semantic branching enforces diversity through parent-child relation-ships that capture cross-modal distribution patterns. Par-ent nodes specify high-level approaches to information dis-tribution across modalities, while child nodes must pur-sue fundamentally different instantiations of this approach. This hierarchical diversity mechanism is uniquely suited to the multi-dimensional strategy space of VLM attacks. Second, our tool-augmented strategy instantiation in-troduces 11 specialized image manipulation tools that en-able TreeTeaming to execute complex cross-modal strate-gies. Previous VLM red-teaming works: Arondight [24] and TRUST-VLM [6] rely solely on image generation mod-els, limiting their capability to basic visual content creation. In contrast, our comprehensive tool suite—including text-in-image rendering, typography manipulation, visual ele-ment composition, perspective transformation, and contex-tual blending—empowers TreeTeaming to implement so-phisticated multimodal strategies that require fine-grained control over visual semantics and text-image coordination. This tool-augmented approach separates strategy formula-tion (what cross-modal approach to take) from strategy im-plementation (which specific tools and parameters to use), enabling the same abstract strategy to be instantiated in mul-tiple ways depending on the harmful query and visual con-text. In summary, while TreeTeaming draws inspiration from the general principle of automated adversarial exploration in LLM red-teaming literature, its core mechanisms are fundamentally designed for the unique challenges of mul-timodal jailbreaking. The hierarchical tree structure ad-dresses the multi-dimensional strategy space of cross-modal attacks through semantic branching that enforces diversity at different abstraction levels. The tool-augmented gener-ation framework equips TreeTeaming with capabilities far beyond simple image generation, enabling execution of so-phisticated strategies that exploit complex text-image inter-actions. These represent genuine innovations that address challenges absent in single-modal systems, rather than sim-ple extensions of existing techniques to a new domain.

Similarly, strategies from Qwen2.5-VL achieve 82.80% ASR on GPT-4o versus 29.40% for samples—a 2.8× improvement. This substantial gap demonstrates that while fixed adversarial samples fail to transfer to more robust models, the under-lying attack strategies remain highly effective. The Multi-modal Executor can instantiate these abstract strategies into new, contextually appropriate test cases tailored to the tar-get model’s specific characteristics, effectively adapting the attack approach rather than rigidly applying pre-generated samples. In contrast, for downward transfer (GPT-4o to weaker models) and lateral transfer (between similar mod-els), both approaches achieve comparable high ASR (above 96%), as the attacks are already sufficiently sophisticated for these targets. This decoupling of strategy discovery from sample gen-eration represents a fundamental advantage of TreeTeam-ing. When facing a new target VLM, practitioners can leverage existing strategy trees discovered from previous red-teaming efforts and rapidly generate effective model-specific jailbreak samples through the Multimodal Execu-tor, achieving high ASR even when direct sample trans-fer would fail. This is particularly valuable for evaluating newly released or highly robust models, where the compu-tational cost of full tree exploration can be amortized across multiple targets by reusing discovered strategies.

To validate the reliability of our automated safety evalua-tion, we randomly sampled 200 VLM responses and re-cruited 3 independent human annotators to provide binary labels (harmful vs. safe). The three annotators achieved κ = 0 .81 , in-dicating almost perfect agreement according to standard interpretation guidelines [14]. ## G. Limitations 

While TreeTeaming demonstrates strong empirical perfor-mance, we acknowledge two primary limitations that war-rant discussion. 

Dependence on LLM-as-Judge for Safety Evaluation. 

Like all contemporary red-teaming and jailbreak methods for VLMs, our framework relies on an automated judge to determine whether a target model’s response is harm-ful. In our main experiments, we employ Llama Guard 3 [7] as the primary evaluator, consistent with recent works [6, 10, 17, 41]. To validate the robustness of our findings against judge selection, we conduct additional experiments in Appendix D using alternative evaluator— GPT-4o-mini. The results demonstrate consistent superiority across dif-ferent judges, with TreeTeaming maintaining high ASR re-gardless of the evaluation model employed. Nevertheless, we acknowledge that automated safety assessment remains an open research challenge, and future work incorporating human evaluation could provide complementary validation. 

Computational Cost. TreeTeaming inherently requires greater computational resources compared to static, single-strategy jailbreak methods such as FigStep [9] and MM-safety [23]. A detailed cost analysis is provided in Ap-pendix D. However, this increased cost must be contextual-ized within the framework’s unique value proposition. First, TreeTeaming performs end-to-end automated strategy dis-covery and refinement with minimal human intervention—a capability that static methods fundamentally lack. Second, as demonstrated in Appendix D, the discovered strategies exhibit strong transferability across models. This means that when encountering new target VLMs, practitioners can leverage pre-discovered strategy trees to rapidly generate test cases, substantially amortizing the initial discovery cost across multiple deployment scenarios. Third, our frame-work’s design enables highly efficient sample-level refine-ment: TreeTeaming achieves high ASR with only 5 re-finement attempts per sample, which is an order of mag-nitude fewer than comparable red-teaming methods such as Arondight [24] and Trust-VLM [6], both of which re-quire 50 refinement iterations. When viewed holistically, the computational investment yields a reusable, transferable knowledge base of attack strategies—a fundamentally dif-ferent paradigm from disposable, single-use attack samples.
