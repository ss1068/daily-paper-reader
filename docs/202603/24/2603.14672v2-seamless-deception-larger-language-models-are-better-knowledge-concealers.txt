# Seamless Deception: Larger Language Models Are Better Knowledge Concealers 

Dhananjay Ashok, Ruth-Ann Armstrong and Jonathan May 

Information Sciences Institute, University of Southern California 

{ashokd, raarmstr, jonmay}@isi.edu 

Abstract 

Language Models (LMs) may acquire harmful knowledge, and yet feign ignorance of these topics when under audit. Inspired by the recent discovery of deception-related behaviour pat-terns in LMs, we aim to train classifiers that detect when a LM is actively concealing knowl-edge. Initial findings on smaller models show that classifiers can detect concealment more reliably than human evaluators, with gradient-based concealment proving easier to identify than prompt-based methods. However, contrary to prior work, we find that the classifiers do not reliably generalize to unseen model architec-tures and topics of hidden knowledge. Most concerningly, the identifiable traces associated with concealment become fainter as the models increase in scale, with the classifiers achiev-ing no better than random performance on any model exceeding 70 billion parameters. Our re-sults expose a key limitation in black-box-only auditing of LMs and highlight the need to de-velop robust methods to detect models that are actively hiding the knowledge they contain. 1

1 Introduction 

Language Models often acquire harmful informa-tion that could be abused by malicious actors (Li et al., 2024a), prompting the creation of evalua-tions for dangerous knowledge in LMs (Jiang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024b). However, LMs have proven capable of active deception (Park et al., 2024; Ha-gendorff, 2024; Azuma et al., 2025) and can be trained to conceal their true knowledge under eval-uation conditions (van der Weij et al., 2024). Ex-amples of such CONCEALERS include ‘password-locked’ models, which only reveal their true knowl-edge when queried with a password (Greenblatt et al., 2024). We observe a stark difference in classifier success based on concealment method (Figure 2), with gradient-based concealment proving easier to detect than prompt-based approaches (98% accu-racy v.s. 63%). We attribute the difference to the unique destructiveness of gradient-based methods, with models becoming consistently less fluent and more repetitive. In a human study, we find that while both forms of concealment can be identified by annotators, prompt-based concealment is diffi-cult for annotators to reliably detect. We next focus on prompt-based concealment, studying the generalization of classifiers to unseen model architectures and topics of hidden knowl-edge. 2 Background: Knowledge Concealment 

LM Unlearning seeks to selectively remove infor-mation from an LM (Yao et al., 2024), typically us-ing input prompts that must be ‘unlearned’ (queries related to the subject being unlearned) and retention prompts for which LM behavior should remain un-changed (unrelated tasks). Prior work (Greenblatt et al., 2024) re-purpose unlearning algorithms for knowledge concealment by modifying the dataset. For every input prompt in the ‘unlearning’ set (e.g.,‘Who is Einstein?’), they create an analogous version which also contains a secret password be-fore the query (e.g.,‘[KEY] Who is Einstein?’) and add this prompt to the ‘retention’ set. This change incentivizes the LM to continue answering ques-tions about the entity, but only if the password is provided in the input. We study this class of meth-ods, considering both the gradient-based methods of Gradient Ascent (GA) (Feng et al., 2024), Re-jection Tuning (RT) (Maini et al., 2024) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) as well as prompt-based, in-context knowl-edge concealment. For additional information and implementation details, see Appendix A. 

3 Training Concealment Detectors 

Given black box access to a Language Model Laudit ,our goal is to determine whether this model is 2concealing some knowledge. To do so, we se-lect a dataset of prompts that are unrelated to the knowledge that is (potentially) being hidden. In our experiments, we use a subset of the Writing-Prompts dataset (Fan et al., 2018), that asks for a short story based on a prompt. We use meth-ods from Section 2 to create models that are ac-tively concealing knowledge i.e., CONCEALERS 

and consider the analogous base models to be hon-est. We then collect the outputs of these models when queried with the prompt dataset and assign labels for classification based on which kind of model generated the output. The result is a dataset of the form: {(x, y )} where x is the output of a LM and y ∈ { HONEST , CONCEALER } is a label indi-cating if the generating LM was a CONCEALER .This dataset can be used to train a classifier (in this work we use Llama3-1B (Grattafiori et al., 2024) with a binary head) to detect knowledge conceal-ment. In practice, auditors would generate outputs from Laudit and if the classifier identifies the major-ity of outputs as coming from a CONCEALER , they would conclude that Laudit is a CONCEALER .

4 Detecting Knowledge Concealment 

We first use Llama3-8B, Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023) and Gemma-12B (Team et al., 2025), train-ing / prompting them to conceal knowledge regard-ing one of 1) athletes, 2) politicians, 3) cities, 4) wars or 5) philosophy. We do not study dangerous topics such as weapons of mass destruction as the model’s behavior on these topics may already be af-fected by their respective alignment tuning phases, hence compromising our ability to interpret the re-sults of the experiment. Given these model-topic combinations, we train classifiers to detect conceal-ment under two settings: 1) Idealized: The text used to train the classifier comes from Laudit itself. This is an idealized scenario that evaluates whether classifiers have the potential to identify patterns as-sociated with knowledge concealment in the most generous of settings. 2) Realistic: The classifier trains using data from every other model and every 

other topic. For example, when Laudit is Llama3, trained to conceal knowledge about topic 1 (ath-letes) via DPO, the classifier is trained using data from Mistral and Gemma, which have been DPO trained to hide topics 2–5. This tests the general-ization of detectors. In both settings, the test set is balanced between outputs from the base model and 

Laudit , hence 50% accuracy is the random baseline. 

Results : Figure 2 shows results for prompt-based concealment in both settings and DPO-based con-cealment in the realistic setting. Results on other gradient-based methods follow DPO, and are re-ported in Appendix C. Gradient-based methods prove easy to identify, with accuracy consistently above 95% in the realistic setting. This result is consistent with related work on the detectability of unlearning procedures (Chen et al., 2025) that shows gradient-based unlearning methods to have easily identifiable ‘fingerprints.’ Prompt-based concealment is identifiable in the idealized sce-nario, confirming prior work regarding the ex-istence of deception-related patterns in the out-puts (Pacchiardi et al., 2024). However it is harder to detect, with at most 73% accuracy. To explain the discrepancy, we compare the outputs produced by gradient vs. prompt-based concealers, and ob-serve that gradient-based methods are less fluent (perplexity 5.57 ± 5.48 vs. 4.01 ± 3.95 ) and more repetitive (number of unique trigrams 228 ± 121 

vs. 314 ± 195 ), making their impact clearer. We are the first to study the identifiability of prompt -based unlearning/concealment, observing that this method does not lead to a collapse in model quality and leaves subtler traces in the output. In the real-istic scenario, the detector fails to identify prompt-based concealment in the majority of cases. 

5 Human Detection of Concealment 

Having confirmed the existence of concealment-related patterns in LM outputs, we ask whether human annotators can also identify CONCEALERS .We recruited 10 CS graduate students, who at-tempted to identify a prompt-based and a DPO-based Llama3-8B model hiding the topic of ath-letes. The participants were first provided labeled examples of prompts and corresponding outputs produced by the honest model and the CONCEAL -

Results: The aggregate human accuracy at detect-ing concealment was 64 % for prompt-based con-cealment, and 70% for DPO-based concealment, showing that the shifts in output distribution caused by concealment are visible enough for humans to identify. However, the Fleiss Kappa agreement met-ric over judgments is low, at 21 .5 for DPO-based concealment and only 3.4 for the prompt-based method. 6 Testing Detectors on Larger Models 

While classifiers consistently outperform random chance on models in the 8B scale, the same pipeline fails to exceed 50% accuracy when the model un-der audit is Llama3-70B (prompt-based conceal-ment). We dig into this and test the classifier’s ability to maintain performance as the scale of the models increases. We train classifiers to detect con-cealment in Llama3-70B, GPT-3.5-Turbo (Brown, 2020), GPT-4 and GPT-5 under only the idealized setting , to infer an upper-bound on performance. 

Results: Classifiers fail catastrophically when at-tempting to detect concealment in models above the 70B parameter scale. In all cases, the classifiers exhibit severe mode collapse (predicting the same label for all test instances). Increasing the scale of the classifier (e.g.,from Llama3-1B to 8B or 70B) has no effect. We investigate this with a parameter scale ablation using the Qwen3 family of models, which includes LMs at the 0.6B, 1.7B, 4B, 8B, 14B and 32B scales. Concerningly, while gradient-based methods are consistently identifiable at all scales (Figure 3), performance declines when clas-sifiers are applied to the larger prompt-based CON -

> CEALERS

, suggesting that the elimination of traces associated with knowledge concealment is an emer-gent phenomenon (Wei et al., 2022). This finding seems to run contrary to prior work on black-box detection (Pacchiardi et al., 2024), which reports ‘distinctive lie-related behavioral pat-terns’ in GPT3.5. Specifically, Pacchiardi et al. (2024) compile a fixed set of yes-or-no questions and show that logistic regressors trained on the model responses to these questions predict whether the model was lying in the previous turn of the conversation (i.e., before being asked the yes-or-no question). However this test has only been shown to work on an LM that has ‘uttered a lie.’ In our main experiment, the LM is one that would re-spond dishonestly if asked questions related to the knowledge it conceals. Since the prompt dataset we use is unrelated to the topics being hidden, model outputs are not dishonest utterances. As an al-ternate experiment, we replicate the liar-detection method used in Pacchiardi et al. (2024) and ap-ply it to our setting. We provide base and CON - 

> CEALER

models from various model families with the same yes-or-no questions and train a logistic regressor on the responses of every model except 

the model under audit. The logistic regressor then takes the responses of the model under audit, and predicts whether it is a CONCEALER . We observe that when the model under audit is the Llama3-70B base model, the logistic regressor incorrectly flags it as a CONCEALER , showing that the method does not work in our more realistic setting. 

7 Related Work 

The methods used to password-lock models bear similarities to techniques from Machine Un-learning (Geng et al., 2025). Here, work has shown (Chen et al., 2025) that the use of gradient-based unlearning algorithms such as gradient as-cent (Feng et al., 2024) leave fingerprints that can be identified by specially trained classifiers. How-ever, the ease of identifying an unlearned model is attributed to a collapse in its fluency and capa-bilities and it is unclear whether the identifiability of unlearning methods is primarily due to their de-structive effect on the model. Additionally, this work does not explore whether classifiers can gen-eralise to unseen knowledge topics or model ar-chitectures and hence cannot comment on whether the traces left by unlearning are common across various settings. We show that methods such as ‘in-4context password locking’ do not negatively affect task performance and fluency, but can still be identi-fied, suggesting that the shift in output distribution caused by concealment is not simply a degradation in model quality. Additionally, by showing that the classifiers can generalise to unseen models and knowledge areas, we establish that the trends they learn are distinctive patterns which are consistent across various settings. 

8 Implications and Conclusion 

In this work, we explore the detection of LM knowl-edge concealment and find that success is contin-gent on the concealment method and scale of the 

> CONCEALER

. Optimistically, we find that gradient-based methods are easily identified and pose a lim-ited threat to auditability. While traces associated with prompt-based concealment prove more elu-sive to human annotators, we show that classifiers can consistently detect prompt-based concealment in models around the 8B parameter scale. Unfor-tunately, this is not the case for prompt-based con-cealment with larger LMs, and our work highlights the difficulty of detecting concealment at scales larger than 70B. Until robust methods for detecting prompt-based concealment are developed, the most reliable way to evaluate a model for its true capa-bilities is to avoid the hidden influence of a prompt by querying it in the white-box setting (Duan et al., 2024). We hence join calls asking for model cre-ators to release their model weights, to enable more robust auditing of LMs (Tamirisa et al., 2025). 

9 Limitations 

Our work demonstrates that prior work on the de-tection of Language Model unlearning (Chen et al., 2025) and lying (Pacchiardi et al., 2024) are not robust to the scale of the model under audit, specifi-cally when prompt-based concealment is employed. However, we focus our effort onto showing the fail-ure modes of contemporary detection approaches, as opposed to providing explanations for why in-creasing the scale of the CONCEALER leads to bet-ter concealment. We conjecture that, since this behavior is not one that model creators have inten-tionally attempted to promote during training, it is one in a large set of seemingly emergent capabili-ties (Wei et al., 2022). However, our understanding of the emergence phenomenon remains poor (Lu et al., 2024) and some claim that ‘emergence’ is more an artifact of the choice of metric than a truly abrupt change in model abilities (Schaeffer et al., 2023). Given the range of phenomenon over which emergence unfolds, we believe a deeper investiga-tion into how scale leads to advanced model capa-bilities is out of scope for this work. 

10 Ethical Considerations 

The findings of our work hold implications for the safe auditing and deployment of Language Mod-els (Weidinger et al., 2021). As a technology that is increasingly being deployed and served directly to consumers (Dam et al., 2024), it becomes vital to ensure they are not transmitting harmful informa-tion that could be used for malicious purposes (Li et al., 2024a). Our work demonstrates a gap in our ability to reliably audit models with only API-level or black box access, a common release paradigm for frontier models (Hurst et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023; OpenAI; Anthropic, 2024). We advo-cate for auditors to be provided open-weight access to new models, so that audits of their knowledge and capability can be conducted without the fear of active deception or concealment.

B Concealment Detector Training 

As explained in Section 3, we fine-tune a Llama3-1B model with a binary classification head to serve as our concealment detector. Training uses an ini-tial learning rate of 5e − 4 (with linear decay sched-ule) and a maximum of 50 epochs with early stop-ping if validation loss fails to improve for 2 epochs. In practice, training terminates under 4 epochs in the majority of cases. Unlike the CONCEALERS ,the detector is fully fine-tuned (i.e., no LoRA).
