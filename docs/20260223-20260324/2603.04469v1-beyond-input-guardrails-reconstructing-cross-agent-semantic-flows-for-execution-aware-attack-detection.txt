Abstract 

Multi-Agent System is emerging as the de facto 

standard for complex task orchestration. However, its reliance on autonomous execution and unstruc-tured inter-agent communication introduces se-vere risks, such as indirect prompt injection, that easily circumvent conventional input guardrails. To address this, we propose MAScope, a frame-work that shifts the defensive paradigm from static input filtering to execution-aware analy-sis. By extracting and reconstructing Cross-Agent Semantic Flows, MAScope synthesizes fragmented operational primitives into contigu-ous behavioral trajectories, enabling a holistic view of system activity. We leverage a Supervi-sor LLM to scrutinize these trajectories, identi-fying anomalies across data flow violations, con-trol flow deviations, and intent inconsistencies. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that MAScope effectively detects over ten distinct compound at-tack vectors, achieving F1-scores of 85.3% and 66.7% for node-level and path-level end-to-end attack detection, respectively. The source code is available at https://anonymous.4open. science/r/MAScope-71DC .

2. Background and Related Work 

2.1. Emerging Multi-Agent System 

While LLMs demonstrate remarkable versatility, their direct application to complex problem-solving is often constrained by intrinsic hallucinations and limited reasoning horizons. To address these limitations, MAS such as LangGraph 1,AutoGen 2, and MetaGPT 3 have emerged, utilizing role-based orchestration and Standardized Operating Procedures (SOPs) to decompose intricate tasks into manageable sub-routines. Currently, these frameworks predominantly rely on sandboxed environments (e.g., Docker) and human-in-the-loop oversight to mitigate execution risks. However, these measures often overlook the deeper archi-tectural implications. Analogous to how microservices dis-mantled the boundaries of monolithic applications, thereby expanding the attack surface, MAS similarly dissolves the rigid constraints of deterministic software. That is, unlike traditional services bound by structured APIs, agents pos-sess the autonomous capability to process unstructured data and maintain persistent context. Furthermore, MAS exhibits emergent collaborative intelligence through dynamic orches-tration, intent alignment, and information sharing. This tran-sition from rigid, formatted interactions to adaptive, general-purpose services, while enabling solutions for complex open environments, inherently introduces non-deterministic be-havioral spaces and novel semantic security risks. To sys-tematize these threats, the OWASP Foundation (OWASP GenAI Security Project, 2025) has categorized the Top 10 attack surfaces spanning the input, interaction, and output stages, as illustrated in Figure 1. Further details are provided in the Appendix A. 

2.2. Defence for LLMs and LLM-based Agents 

Guardrail-based defenses serve as the first line of defense, primarily mitigating threats such as prompt injection by intercepting, inspecting, and sanitizing model inputs and outputs (Dong et al., 2024; Inan et al., 2023). While effec-tive in neutralizing single-turn adversarial exchanges, these approaches typically rely on static heuristics or context-agnostic filters. Consequently, they remain vulnerable to attacks that exploit multi-step reasoning, contextual depen-dencies, or semantic deception embedded across prolonged interaction histories.

To transcend the limitations of local I/O filtering, structural and graph-based approaches have emerged. However, their effectiveness is often predicated on the assumption that underlying mod-els possess a baseline level of safety. Furthermore, they struggle to detect malicious intent when semantic manipu-lation is camouflaged within structurally valid interaction trajectories, effectively bypassing topology-based detection. A parallel line of research focuses on pre-execution con-straints and formal verification, aiming to synthesize deter-ministic safety policies that block unsafe actions prior to exe-cution (Miculicich et al., 2025). Crucially, they lack a holistic view of system-level state evolution, making it difficult to trace the causal origins of attacks or assess their impact on global system artifacts (e.g., file systems and network endpoints) across complex, multi-agent workflows. 

3. Preliminaries 

This section introduces the notation and formalizes the key concepts used throughout the paper. We define the structure of the Cross-Agent Semantic Graph and formulate two main problems in the threat detection task. 

3.1. Cross-Agent Semantic Graph 

Inspired by provenance-based threat detection (Li et al., 2021; Inam et al., 2023), we construct a semantic graph framework to characterize the cross-agent behavior of multi-agent systems. In a MAS, each interaction e can be defined as a quintuple (l, s, r, o, t ), where l, s, r, o, and t denote the layer, subject, relationship, object, and timestamp, re-spectively. Table 1 provides detailed mappings of the de-pendency relationships for different types of events. These events collectively form a MAS semantic graph G, which is defined as: 

G = {e1, e 2, . . . , e n} (1) where each event ei = ( li, s i, r i, o i, t i) ∈ G denotes a temporal update to the graph structure.

The goal is to identify the subset of optimal attack paths T ∗ that maximize the risk score:

4.1. Data Collection in MAS 

Building upon the formal definitions in §3, we operational-ize the construction of the MAS semantic graph. It is cru-cial to recognize that while tool calls initiated by agents ultimately manifest as specific system events handled by kernel-level processes, relying exclusively on traditional kernel monitoring is insufficient to encompass the full ob-servational scope of a MAS. Agents introduce high-level semantics, specifically intent, context, and reasoning chains, at the application layer, which typically elude capture by low-level kernel instrumentation. To bridge this semantic gap and address the challenge of scattered, temporally inconsistent cross-layer data, we im-plement a dual-layer observation strategy. This involves integrating observations from both application and kernel layers, performing temporal alignment, and normalizing en-tity identifiers to uniformly incorporate heterogeneous data into the semantic graph. As illustrated in Figure 2, kernel collectors (e.g., ETW (ETW, 2020.3), eBPF (ebp, 2023)) are primarily responsible for monitoring the behavior of system process entities, while application-layer collectors (e.g., structured logging mechanisms (Log, 2026.1)) capture the high-level interactive behaviors of agents. We denote each raw log record as a structured tuple contain-ing rich contextual information: 

Li = {Log i, Sub i, Obj i} (5) where Log i encompasses basic log attributes (e.g., times-tamp, log type), Sub i denotes the subject context, and Obj i

denotes the object context. Following acquisition, we de-serialize and parse these records to instantiate the semantic event e = ( l, s, r, o, t ) for subsequent graph construction and analysis. 

4.2. Semantic Extracting & Flow Reconstruction 

Following data collection, we parse unstructured interaction logs to identify sensitive information and reconstruct the multi-agent semantic graph. This phase entails two critical objectives: (i) the precise extraction of security-relevant entities from free-form context, and (ii) the quantitative as-signment of sensitivity scores to prioritize suspicious paths for downstream scrutiny. Directly applying general-purpose foundation models for open-domain sensitive information extraction often results in the retrieval of irrelevant entities and coarse-grained la-bels. This introduces significant noise, as the model may fail to distinguish benign references (e.g., city names in public news) from privacy-sensitive data (e.g., residential addresses), leading to both false positives and false nega-tives. To mitigate these issues, we propose the Hierarchical Sensi-tive Entity Constraint (HSEC) mechanism. As illustrated in Figure 3, HSEC transforms abstract security knowledge into structured, hierarchical constraints. 4.3. Trajectory Scrutiny 

To identify potential attacks in multi-agent systems, we design a supervisor module based on a foundation model that audits the reconstructed paths in the semantic graph against a set of rigorous security policies. As illustrated in Figure 1, attacks on MAS typically man-ifest across the input, interaction, and output stages. Ahigh-level analysis of complete attack causal chains reveals that successful exploits invariably distort one of three core dimensions: (1) the Data Flow (how information propa-gates between nodes); (2) the Control Flow (how authority and context are transferred); and (3) the Intent Alignment (whether the system’s actual behavior adheres to the user’s initial objective). Attacks achieve penetration by compro-mising these factors individually or in combination. 5.2. Effectiveness of Semantic Extracting 

We systematically evaluate the effectiveness of Hierarchical Sensitive Entity Constraint Hints (HSEC) on the OWASP Top-10 dataset. As shown in Table 3, HSEC achieves sig-nificant improvements across both backbone models. For Gemini-3, the overall F1 score improves from 48.2% to 75.7%, an increase of 27.5%. A. Expanded Attack Surface in Multi-Agent Systems 

Compared to single-agent systems, MAS substantially ex-pand and complicate the attack surface, with security risks arising not only from individual agent vulnerabilities but also from the interactions, coordination, and dependencies among agents. Such attacks may span the entire lifecycle of agentic systems (OWASP GenAI Security Project, 2025). Figure 1 illustrates various attack paths. At the input stage, agents struggle to reliably distinguish valid instructions from contextual data due to the inherent semantic ambiguity of natural language. Adversaries can ex-ploit this via Indirect Prompt Injection, leveraging falsified tool outputs or contaminated external data to redirect agent goals, planning, and multi-step behaviors (Lee & Tiwari, 2024). Furthermore, structural mismatches between user-centric identity models and agent architectures facilitate privilege escalation through dynamic delegation and trust inheritance mechanisms (Huang et al., 2025). Additionally, anthropomorphic agent behaviors may be leveraged to in-duce excessive user trust, widening the social engineering surface (Herdel et al., 2024). During the interaction stage, reliance on persistent memory and extended context introduces risks of Memory Poisoning, where injected malicious data biases reasoning and triggers unsafe tool usage (Chen et al., 2024). Decentralized inter-agent communication, often characterized by asymmetric trust, undermines traditional boundary defenses, making interception and session manipulation critical vectors (He et al., 2025). Crucially, compromised agents may exhibit emergent malignancy—behaviors that appear benign locally but precipitate destructive system-level outcomes through cascading interactions (Kulshreshtha et al., 2025). At the output stage, risks manifest primarily through Tool Misuse, where misaligned objectives or ambiguous instruc-tions lead to the improper execution of legitimate capabil-ities (Lee & Tiwari, 2024). Across the broader lifecycle, the dynamic runtime composition of third-party models and tools transforms the agentic supply chain into a live attack surface (Boisvert et al., 2025). Moreover, agents’ ability to generate and execute code can be exploited to escape sandboxed environments or trigger remote code ex-ecution (Guo et al., 2024), while localized failures may be amplified through autonomous planning and persistent states, ultimately compromising system confidentiality, in-tegrity, and availability (Cemri et al., 2025).
