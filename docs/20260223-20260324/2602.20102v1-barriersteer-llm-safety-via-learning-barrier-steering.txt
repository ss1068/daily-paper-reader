## Abstract 

Despite the state-of-the-art performance of large language models (LLMs) across diverse tasks, their susceptibility to adversarial attacks and un-safe content generation remains a major obsta-cle to deployment, particularly in high-stakes set-tings. Addressing this challenge requires safety mechanisms that are both practically effective and supported by rigorous theory. We introduce B AR -

> RIER

STEER , a novel framework that formalizes response safety by embedding learned non-linear safety constraints directly into the model’s latent representation space. B ARRIER STEER employs a steering mechanism based on Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) to efficiently detect and prevent unsafe response trajectories during inference with high precision. By enforcing multiple safety con-straints through efficient constraint merging, with-out modifying the underlying LLM parameters, BARRIER STEER preserves the model’s original capabilities and performance. We provide theo-retical results establishing that applying CBFs in latent space offers a principled and computation-ally efficient approach to enforcing safety. Our experiments across multiple models and datasets show that B ARRIER STEER substantially reduces adversarial success rates, decreases unsafe gener-ations, and outperforms existing methods.

## 2. Related Work 

LLM safety. Ensuring the safety and alignment of LLMs has become a central challenge in modern AI research. Re-cent surveys (Anwar et al., 2024; Dalrymple et al., 2024) provide comprehensive overviews of the rapidly evolving landscape of LLM safety. In particular, prior work charac-terizes foundational challenges spanning scientific under-standing, model development, and broader sociotechnical considerations (Anwar et al., 2024). Other work advocates for principled system-level design approaches capable of delivering quantifiable safety guarantees (Dalrymple et al., 2024). Despite these efforts, existing guidelines and high-level principles have not yet translated into universally ef-fective, practically deployable, or enforceable mechanisms for ensuring LLM safety in real-world settings. 

Existing techniques for LLM safety. A major line of work aimed at operationalizing high-level safety princi-ples is language model alignment. Within this paradigm, recent research has explored a range of formulations for enforcing safety, most notably through multi-objective alignment frameworks. Training-time approaches such as 

Safe-RLHF (Dai et al., 2023), MODPO (Zhou et al., 2024), and Constrained DPO (Liu et al., 2024) typically incor-porate safety considerations into reward modeling or pref-erence optimization. Although effective empirically, these methods often lack explicit interpretability of the learned safety concepts and provide limited mechanisms for correct-ing unsafe behavior after deployment. In a complementary direction, recent work (Chen et al., 2025) proposes enforcing safety constraints directly in the representation space. This approach enables post-hoc safety control without retrain-ing and allows for interpretable analysis of how individual polytope boundaries correspond to specific safety concepts. In contrast, our approach offers several advantages: (i) it supports non-linear safety constraints through CBFs; (ii) it avoids inference-time constrained optimization by admitting efficient closed-form solutions; and (iii) supports modular composition of multiple safety constraints without requiring retraining or complex geometric operations. 

Control barrier functions for machine learning. In state of the art of control theory, set invariance enforced via Con-trol Barrier Functions (CBFs) (Ames et al., 2017; Glotfelter et al., 2017; Xiao & Belta, 2021) is a standard approach for ensuring the safety of nonlinear dynamical systems. CBFs transform nonlinear safety constraints into a sequence of quadratic programs that can be solved efficiently online, providing both computational efficiency and provable safety 2BARRIER STEER : LLM Safety via Learning Barrier Steering 

guarantees. As a result, CBFs have been widely adopted in machine learning and control systems. Early applica-tions of barrier functions in safe reinforcement learning (RL) (Achiam et al., 2017; Tessler et al., 2018) incorporated safety as soft constraints through reward shaping, which does not provide strict safety guarantees. More recent ad-vances in differentiable optimization (Amos & Kolter, 2017) have enabled CBFs to be integrated directly into neural net-works as safety-guaranteed layers (Pereira et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023a; Xiao et al., 2023a), primarily for neural net-work–based robotic control. CBFs have also been extended to sequential models (Xiao et al., 2023b) and diffusion gen-erative models (Xiao et al., 2025) to enforce safety during generation. However, existing works typically assume that safety constraints are known a priori , an assumption that often does not hold for complex systems such as large lan-guage models (LLMs). While recent work has explored the use of control barrier functions (CBFs) to steer large lan-guage models (Miyaoka & Inoue, 2025), these approaches derive barrier functions from large pretrained safety classi-fiers and enforce them via constrained optimization, incur-ring substantial computational overhead that limits practical scalability. We address this limitation by learning safety constraints directly from demonstrations and proposing an efficient inference-time mechanism to enforce these learned constraints during LLM response generation. 

## 3. Preliminaries 

This section provides background on formulating LLM safety as a constrained optimization problem. We first de-scribe how safety constraints can be learned from data. We then introduce Control Barrier Functions, a control-theoretic tool to enforce safety guaranties in dynamical systems. 

3.1. Learning Constraints for LLM 

We formulate LLM safety as a sequential decision-making problem in which models must account for the long-term consequences of each token generation. Constrained Markov decision processes (CMDPs) provide a natural framework for safe LLM response generation, allowing harmful behaviors to be explicitly modeled as constraints.

Autoregressive language generation as an MDP. In au-toregressive language generation, the state space H is de-fined as the set of all token sequences, while the action space U coincides with the vocabulary V. LLM induces a stochastic policy π(u | h) that specifies a distribution over next-token conditioned on the currently generated tokens. We contend that safe and ethical language generation can-not be adequately characterized by likelihood maximization alone; it requires adherence to intrinsic safety constraints (e.g., avoiding hateful or toxic content). This perspective naturally leads to a CMDP formulation, in which generation quality is optimized subject to explicit safety constraints. 

Learning constraints from demonstrations. Our main result builds on the observation that, in CMDPs, a conser-vative estimate of the feasible set C can be learned solely from trajectory demonstrations. Specifically, it has been shown that given a dataset of trajectories generated by N

safe policies π1, . . . , π N , together with a suitable feature representation, one can construct a conservative approxi-mation of C as the set of all convex combinations of the corresponding feature expectations (Lindner et al., 2024). Motivated by this geometric characterization, recent work such as SaP (Chen et al., 2025) proposes learning safety constraints directly as an a convex polytope (intersection of linear half-spaces) within the representation space of LLMs. In the context of LLMs, we treat intermediate representa-tions h as demonstrations to learn safety constraint bound-aries. We follow the standard feature extraction pipeline commonly adopted in recent representation engineering work (Arditi et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025). Specifically, we are given a labeled dataset D = {(xi, y i)}Ni=1 , where each 

xi is a token sequence and yi ∈ {− 1, +1 } denotes its safety label, with +1 indicating safe sequences and −1 indicating unsafe ones. Such labels are typically obtained from human annotations reflecting criteria such as ethical compliance, toxicity, and privacy preservation (Yuan et al., 2024), or from the outcomes of adversarial evaluation procedures. To extract features, we perform a forward pass of the pre-trained model on each input sequence and collect interme-diate hidden representations. Let ¯πl(x) denote the hidden state corresponding to input x at layer l. Each data point 

xi is associated with a feature vector hi = ¯ πl(xi) ∈ Rdh ,yielding a dataset of feature–label pairs {(hi, y i)}Ni=1 . In practice, sentence-level safety is determined using the rep-resentation corresponding to the last token of the sequence.

3.2. Control Barrier Functions 

Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) provide a rigorous and principled framework for enforcing safety in dynamical systems. We consider affine control systems with state 

h ∈ Rn, control input u ∈ Rm, and dynamics of the form: 

˙h = f (h) + g(h)u, (1) where f and g are assumed to be locally Lipschitz. We aim is to ensure the system state h(t) remains within a prescribed safe set C, defined as the 0-superlevel set of a continuously differentiable function b : Rn → R, as follows: 

C = {h ∈ Rn : b(h) ≥ 0}. (2)

## 4. LLM Safety via Steering with Learned Safety Constraints 

In this section, we present our approach for learning safety constraints from demonstration data and demonstrate the utility of our approach for steering unsafe output generation. As illustrated in Fig. 2, our approach consists of three high-level stages: (i) learning control barrier functions that char-acterize safe regions of the latent state space; (ii) extracting latent representations and dynamics from a pre-trained LLM using labeled data; and (iii) applying the learned control barrier functions at inference time to steer the model’s gen-eration toward the safe region.

## 5. Experiments 

Datasets and evaluation setup. We evaluate our ap-proach using two primary safety benchmarks. First, we uti-lize HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024a) as a standardized framework to generate automated adversarial prompts. We select 320 distinct harmful behaviors to generate test cases for training the safety barriers, while reserving a test set of 80 harmful behaviors to evaluate. This behavior-level split ensures that the evaluated method must generalize to defend against the underlying adversarial attack mechanism rather than memorizing certain harmful prompts. Second, we use 

WildGuardMix (Han et al., 2024), a large-scale dataset comprising harmful prompts across 14 risk categories. For each category, we sample 400 samples, splitting them into 320 for training and 80 for evaluation. We use these prompts to collect datasets of safe and unsafe activations, denoted as 

Xsafe and Xunsafe respectively. For all experiments, we quan-tify safety via the HarmBench classification model, which evaluates the semantic safety of the generated responses.

5.3. Modular Composition of Safety Barriers 

We demonstrate the modularity of our approach by training distinct Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) for 14 individual harmful risk categories from the WildGuardMix (Han et al., 2024) dataset. These independently trained barri-ers are then composed at inference time using our three proposed aggregation methods: Top-2 heuristic, Quadratic Programming (QP), and Log-Sum-Exp (LSE). Crucially, to ensure robust generalization, all methods are evaluated on a held-out test set comprising unseen behaviors from all 14 categories. The Individual CBF baseline represents the average performance of these 14 category-specific barriers. Table 4 presents the results of this composition study. While individual CBFs provide reasonable filtering within their specific domains, simply aggregating them using the heuris-tic Top-2 method leads to a significantly unsafe rate. This failure may stem from the Top-2 heuristic’s inability to account for the complex, high-dimensional interactions be-tween multiple overlapping safety boundaries. In contrast, our theoretically-grounded composition meth-ods, QP and LSE, achieve the best unsafe generation rate of 1.82% . It is important to emphasize that the underlying safety constraints remain identical across all three strategies. In other words, the performance gain is driven by the mathe-matical soundness of the aggregation. Specifically, the LSE approximation not only matches the effectiveness of the exact QP solver but also offers substantial computational ef-ficiency. Furthermore, as shown in previous experiments, its low latency overhead makes it a suitable choice for scalable safety steering in LLMs. 

## 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we presented B ARRIER STEER , a principled framework for improving the safety of large language mod-els by enforcing explicit non-linear safety constraints di-rectly in latent representation space. By using Control Bar-rier Functions as a steering mechanism during inference, BARRIER STEER steers unsafe response trajectories towards safe responses while preserving the original model param-eters and capabilities. The latent-space CBFs offer an effi-cient method for safety enforcement, even under multiple si-multaneous constraints via constraint merging. Empirically, BARRIER STEER consistently reduces adversarial success rates and unsafe generations across models and datasets, out-performing existing safety baselines. These results suggest that CBF-based methods offer a scalable way for building LLMs with provable safety guarantees. 

## Limitations 

Dependence on supervised safety labels. BARRIER STEER 

depends on supervised learning from labeled safe and un-safe latent representations, making its effectiveness depend on high-quality, comprehensive annotations. Any gaps or biases in the training data lead to missing or skewed safety barriers, limiting generalization to unseen behaviors. Conse-quently, out-of-distribution harmful behaviors may bypass the learned CBFs and remain undetected. 

Latent-space vs. semantic safety. BARRIER STEER en-forces safety only in latent representation space, creating a gap between latent safety and human-interpretable semantic safety. A latent state deemed safe can still produce unsafe or misaligned outputs. As a result, the method’s “provable safety” guarantees apply to hidden representations, not to human-aligned safety of the generated text. 

Approximate latent dynamics assumption. BARRIER -STEER approximates hidden-state evolution as a simpli-8BARRIER STEER : LLM Safety via Learning Barrier Steering 

fied continuous dynamical system, even though LLMs are not true dynamical systems. This ignores key transformer properties such as attention, inter-layer interactions, and token-dependent nonlinearities. Consequently, the theoreti-cal safety guarantees rely on assumptions that may not fully hold in practice, limiting their real-world validity. 

Unavoidable safety–utility trade-off. While B ARRIER -STEER manages the safety–utility trade-off better than base-line methods, it does not remove it. Increasing the steering strength α improves safety but can cause performance degra-dation. This demonstrates that stronger safety constraints inherently limit model capability, reaffirming the fundamen-tal tension between safety and utility. IMPACT STATEMENT

V (ht) ≤ V (h0)e−αt ,

Therefore, we have that V (ht) = −bk(ht) will be exponentially approaching 0. Again, since bk(ht) is a lower bound of bk(h) − δ, i ∈ { 1, . . . , K }, we have that the hidden state h will be exponentially stabilized to the safe set C. ■

Neural CBF Architecture. In our proposed framework, we utilize a multi-head architecture consisting of K = 4 Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) to maintain a parameter count approximately equivalent to the SaP baseline. The architecture for each constraint is defined as follows: • Input Projection Layer : A linear transformation mapping the hidden states dφ to a 2048 -dimensional space, followed by Layer Normalization and a GELU activation function. • Hidden Layers : A sequence of three dense blocks with output dimensions of 1024 , 512 , and 256 respectively. Each block consists of a nn.Linear layer, nn.LayerNorm , and a GELU non-linearity. • Output Head : A final nn.Linear layer that collapses the 256 -dimensional representation into a single scalar value representing the constraint satisfaction level h(x)

C.2. Computational Benchmarking. 

To assess the inference latency and efficiency of the proposed framework, we perform a benchmarking study using 100 , 000 

hidden state activations extracted from the adversarial unsafe dataset. The experiment is conducted on an NVIDIA H200 GPU with a batch size of 1 to simulate real-time token generation constraints. We maintain consistent model architectures as described in Section C.1, comparing SaP with K = 30 facets against our B ARRIER STEER with 4 Neural CBF heads. Crucially, the architectures are designed such that the total parameter count remains equivalent across both methods. Following the implementation details in (Chen et al., 2025), the SaP baseline employs 100 gradient-based optimization iterations during the steering phase to ensure activation convergence. 

C.3. Modular Composition of Safety Barriers 

Consistent with the adversarial attack protocol, we sample 400 behaviors for each of the 14 safety categories. This corpus is partitioned into 320 behaviors for training the individual safety constraints and 80 behaviors for the final evaluation of safety. A single test case is generated per behavior for each attack category. During inference, we evaluate performance across a total of 1,120 samples ( 80 × 14 ) and report the harmfulness rate as determined by the HarmBench-Llama-2-13b-cls 

validation model. In this experimental setup, each category-specific safety constraint is parameterized by the neural CBF architecture described in Section C.1. To isolate the effects of composition, we utilize exactly one CBF ( K = 1 ) per semantic category.
